## The invisible ecosystem that rides with you

Thirty-eight trillion microorganisms inhabit the human gut, a figure that slightly exceeds the total number of cells in the body. In endurance cyclists, this microbial community is not a silent passenger: it produces metabolites that modulate inflammation, processes energy substrates, and can influence recovery speed between stages or intense training sessions. The evidence accumulated over the past decade shows that microbiome composition not only reflects an athlete's level of physical activity but can actively condition it.

The first study to put this topic on the scientific table was by Clarke and colleagues, published in the journal Gut in 2014. The researchers analyzed the microbiota of professional rugby players and compared it with two sedentary control groups matched by age and body mass index. Athletes exhibited significantly greater microbial diversity, with representation of 22 distinct phyla compared to 11-12 in controls. This diversity correlated positively with protein intake and creatine kinase levels, a marker of muscle damage associated with intense exercise (Clarke et al., 2014, Gut, 63(12):1913-20).

For an amateur cyclist accumulating between eight and twelve weekly training hours, these data pose a concrete question: can diet and exercise reshape the intestinal ecosystem in ways that favor recovery and performance?

## Prevotella: the cyclist's bacterium

A team led by Petersen at the Jackson Laboratory partially answered that question by specifically studying competitive cyclists. They analyzed fecal samples from 33 professional and amateur cyclists using metagenomic sequencing and found that microbiomes clustered into three taxonomic groups: one dominated by Prevotella, another by Bacteroides, and a third mixed cluster featuring Ruminococcus, Eubacterium, and Akkermansia. The most relevant finding was that Prevotella abundance (≥2.5%) correlated significantly with weekly exercise hours reported by the cyclists (Petersen et al., 2017, Microbiome, 5(1):98).

Cyclists who maintained training regimens exceeding 16 hours per week had an average of 12% Prevotella in their microbiota. This bacterial genus is associated with diets rich in complex carbohydrates, exactly the typical dietary pattern of an endurance cyclist. From a metabolic standpoint, Prevotella participates in branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) synthesis pathways, which are involved in muscle repair and post-exercise metabolic signaling. Another notable finding from the same study: professional cyclists showed greater activity of Methanobrevibacter smithii, an archaeon that optimizes intestinal fermentation and could improve caloric extraction from the diet.

## Veillonella and lactate recycling

The study that has received the most media attention in this field was published in Nature Medicine by Scheiman and colleagues from Harvard's Wyss Institute in 2019. The researchers collected fecal samples from Boston Marathon runners before and after the race and detected a significant increase in the relative abundance of the genus Veillonella following the effort. Upon isolating the Veillonella atypica strain and orally administering it to mice, they observed a statistically significant increase in exhaustive treadmill run time (Scheiman et al., 2019, Nature Medicine, PMID: 31235964).

The proposed mechanism is elegant in its simplicity. Veillonella uses lactate as its sole carbon source and converts it to propionate through the methylmalonyl-CoA pathway. Lactate produced by muscles during intense exercise crosses the intestinal barrier and reaches the lumen, where Veillonella metabolizes it. The resulting propionate, a short-chain fatty acid, contributes to energy production and has anti-inflammatory properties. When the researchers administered propionate intrarectally to mice without the bacterium, they reproduced the same ergogenic effect, confirming that the metabolite, not the bacterium itself, mediated the benefit.

To put it in cycling terms: during a threshold climb, muscles produce elevated amounts of lactate. If the gut microbiota harbors a robust Veillonella population, part of that lactate is recycled into propionate, which re-enters the circulation as an energy substrate. It is a bacteria-mediated metabolic recycling circuit.

## Short-chain fatty acids: the silent fuel

The propionate produced by Veillonella is just one of the short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) relevant to cyclists. Butyrate, acetate, and propionate form the main triad of SCFAs produced by bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber in the colon. Barton and colleagues demonstrated in 2018 that professional rugby athletes had significantly higher fecal SCFA concentrations than sedentary controls, along with greater representation of the bacterial metabolic pathways responsible for their production (Barton et al., 2018, Gut, 67(4):625-633).

Butyrate deserves special attention. It is the primary energy source for colonocytes, the cells lining the colon, and its presence reinforces tight junctions between them, reducing intestinal permeability. Allen and colleagues published a controlled trial in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise where 32 sedentary subjects completed six weeks of supervised aerobic exercise. In lean participants, exercise increased the abundance of butyrate-producing taxa and fecal concentrations of this fatty acid. In obese participants, this effect was not observed, suggesting that the microbiome's response to exercise depends on baseline body composition (Allen et al., 2018, MSSE, 50(4):747-757).

## The dark side: intestinal permeability and intense exercise

Not everything is favorable. High-intensity, long-duration exercise can compromise the intestinal barrier. Costa and colleagues conducted a systematic review in 2017 that coined the term "exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome" to describe a set of alterations including increased intestinal permeability, epithelial damage, and malabsorption. According to their findings, exercise performed at an intensity equal to or greater than 60% of VO₂ max for two hours or more causes significant gastrointestinal perturbations, with effects aggravated by ambient heat and dehydration (Costa et al., 2017, Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics, 46(3):246-265).

When the intestinal barrier is compromised, fragments of bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS) can pass into the systemic circulation, triggering an inflammatory response known as endotoxemia. In cyclists, this translates to elevation of markers such as IL-6, TNF-α, and C-reactive protein, which not only delay recovery but generate acute gastrointestinal symptoms: cramps, nausea, and diarrhea — problems any endurance cyclist knows well. Biomarkers of this damage include intestinal fatty acid-binding protein (I-FABP) and the lactulose/rhamnose ratio, tools that allow objective quantification of barrier integrity.

This is where the microbiome enters the picture again. A microbiota with high butyrate production reinforces the tight junctions of the intestinal epithelium, functioning as a protective factor against exercise-induced permeability. The cyclist who maintains a diet rich in fermentable fiber not only feeds beneficial bacteria but indirectly strengthens the intestinal barrier to better withstand the stress of long, intense rides.

## Probiotics: what the evidence says

The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) published its official position on probiotics in athletes in 2019, led by Jäger and colleagues. The document reviewed available evidence and concluded that certain probiotic strains can reduce the incidence and duration of upper respiratory tract infections in athletes, one of the leading causes of lost training days. It also found moderate evidence that probiotics can reduce the severity of gastrointestinal symptoms and intestinal permeability associated with high-intensity exercise (Jäger et al., 2019, JISSN, 16:62).

However, the ISSN was cautious in noting that benefits are strain-specific and dose-dependent. Not all probiotics work equally, and a product that reduces respiratory infections does not necessarily improve gastrointestinal symptoms. Strains with the most evidence in sports contexts include Lactobacillus acidophilus, Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG, and Bifidobacterium animalis subsp. lactis BB-12, although research continues to expand. For a cyclist, this means taking a generic pharmacy probiotic does not guarantee results; selection should be based on evidence for the specific strain and the particular problem being addressed.

## What a cyclist can do today

Microbiome modulation does not require sophisticated interventions. Dietary fiber remains the most influential factor on microbiota composition. Legumes, oats, green bananas, artichokes, and tubers like sweet potato provide fermentable substrates that increase SCFA production. For cyclists who tend toward high-protein, low-fiber diets, this adjustment can have a measurable impact within weeks, as Allen's study demonstrated with just six weeks of intervention.

Fermented foods such as natural yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, and kimchi provide live bacterial strains that can transiently integrate into the intestinal community. While their direct effect on sports performance is not as well documented as pharmaceutical probiotics, they contribute to overall microbial diversity — the factor Clarke identified as the primary difference between athletes and sedentary individuals.

Hydration during exercise is another direct intervention point. Dehydration worsens exercise-induced intestinal permeability, as Costa's review showed. Maintaining a consistent hydration strategy during long training sessions not only preserves cardiovascular performance but protects intestinal barrier integrity. This is particularly relevant in hot climates or when training at altitude.

## Limits of current evidence

It is worth being precise about what science can and cannot affirm today. Human studies are mostly observational and have modest sample sizes. The Veillonella finding, for example, was demonstrated in mice; no clinical trials yet confirm the same ergogenic effect in human cyclists. The correlation between Prevotella and exercise hours does not establish causality: it could be the high-carbohydrate diet, not exercise itself, that selects for these bacteria.

Interindividual microbiome variability adds another layer of complexity. Two cyclists with the same training volume and similar diet can have very different microbial compositions due to genetic factors, antibiotic history, environment, and early microbial exposure. This makes universal recommendations difficult and reinforces the need for a personalized approach. Gut microbiome sequencing through commercial kits is increasingly accessible, although the ability to translate that data into actionable dietary recommendations remains limited.

What can be stated with confidence is that regular moderate aerobic exercise, combined with a diet rich in fiber and plant diversity, promotes a microbial profile associated with lower inflammation, greater SCFA production, and better intestinal barrier integrity. For the cyclist looking to optimize every available variable, the microbiome is a frontier worth considering — not as a magic solution, but as another gear in the system that determines recovery and performance on the bike.
