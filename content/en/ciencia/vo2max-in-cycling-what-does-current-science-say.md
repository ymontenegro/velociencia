## The Number That Defines the Cyclist

Miguel Indurain recorded a VO2max of 88 ml/kg/min in laboratory tests at the COES in Pamplona during the 1990s. Tadej Pogačar, according to data from the UAE Emirates team, maintains values close to 90 ml/kg/min. These numbers represent the maximum amount of oxygen the body can consume during extreme physical effort, and for decades have served as the physiological calling card of cycling’s greatest champions.

VO2max is measured in milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (ml/kg/min). A recreational cyclist typically ranges between 40 and 55 ml/kg/min. A trained cyclist with several years of competition reaches 55-65 ml/kg/min. WorldTour professionals operate between 70 and 85 ml/kg/min, and elite climbers approach or exceed 90. That 50-point difference between an amateur and a Grand Tour champion cannot be explained by training alone.

## The Genetic Lottery of Oxygen

Bouchard et al. (1999) conducted the HERITAGE study, one of the largest projects on genetics and exercise response, concluding that approximately 50% of the variability in VO2max among individuals is explained by hereditary factors. Participants followed identical training programs, but their responses varied enormously: some improved their VO2max by 40%, while others barely improved by 5% with the same stimulus.

This genetic variability determines the starting point, but not the destination. Even the low responders in the HERITAGE study achieved significant absolute improvements in aerobic capacity. Genetics sets a potential range, and training determines how much of that range materializes. A cyclist with a genetic VO2max ceiling of 65 ml/kg/min who trains rigorously will perform better than one with a ceiling of 75 who does not train systematically.

## What Limits Oxygen Consumption

The physiology of VO2max can be summarized in one equation: the product of maximal cardiac output and the arteriovenous oxygen difference. Cardiac output depends on stroke volume and maximal heart rate. The arteriovenous difference reflects how much oxygen muscles extract from the blood perfusing them.

Joyner and Coyle (2008), in a review published in *The Journal of Physiology*, argued that in highly trained endurance athletes, cardiac output is the primary limiting factor for VO2max. Trained muscles can extract more oxygen than the heart can deliver. This is why endurance training enlarges the left ventricle and increases stroke volume: the bottleneck lies in the pump, not the periphery.

The heart of elite cyclists can pump more than 40 liters of blood per minute during maximal effort, compared to 20-25 liters in a sedentary person. This difference in cardiac output, documented in multiple exercise physiology studies, explains much of the VO2max gap between these extremes.

## Relative VO2max Makes the Difference in the Mountains

For climbing performance, VO2max relative to body weight is more relevant than absolute VO2max. An 85 kg cyclist with an absolute VO2max of 6.0 L/min has a relative value of 70.6 ml/kg/min. A 60 kg climber with an absolute VO2max of 5.0 L/min reaches a relative 83.3 ml/kg/min. On flat terrain, the heavier cyclist produces more total watts. On an 8 km climb at 8%, the climber gains several minutes.

Chris Froome, during his dominant years in the Tour de France, maintained a VO2max close to 84 ml/kg/min at a racing weight of about 67 kg. Nairo Quintana, lighter at 58 kg, compensated for a lower absolute VO2max with a competitive relative value thanks to his weight. The relationship between VO2max and watts produced depends on multiple factors—pedaling efficiency, movement economy, muscle fiber type—but as a general rule, each point of relative VO2max translates to approximately 0.07 W/kg of maximal aerobic power, according to Joyner and Coyle (2008).

## How VO2max Responds to Training

Stöggl and Sperlich (2014) compared four training models in endurance athletes over nine weeks. The polarized model, which distributes volume with approximately 80% of sessions at low intensity and 20% at high intensity, produced the greatest VO2max improvements: between 6% and 11%. The threshold model, focused on work at sustained lactate pace, improved VO2max between 4% and 7%. Pure high-intensity interval training (HIIT) showed rapid gains of 5% to 8% in the first six weeks but had less long-term progression potential.

The key to the polarized model lies in accumulating volume in zone 1, which develops central cardiac adaptations—larger left ventricle volume, greater stroke volume—combined with targeted high-intensity stimuli that push the ceiling. Seiler (2010), in a review published in *Sportscience*, documented that the world’s most successful endurance athletes distribute their training according to this polarized pattern, regardless of discipline.

For cyclists with less than two years of structured training, virtually any regular stimulus improves VO2max. For experienced cyclists with more than five years of consistent work, gains compress. At that point, VO2max approaches the genetic ceiling, and performance improvements come more from shifting the lactate threshold than increasing maximal oxygen consumption.

## A Necessary but Insufficient Indicator

VO2max sets the upper limit of aerobic performance but does not alone determine who wins the race. Two cyclists with identical VO2max values of 75 ml/kg/min can differ by 1 W/kg in sustainable power if one maintains their lactate threshold at 85% of maximum and the other at 75%. Pedaling economy, anaerobic capacity, pain tolerance, and race tactics complete the picture.

Knowing your VO2max serves as a compass: if it improves, training is progressing in the right direction; if it plateaus, it’s time to review stimuli and volumes. Laboratory tests with gas analyzers remain the gold standard, costing between 100 and 300 euros depending on the center. Field estimates from a maximal five-minute effort with a power meter offer a useful, though less precise, approximation. In any case, VO2max is the starting point of physiological analysis, not its conclusion.
