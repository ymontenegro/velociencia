## The numbers that define an era

In professional cycling, power output on the climbs is the currency by which greatness is measured. Not classifications, not tactics, not luck with the wind. Watts per kilogram sustained for 30, 40 or 60 minutes uphill are the most objective indicator of climbing performance. And those numbers tell a complex story spanning two decades of controversy, science and a new generation of riders rewriting the record books.

The era of in-competition power data began in the mid-2000s, when professional teams started using SRM power meters systematically. Before that, estimates were based on retrospective calculations using rider weight, elevation gain, distance and climbing time. Those estimates carry a margin of error, but they are sufficient to identify clear trends.

## Alpe d'Huez: the open-air laboratory

Alpe d'Huez is the most analyzed climb in cycling. Its 13.8 km at an average gradient of 8.1%, with the 21 numbered hairpins, provides a relatively standardized setting for comparing performances across eras. It is not a technical climb. It is a sustained effort lasting between 36 and 45 minutes where relative power output determines the result.

Estimated W/kg on Alpe d'Huez reveal three distinct periods. The pre-EPO era (before 1991), where climbing times corresponded to approximately 5.5-5.8 W/kg. The EPO era (1995-2008), where figures soared to 6.2-6.7 W/kg, with Marco Pantani as the ultimate benchmark. And the post-biological passport era (2009 onward), which initially saw a correction toward 5.8-6.1 W/kg before climbing again with the new generation.

## The Armstrong era: numbers under suspicion

Lance Armstrong dominated the Tour de France between 1999 and 2005, and his climbing times were the fastest ever recorded at that point. Estimates place his best ascents between 6.1 and 6.3 W/kg sustained for 35-40 minutes. On Alpe d'Huez in 2001, his time of 38 minutes and 1 second corresponded to an estimated power output of 6.3 W/kg, lower than Marco Pantani's in 1997 (36:50, ~6.7 W/kg), whose weight of 56 kg gave him a decisive W/kg advantage over the American at 72-75 kg.

Those figures turned out to be consistent with what was later confirmed about the systematic use of EPO and blood transfusions in the peloton of that era. The USADA Reasoned Decision (2012) documented the US Postal team's doping program in exhaustive detail. Armstrong was stripped of his seven titles, and the power data from that era became a benchmark for what was physiologically achievable with pharmaceutical assistance.

The post-Armstrong era brought a visible correction. When Alberto Contador won the Tour in 2009, his climbing figures (estimated at 6.0-6.1 W/kg) sat significantly below the peaks of 2001-2003. Cadel Evans in 2011 and Chris Froome in 2013 showed similar values, in the 5.8-6.0 W/kg range. The biological passport, implemented by the UCI in 2008, appeared to be working.

## Froome and the Sky era: efficiency and marginal gains

Chris Froome won four Tours between 2013 and 2017 with an approach that prioritized consistency over explosive attacks. His climbing figures held steady in the 5.9-6.1 W/kg range, lower than the Armstrong era but sustained with remarkable regularity. Team Sky (later Ineos) introduced the concept of "marginal gains": incremental improvements in nutrition, aerodynamics, equipment and race strategy.

Froome's ascents rarely produced record times. His dominance was built on maintaining a high tempo for longer than his rivals, not on producing explosive peaks of power. On the Ventoux in 2013, his estimated output was 5.9 W/kg over 57 minutes. At La Pierre-Saint-Martin in 2015, 6.0 W/kg over 32 minutes. Credible numbers for a clean elite athlete with an estimated VO2max of 84.6 ml/kg/min, according to data published by the team.

## Pogacar and Vingegaard: the new ceiling

Everything changed in 2020. Tadej Pogacar, a 21-year-old Slovenian, won the Tour de France with an uphill time trial at La Planche des Belles Filles that left the cycling world in a state of shock. His estimated power data on that time trial exceeded 6.0 W/kg sustained over 36 minutes. It was the dawn of a new era.

In the years that followed, Pogacar and Jonas Vingegaard raised the bar in sustained fashion. At the 2022 Tour, the Hautacam stage saw Pogacar and Vingegaard locked in a duel with estimated figures of 6.2-6.3 W/kg over the final 17 km. In 2023, Vingegaard dominated with a climbing consistency reminiscent of Froome's best years but at a higher absolute level. In 2024, Pogacar dismantled the peloton with figures that analysts estimated at 6.4-6.5 W/kg across several mountain stages.

## What changed: nutrition, aerodynamics and science

The inevitable question is how cyclists from 2022-2025 can produce figures comparable to the era of systematic doping. The answer involves multiple factors that have evolved simultaneously.

In-race nutrition has undergone a revolution. Until 2015, carbohydrate intake during competition hovered around 60 g/h. Studies by Jeukendrup (2014) demonstrated that the gut can be trained to absorb up to 90-120 g/h using multiple transporters (glucose + fructose). Modern teams apply protocols of 90-120 g/h during mountain stages. This translates to significantly greater energy availability in the final hours of racing, precisely when the decisive climbs are tackled.

Aerodynamics have advanced even in climbing. Optimized positions, skin suits and aero helmets save between 5 and 15 W at climbing speeds of 20-25 km/h. At 6.0 W/kg for a 65 kg rider, that represents a 1-4% saving of total power output. It is not trivial when the difference between winning and losing is measured in seconds.

Body composition has been optimized to unprecedented levels. Pogacar races at approximately 66 kg with 5-6% body fat. Vingegaard drops to 60-61 kg in peak form. These weights, combined with absolute power outputs that are maintained or improved, yield higher W/kg without needing to produce more total watts than the previous generation.

## The anti-doping context

The UCI biological passport, introduced in 2008, monitors blood variables longitudinally to detect variations consistent with the use of prohibited substances. Since its implementation, climbing times dropped measurably between 2009 and 2018. The subsequent rise with Pogacar and Vingegaard has generated skepticism in certain corners of the cycling world.

Defenders of the new data argue that the combination of nutritional advances, better talent identification, data-driven training from an early age and global performance optimization can explain the difference. Critics point out that figures of 6.3-6.5 W/kg sustained for 30-40 minutes sit at the boundary of what human physiology can produce without pharmaceutical assistance.

The truth probably lies somewhere in between. Modern cycling is faster than the 2010-2018 period for legitimate and documentable reasons. But the speed at which the leap has occurred has reignited a debate that cycling never fully closed.

## The future of professional climbing

The trend suggests the numbers will keep rising, at least marginally. In-race nutrition protocols continue to evolve, with some teams experimenting with intakes of up to 140 g/h of carbohydrates. Talent identification has gone global. Countries with no cycling tradition now produce elite climbers thanks to access to structured training and power data from adolescence onward.

Pogacar is 27 in 2026 and has probably not yet reached his physiological ceiling. Vingegaard proved in 2023 that he can compete at that level. Behind them, a generation of sub-25 riders who grew up with power meters and modern nutritional protocols is preparing to contest the same climbs with similar ambitions.

Watts on the Tour climbs are more than data. They are the record of how the human limit evolves on a bicycle, with all the light and shadow that entails. The history of cycling is written uphill, one watt at a time.
