## Five Races, a Century of History

Professional cycling features hundreds of races on its calendar, but only five hold the title of Monument. These are the oldest, longest, and most prestigious classics in the men's peloton, with distances ranging from 240 to 300 kilometers and histories that in some cases exceed 120 years. Winning a Monument places a rider among the sport's elite. Winning several turns them into a legend.

The five races are Milan-San Remo, the Tour of Flanders, Paris-Roubaix, Liège-Bastogne-Liège, and Il Lombardia. Each has a radically different character: there are centuries-old cobblestones, paved walls with gradients of 20%, Alpine climbs between lakes, and the longest, flattest finish in professional cycling. What unites them is their demand. There are no transition stages or rest days here. These are one-day races where six or seven hours of competition are decided in the final kilometers, sometimes in the last meters.

## Milan-San Remo: The Spring Classic

The longest of the five. Milan-San Remo covers approximately 298 kilometers between the Po Valley and the Ligurian coast, making it the longest one-day race on the WorldTour calendar. It has been held every March since 1907, and its nickname, La Primavera, reflects its position as the first major target of the classics season.

For more than 250 kilometers, nothing decisive happens. The peloton rides together along the highway of the sun, crosses the Ligurian coast, and faces the three capi — Capo Mele, Capo Cervo, and Capo Berta — without gaps forming. Everything changes in the last 30 kilometers. The Cipressa, introduced to the route in 1982, and the Poggio, just 9 kilometers from the finish, are the only real opportunities to break the race. The Poggio is just 3.7 kilometers long with an average gradient of 4%, but its final ramp at 8% and the technical descent toward San Remo’s Via Roma have produced some of cycling’s most dramatic finishes.

Mathieu van der Poel won the 2025 edition in a three-man sprint against Pogačar and Filippo Ganna after an aggressive descent of the Poggio. For Pogačar, Milan-San Remo remains one of the two Monuments he has yet to conquer, alongside Paris-Roubaix. The 2026 edition, held today on March 21, once again pits them against each other over the same 298 kilometers that have defined this rivalry.

## Tour of Flanders: De Ronde

The Tour of Flanders is Belgium’s most important race and probably the most beloved by European fans. It has been held since 1913 over a route of approximately 260 kilometers through the hills of the Flemish region, where the cobbled walls — the hellingen — define the outcome. The modern edition includes 17 of these walls, short but brutal, with gradients exceeding 20% on irregular, wet cobblestones.

The race’s dynamic centers on two climbs that function as an inseparable duo. The Oude Kwaremont measures 2.2 kilometers with 1,600 meters of cobbles and a maximum gradient of 11.6%. It is the longest wall in Flanders and the point where favorites begin to make their moves. Three kilometers later comes the Paterberg, just 360 meters long but with an average gradient of 12.9% and a maximum of 20.3%. The Kwaremont-Paterberg combination is repeated twice in the modern route, and the second passage, 13 kilometers from the finish, has decided every recent edition.

Pogačar won the Tour of Flanders in 2023 and 2025, both times with solo attacks that left the rest without options. Van der Poel won in 2022 and 2024. The alternation between the two illustrates just how dominant they are in the Flemish classics: between 2022 and 2025, no other rider has stood atop the podium in Oudenaarde.

## Paris-Roubaix: The Hell of the North

If there is a race that transcends cycling as a sport and enters the realm of epic, it is Paris-Roubaix. It has been run since 1896 over 259 kilometers between Compiègne and the Roubaix velodrome, in France’s industrial north. What makes it unique are its 30 sectors of pavé — centuries-old cobblestones originally designed for horse-drawn carriages — totaling 55.3 kilometers of suffering spread across the second half of the route.

The sectors are rated from one to five stars according to difficulty. The most feared is the Trouée d'Arenberg, a 2,300-meter corridor of cobbles that crosses a forest on a slight descent at speeds exceeding 50 km/h. A bump, a loose stone, or a moment of distraction can mean a crash that ends the race. The Mons-en-Pévèle sector (3,000 meters, five stars) and the Carrefour de l'Arbre (2,100 meters, five stars) complete the trilogy of decisive sections in the last 50 kilometers.

Van der Poel has won three consecutive editions between 2023 and 2025, a streak that places him among the greatest specialists in the race’s history. His ability to accelerate on the cobbles, maintain balance where others fall, and close gaps in technical sectors makes him the natural master of Roubaix. For Pogačar, this remains the most elusive Monument. He finished second in 2025, his best result to date.

## Liège-Bastogne-Liège: La Doyenne

Liège-Bastogne-Liège is the oldest Monument in cycling. It has been held since 1892, four years before Paris-Roubaix, and its nickname — La Doyenne, the dean — reflects that seniority. The 252-kilometer route crosses the Belgian Ardennes with approximately 4,300 meters of positive elevation gain, a climbing volume comparable to a mountain stage in the Tour de France.

Unlike Flanders, where the walls are short and explosive, the Ardennes’ côtes are longer, sustained climbs that punish pure rouleurs. The Côte de La Redoute, 35 kilometers from the finish, measures 2 kilometers at an average of 8.9% with ramps touching 20%. Historically, it was the point where favorites launched their decisive attacks. The Côte de la Roche-aux-Faucons, closer to the finish, has taken on that role in recent editions as the last springboard for climbers.

Pogačar dominates this race with almost unprecedented authority. He has won Liège three times, including the 2024 and 2025 editions, the latter with an attack on La Redoute that no one could follow. It is his most natural Monument: the undulating, demanding profile favors his ability to attack on long climbs and maintain the gap in the final kilometers. Only Eddy Merckx, with five victories, has more wins in this race.

## Il Lombardia: The Autumn Classic

Il Lombardia closes the Monument season every October with a 241-kilometer route and 4,400 meters of elevation gain between the lakes and mountains of Italian Lombardy. Its poetic nickname — The Autumn Classic — comes from its place on the calendar, when autumn dyes the forests around Lake Como and Lake Lecco ochre. It is the most beautiful race in professional cycling and also one of the toughest.

The 2025 edition, connecting Como with Bergamo, featured a parade of climbs that left not a single flat kilometer in the last 145 kilometers. The Ghisallo from the Asso side, the Roncola Alta (9.4 km at 6.6%), the Passo della Crocetta (11 km at 6.2%), and the Passo di Ganda formed a succession of climbs that raised the elevation gain to over 4,400 meters. The final ascent to Colle Aperto, with ramps of 12% at four kilometers from the finish, was the last filter before arriving at Bergamo Alta.

Pogačar won Il Lombardia five consecutive times between 2021 and 2025, equaling Fausto Coppi’s record. In 2025, he broke away solo on the Passo di Ganda and arrived alone in Bergamo with more than a minute’s advantage. With that victory, he reached ten Monuments in his palmarès, placing third on the all-time list alongside Coppi, Costante Girardengo, and Sean Kelly. Only Roger De Vlaeminck (11) and Eddy Merckx (19) have more.

## The Pogačar-Van der Poel Era in Numbers

The dominance of these two riders over the Monuments is unprecedented in the modern era. Since 2022, Pogačar and Van der Poel have won 15 of the 20 Monuments contested. The figure is so overwhelming it has redefined the peloton’s expectations: in each of these races, the question is no longer who will win, but which of the two will.

| Rider | Milan-San Remo | Tour of Flanders | Paris-Roubaix | Liège-Bastogne-Liège | Il Lombardia | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tadej Pogačar | 0 | 2 (2023, 2025) | 0 | 3 (2021, 2024, 2025) | 5 (2021-2025) | 10 |
| Mathieu van der Poel | 2 (2023, 2025) | 2 (2022, 2024) | 3 (2023-2025) | 0 | 0 | 7 |

Their profiles are complementary and opposite at the same time. Van der Poel is the most explosive rider in the peloton, capable of accelerating on cobbles, winning reduced sprints, and descending with technique derived from cyclocross. His seven Monuments include victories in the three northern classics — Milan-San Remo, Tour of Flanders, and Paris-Roubaix — a territorial dominance only Eddy Merckx exhibited so broadly. Pogačar, on the other hand, dominates races where climbing makes the difference. His ten Monuments concentrate in the three classics with the most elevation: Flanders, Liège, and Lombardia.

The fact that illustrates the magnitude of this rivalry is that between them they have won at least one Monument in each of the five races. There is not a single terrain where one of the two is not the main favorite. In 2025, Pogačar became the first rider to podium in all five Monuments in the same season. Van der Poel has never finished in the top three in Liège or Lombardia, but his victories in the north more than compensate for that absence.

## Wout van Aert: The Third Man

Any conversation about contemporary Monuments is incomplete without mentioning Wout van Aert. The Belgian from Visma-Lease a Bike won Milan-San Remo 2020 and has multiple podiums in Flanders and Roubaix. His versatility is extraordinary: he can contest a mass sprint, survive the northern cobbles, and attack on the Ardennes’ côtes. But injuries have slowed his progression in recent years, and the gap with Pogačar and Van der Poel has widened.

Van Aert remains a rider capable of winning any Monument on a favorable day. His power in short efforts exceeds 1,800 watts, and his ability to ride at the front of the peloton for hours before contesting the finish makes him a formidable rival. In 2026, his classics season aims to recover the level that placed him as the world’s third-best rider between 2020 and 2023.

## Isaac del Toro: The Promise Eyeing the Classics

Among the names emerging on the Monument horizon, Mexican Isaac del Toro occupies a singular place. At 21 years old, he has already won the UAE Tour and Tirreno-Adriatico in 2026 and finished second in the 2025 Giro d’Italia, where he took the young rider classification as the first North American to do so. His profile is that of a climber with the capacity for demanding one-day races, similar to a young Pogačar before the Slovenian began dominating the classics.

Del Toro rides for UAE Emirates-XRG under Pogačar’s direct tutelage, and in 2025 he finished fifth in Il Lombardia at just 20 years old. His call-up for the 2026 Ardennes, according to recent reports, suggests the team sees him as a future candidate for Liège and Lombardia. He is not a cobblestone or sprint rider, but the two classics with the most elevation suit his characteristics. If he maintains his current progression, Latin American cycling could have its first Monument winner in the coming years.

## Why the Monuments Matter

In an era dominated by three-week Grand Tours, the Monuments offer something the Tour de France cannot replicate: the brutality of a decision made in a single day. There are no recovery stages, no time trials to claw back seconds, no margin for a bad day. The rider who crosses the finish line first in Roubaix or Bergamo does so because for six or seven hours they were the strongest, smartest, and most resilient in the peloton.

Eddy Merckx won 19 Monuments throughout his career. Roger De Vlaeminck accumulated 11, with four Paris-Roubaix victories that earned him the nickname Monsieur Paris-Roubaix. Sean Kelly won all five different classics throughout the 1980s and 1990s. Now Pogačar and Van der Poel rewrite those records at a speed that once seemed impossible. At 27 and 31 years old respectively, both have room to approach numbers only Merckx has reached.

The 2026 Monument season begins today with Milan-San Remo. It will be followed by the Tour of Flanders on April 5, Paris-Roubaix on April 12, and Liège-Bastogne-Liège on April 26. Il Lombardia will close the cycle in October. Five races, five distinct stages, and two riders who have turned one-day cycling into a personal duel while the rest of the peloton watches from a distance.
