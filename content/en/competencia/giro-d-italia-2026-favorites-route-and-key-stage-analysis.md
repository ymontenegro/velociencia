## Bulgaria as the starting point

The 2026 Giro d'Italia will make history from its very first pedal stroke. For the first time in 109 editions, the Corsa Rosa will depart from Bulgaria, making it the twelfth foreign country to host a Grande Partenza. Nessebar, the ancient Greek colony founded in the sixth century BC on the shores of the Black Sea, will be the setting for the opening kilometer. Three stages on Bulgarian soil precede the transfer south to Italy, where the race plunges headlong into the mountains.

The full route covers 3,459 kilometers spread across 21 stages over 24 days, from May 8 to 31. Three rest days (May 11, 18 and 25) punctuate the three weeks. Total elevation gain hovers around 50,000 meters. The structure favors climbers: seven summit finishes, five high-mountain stages, seven medium-mountain stages and eight flat ones. A single 40.2-kilometer individual time trial in Tuscany adds a chronometric dimension that could prove decisive.

## Anatomy of the route

The first week begins on Bulgarian terrain with two sprinter-friendly stages (Nessebar-Burgas and the Sofia circuit) before a hillier stage toward Veliko Tarnovo. The transfer to Italy marks a radical shift. From the fourth stage onward, departing Catanzaro in Calabria, the peloton traverses southern Italy en route to the first major mountain appointment.

Stage seven is the longest of the Giro at 246 kilometers: a coastal traverse past Sperlonga and Gaeta finishing on the Blockhaus climbed from Roccamorice, its hardest approach. The final 10 kilometers sustain gradients around 10%, a ruthless filter in the first week. Stage ten brings the 40.2 km individual time trial on the rolling roads of Tuscany, where the rouleurs have a genuine opportunity to claw back time.

## Second week: Aosta Valley and the Alps

The second week harbors some of the most demanding days. The stage finishing in Pila, in the Aosta Valley, returns to the Giro more than three decades after its last appearance. It spans 133 kilometers with 4,400 meters of elevation gain: a short but brutal stage where the concentration of climbs offers no respite. It is the kind of day where minutes are lost by the handful.

The route also includes a stage on Swiss territory, maintaining the Giro's tradition of crossing borders when Alpine geography allows. The transition stages between mountain and flatland in this second week will be fertile ground for breakaways and tactical moves from teams hunting stage wins without contesting the overall classification.

## Third week: the Dolomites decide

The third week is where the Giro is won or lost. Stage sixteen is a classic Dolomitic tappone: 151 kilometers with 5,000 meters of elevation gain. The menu includes four legendary passes in sequence: Passo Duran, Passo Staulanza (climbed via the Coi variant, with ramps reaching 19%), Passo Giau on its hardest side (designated the 2026 Cima Coppi at 2,236 meters) and Passo Falzarego. The finish, a short 5-kilometer climb averaging 10% with spikes of 15% toward Piani di Pezze, demands that the race leader arrive with legs to respond.

Stage twenty, the penultimate of the Giro, departs from Gemona del Friuli toward Piancavallo. It is the last real opportunity to reshape the classification. Historically, Giros have been decided in this corner of Friuli, and the organizers have placed the final mountain examination here before the ceremonial ride into Rome.

On Sunday May 31, a flat circuit through the Eternal City closes the race with the coronation of the champion. Rome replaces Milan as the venue for the final stage, though the peloton will pass through the Lombardy capital during the third week, four years after its last visit.

## The favorites: Roglic leads the betting

Primoz Roglic arrives at the 2026 Giro as the leading favorite. The Slovenian, who claimed his fourth Vuelta a Espana in 2024 at the age of 34, has built his calendar around the Corsa Rosa. His time-trialing ability gives him a natural advantage on a route featuring a 40 km ITT. His weak spot remains extreme high mountain terrain, but this Giro's stages are demanding without reaching the finishes above 2,000 meters that punish rouleurs.

Antonio Tiberi is Italy's great hope. At 24, he finished third in the 2025 Giro d'Italia and opened 2026 with a convincing victory at the UAE Tour, where he beat Isaac del Toro. His profile as a pure climber with a steadily improving time trial makes him a legitimate podium contender. Racing as team leader on home soil, backed by the Italian tifosi, adds an intangible factor that cannot be measured in watts.

![Isaac del Toro wearing the maglia rosa during stage 16 of the 2025 Giro d'Italia (Photo: Filip Bossuyt, CC BY 2.0, via Wikimedia Commons)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Isaac_Del_Toro_maglia_rosa.jpg/900px-Isaac_Del_Toro_maglia_rosa.jpg)

Isaac del Toro, the 22-year-old Mexican from UAE Team Emirates, arrives as the most intriguing unknown. His talent is beyond question after a 2025 season in which he proved he could compete with the best in stage races. His weakness is the time trial, where he risks conceding time to Roglic and other rouleurs. But in the mountains, Del Toro has the profile to win stages and fight for the podium.

## The outsiders and the unknowns

Ben O'Connor has demonstrated Grand Tour pedigree with his second place at the 2024 Vuelta a Espana and a previous fourth at the Giro. His inconsistency in 2025, where he shone only with a queen-stage victory, raises doubts. If he arrives in strong form, his combination of climbing and endurance makes him a top-five candidate. His team could send him to the Giro chasing UCI points in a race with a thinner field of favorites than the Tour.

Brandon McNulty brings a different profile. His time trial is elite-level and his climbing has improved markedly. A ninth-place finish at the 2025 Giro showed he can compete across a three-week Grand Tour. The 40 km Tuscan ITT is tailor-made for him. If he can limit his losses on the toughest high-mountain stages, the podium is not out of reach.

The confirmed absences carry weight. Tadej Pogacar will not be there, orienting his season toward the spring classics and the Tour de France. Remco Evenepoel, who considered the Giro option, appears to be leaning toward his Tour of Flanders debut and the Tour de France afterward. Jonas Vingegaard is channeling all his efforts into July. These absences open the Giro to a more unpredictable race than the recent editions of the Tour.

## Stages that will define the winner

Five days will prove decisive. Stage 7 to the Blockhaus will establish the first real hierarchy among the favorites. The stage 10 time trial in Tuscany can create gaps of over a minute between pure climbers and complete rouleurs. The Alpine stage to Pila in the second week will punish anyone not in peak condition. The Dolomitic tappone of stage 16, with the Giau and Falzarego, is the day with the greatest potential for upheaval in the general classification. And Piancavallo on stage 20 is the last card for anyone who needs to attack.

## A Giro designed to attract

The organizers have deliberately calibrated the level of difficulty. According to official statements from RCS Sport, the 2026 route is designed to tempt top-tier riders who historically choose the Tour de France. The mountain stages are demanding but do not reach extreme altitudes; the transition days offer opportunities for sprinters and breakaways; and the 40 km ITT restores an element that had vanished from the most recent Grand Tours.

The result is a Giro that promises a race of progressive attrition rather than a single catastrophic day. It favors the most complete rider, the one who can climb with the best in the Dolomites and limit losses against the clock in Tuscany. Roglic fits that profile. Tiberi is building it. Del Toro has the raw talent but lacks three-week consistency. On May 31 in Rome, we will know who solved the equation best.
