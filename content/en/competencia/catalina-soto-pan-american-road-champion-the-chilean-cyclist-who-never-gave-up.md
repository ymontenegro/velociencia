## Gold in Cereté

Catalina Soto crossed the finish line first in Cereté this Saturday, March 21, becoming the Pan American road race champion. The elite women's road race at the 2026 Pan American Road Championships covered 120.6 kilometers, divided into nine laps of a 13.4-kilometer circuit in the Córdoba department of Colombia. The Chilean rider from Laboral Kutxa-Fundación Euskadi claimed the most prestigious continental title in road cycling in the Americas.

The gold medal arrives at the peak of a season that Soto has built with unprecedented consistency for Chilean women's cycling. In the three weeks prior to Cereté, she won the national time trial championship in Pichidegua by two minutes over the runner-up, defended her national road race title in a sprint from a six-rider breakaway, and led stages in the Tour of El Salvador. Four victories in March before setting foot in Colombia. The Pan American title was the fifth.

## From Bicycle as Transport to Bicycle as Profession

Catalina Soto's story differs from that of European cyclists who grow up surrounded by infrastructure, clubs, and tradition. Soto grew up in Chile using the bicycle as a means of transportation. There was no family tradition in cycling nor an obvious path to professional competition. At 13, her family moved to Australia, where she discovered track cycling as a sport.

The breakthrough came at the 2017 South American Youth Games, where she won two gold medals. A coach from the UCI World Cycling Centre spotted her during those races and offered her a place at the training center in Aigle, Switzerland. Soto was 16. She moved alone to Europe to train with the best junior talents worldwide. In 2019, at 18, she finished as the junior world silver medalist in scratch race, a track discipline demanding explosiveness and tactical reading of the peloton.

The transition to professional road cycling was not easy. She signed with a Dutch team and moved to the Netherlands, but the experience pushed her to the brink of retirement. She lived isolated in a rural area, without public transport or community, and the European winter worsened a depression that distanced her from cycling. She returned to Chile without her team, without her bike, convinced that professional cycling was not for her.

Her local club rescued her. Returning to ride without pressure, contracts, or the obligation of results reignited her passion. When Laboral Kutxa-Fundación Euskadi, a Basque team with UCI ProTeam license, offered her a contract, Soto accepted with an implicit condition: this time she would go at her own pace. She settled in the Basque Country, near San Sebastián, and found an environment combining sporting demands with emotional stability. In January 2026, the team renewed her contract through 2027.

## Three National Titles in Eight Days

Preparation for the Pan American Championships in Montería began in Chile. Soto traveled to Pichidegua, in the O'Higgins region, to compete in the national road championships from March 6 to 8. The circuit at Parque Municipal La Torina, with the homonymous climb as a selection point, was the stage for a display of dominance.

On March 6, she won the individual time trial over 20 kilometers with a time of 29 minutes and 26 seconds, two minutes ahead of the second-place rider. It was her third national title in the discipline, after those obtained in 2022 and 2025. A bee stung her during the effort, but she completed the 20 kilometers without stopping. That victory gave Laboral Kutxa its tenth win of the season.

Two days later, on March 8, she prevailed in the road race. The race was decided from a selective six-rider breakaway that consolidated after three laps of the circuit. Soto won the sprint from the reduced group and defended her national champion title for the second consecutive year. "The level of women's cycling in Chile continues to grow," she declared after crossing the finish line wearing the tricolor jersey secured for the European season.

## The Context: Women's Cycling in Latin America

Soto's victory at the Pan American Championships is not just a sporting result. It symbolizes what a Latin American female cyclist can achieve when accessing the structures of European professional cycling. Colombia has dominated the continental men's scene for decades, but women's cycling in the region has advanced with fewer resources, less visibility, and fewer opportunities.

Soto has stated clearly: "It is a sport for the rich practiced by the poor." The phrase sums up the contradiction of professional cycling in South America, where racing bikes cost more than the annual income of many families, international race travel depends on scarce federation funds, and training infrastructure cannot compare to that of countries like the Netherlands, Belgium, or Spain.

In this context, Soto's trajectory is exceptional. She is the only Chilean cyclist competing on a UCI ProTeam in Europe. She participated in two Olympic Games: Tokyo 2020 and Paris 2024, where she was the first Chilean woman to complete the Olympic road race, finishing 60th among 92 competitors. At the 2023 Pan American Games in Santiago, her bronze in the road race was one of the most celebrated medals by the Chilean delegation. In Paris, she featured in one of the most viral moments in Chilean sport when, upon learning mid-race that Francisca Crovetto had won gold in skeet shooting, she shouted to her technical director: "Fran Crovetto won gold!" The emotion ran through her body before refocusing on her own race.

Her ambition goes beyond individual results. "I don't want to be the only Chilean competing abroad," she has said in interviews. Soto understands that her role transcends rankings: every race she contests in Europe, every result appearing on UCI lists, demonstrates that Latin American women's cycling can compete at the highest level. The Pan American gold in Cereté reinforces that message.

## The 2026 Season: A Before and After

Before the national titles and the Pan American, Soto's season started in January with the Tour of El Salvador, where she won the prologue by four seconds over Spaniard Ainara Albert and added a second stage win. Those results confirmed that the preseason in the Basque Country had worked.

The March sequence is historic for Chilean cycling: three national titles in eight days, followed by Pan American gold two weeks later. That's five victories in 21 days for a 24-year-old rider who four years ago considered retiring.

Soto's calendar does not stop in Cereté. Her next goal is the European classics season with Laboral Kutxa, where she will race wearing the Chilean national champion jersey and the Pan American champion jersey. In July, the Tour de France Femmes awaits her as the only Latin American in the race, just as in 2024. If she maintains the progression of these first months, 2026 will be the season that consolidates her as the most important cyclist in the history of Chilean cycling.

## What Remains to Be Built

The gold in Cereté does not solve the structural problems of women's cycling in Chile and Latin America. Soto remains an exception, not a trend. But exceptions matter because they mark the way. Aranza Villalón, another Chilean, stood on the Pan American time trial podium with a bronze that confirms talent exists beyond a single rider.

Catalina Soto is 24 years old, contracted with a European team until 2027, with five victories in three weeks and a Pan American jersey added to the Chilean tricolor. The bicycle that was once just a means of transport has become the vehicle of a professional career that, against all odds, keeps accelerating.
