## The Crash on the Asphalt That Couldn't Stop Him

The 2026 Milan-San Remo was set to be a 298-kilometer race from Pavia to Via Roma. It ended up being a story about a rider's ability to get back on his feet and win a monument. Tadej Pogačar went down with 32 kilometers to go, right on the approach to the Cipressa, dragging Wout van Aert, Biniam Girmay, and Matteo Jorgenson down with him. Mathieu van der Poel got caught behind the crash and suffered cuts on his right hand. The peloton split at the worst possible moment.

Pogačar rose with his rainbow jersey torn and blood on his left elbow. As the group reorganized, the Slovenian hooked onto the back of the peloton just as the road steepened on the Cipressa. Brandon McNulty, his last available domestique after an early crash had taken out another UAE Team Emirates-XRG teammate, carried him from the back of the group to the front in less than four kilometers. Isaac Del Toro gave one more pull before stepping aside. Then Pogačar attacked 2.5 kilometers from the summit, with 24 kilometers still to San Remo.

Only two riders could follow his wheel: Van der Poel and Tom Pidcock. The rest watched as the trio pulled away on the descent toward the Poggio, the final obstacle before the finishing straight on the Ligurian coast.

## The Poggio as the Decider

On the 3.7-kilometer ascent of the Poggio, Pogačar repeated the script he has perfected in every Italian classic: short, brutal accelerations with no respite. Van der Poel, already carrying the bruises from the crash and the effort of chasing on the Cipressa, gave way before halfway up. The Dutchman, two-time winner of this race, couldn’t return to the front before the summit and ultimately lost contact for good.

Pidcock held on. The British rider from Pinarello-Q36.5, Olympic champion in mountain biking and cyclocross, stuck to Pogačar’s wheel with the discipline of someone who knows his only chance is to reach the sprint. On the technical descent toward San Remo, both rode in perfect synchrony, without looking at each other, aware that the race would be decided in the last 300 meters of Via Roma.

The sprint was a matter of centimeters. Pogačar launched first from the left side of the road, and Pidcock responded with everything he had left. At the finish line, the difference was half a tire width. The photo-finish confirmed the Slovenian as the winner with a time of 6 hours, 35 minutes, and 49 seconds. Van Aert, recovered from his own crash, jumped from the peloton in the last kilometer to complete the podium four seconds behind.

“It wasn’t the prettiest victory because I’m going to be licking my wounds,” Pogačar said at the finish, his elbow bandaged and wearing the smile of someone who just crossed off an obsession from his list. “But I’m happy to win.”

## Four Monuments Out of Five

With this victory, Pogačar notched his eleventh monument win and became the fourth different rider to have won at least four of the five. His collection now includes the Giro di Lombardia five consecutive times (2021–2025), Liège-Bastogne-Liège three times (2021, 2024, 2025), the Tour of Flanders twice (2023, 2025), and now Milan-San Remo. The only one missing is Paris-Roubaix to complete the set.

This statistic is no small feat. Eddy Merckx, the historical benchmark against whom every cyclist aspiring to greatness is measured, won all five monuments over his career. Roger De Vlaeminck and Rik Van Looy also achieved this. Pogačar is 27 years old and has Paris-Roubaix on his calendar in three weeks.

## The 2024 Season Collector

To understand the dimension of what Pogačar is building, one must look back to 2024, the year that rewrote the rules of what’s possible in modern cycling. He won the Giro d’Italia in May, the Tour de France in July, and the World Championship in Zurich in September. He became the third rider to achieve the Triple Crown after Merckx in 1974 and Stephen Roche in 1987. Unlike his predecessors, Pogačar dominated each of those races with a margin that left no room for debate.

In 2025, he defended the yellow jersey at the Tour and the rainbow jersey at the World Championships in Kigali, where he attacked 66 kilometers from the finish and arrived solo. He reached 100 professional wins on Stage 4 of the Tour in Rouen, at 26 years old. He ended the season with 108 victories in his palmarès and a fifth consecutive Lombardia, an absolute record in monument history.

## Two for Two in 2026

Pogačar’s 2026 season began on March 7 at Strade Bianche, where he launched a 78-kilometer solo breakaway to win for the fourth consecutive time. Del Toro and 19-year-old Frenchman Paul Seixas completed the podium more than a minute behind. Two weeks later, Milan-San Remo delivered a victory that had eluded him in previous seasons, when the flat format of the Classicissima seemed to favor pure sprinters or Poggio specialists like Jasper Stuyven or Matej Mohorič.

Pogačar’s plan for the coming weeks is public: Tour of Flanders on April 5 and Paris-Roubaix on April 13. If he also wins Flanders, Roubaix, Liège, and Lombardia, he will become the first rider to win all five Monuments in a single season, a record no one has achieved in over a century of professional cycling. In 2025, he stood on the podium in all five but missed victories in San Remo (second) and Roubaix (third).

## The Debate That No Longer Needs Arguments

Comparisons to Merckx have followed Pogačar since his first Tour de France victory at 21. The Slovenian himself has elegantly sidestepped them: “I’m not interested in Merckx’s records,” he told BikeRadar before the start of the 2026 season. But the numbers speak for themselves. At 27, Pogačar has four Tours, one Giro, two World Championships, eleven monuments, and over 110 professional wins. Merckx, at the same age, had five Tours and seven monuments but competed in an era without the physical and logistical demands of today’s cycling.

Fabian Cancellara, the Swiss rider who dominated the classics for a decade, won three different monuments in his career. Tom Boonen amassed four Roubaix wins and one Flanders. Philippe Gilbert completed all five monuments but needed his entire career to do so. Pogačar is one victory away from matching that feat before turning 28, and he does so while simultaneously competing for grand tours and world championships. The breadth of his dominance has no direct precedent.

## The Obsession with What’s Missing

Pogačar has declared he wants to “try everything” before retiring. Milan-San Remo was one of those unfinished business items, a race that slipped away in 2024 when Pidcock beat him on Via Roma and in 2025 when Van der Poel was faster in a group of four. On March 21, with asphalt still embedded in his skin, he proved that the Classicissima also belongs to him.

The next stop is the Tour of Flanders. Then, the cobbles of Roubaix. If he completes the collection, the debate about the greatest cyclist in history will cease to be a nostalgic exercise and become a simple statement of fact.
