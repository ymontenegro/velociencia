## Six Wins in Eight Days: A Demolishing Start

Remco Evenepoel needed exactly eight days of racing to accumulate six victories in 2026. The Belgian debuted with Red Bull-BORA-hansgrohe at the Challenge Mallorca in late January and delivered a statement of intent few expected after the most turbulent year of his career. At the Trofeo Ses Salines, his new team won the team time trial by just four seconds over Movistar. The next day, he attacked with more than 40 kilometers to go at the Trofeo Serra Tramuntana, arriving solo at the Santuari de Lluc with over ninety seconds on the peloton. On the third day, he repeated the formula at the Trofeo Andratx-Pollença with another solo breakaway.

The streak continued at the Volta a la Comunitat Valenciana, where he won the overall classification with a decisive move on stage 4 from the Cumbre del Sol. He also posted the fastest time in the stage 2 time trial, though times were neutralized due to adverse weather conditions. The final classification placed him ahead of João Almeida and Giulio Pellizzari. In total, six victories in just eight race days, his best start to any season.

Those numbers take on another dimension when considering where Evenepoel was coming from. Twelve months earlier, the 26-year-old Belgian was still dealing with the aftermath of an accident that could have ended his career.

## The Year That Nearly Broke Him: 2025 and the Long Shadow of the Crash

On December 3, 2024, Evenepoel collided with the open door of a postal van while training in Belgium. The impact fractured a rib, his right shoulder blade, and his right hand, while also causing contusions to both lungs and a clavicle dislocation requiring same-day surgery. His gold bike was snapped in two. Doctors estimated four months for a full recovery.

The return came in April 2025 with an unexpected victory at Brabantse Pijl, outsprinting Wout van Aert in a two-man finish. It seemed the nightmare was over. But the following weeks revealed the reality: ninth at Flèche Wallonne, where he admitted removing clothing layers too early, and a devastating 59th place at Liège-Bastogne-Liège, more than three minutes behind winner Tadej Pogačar. "I'm not a robot," he declared after the race. It was only his fourth competition since the injury.

The 2025 Tour de France confirmed the recovery was far from complete. Evenepoel was sitting third overall when he abandoned on stage 14, on the Col du Tourmalet. He had lost time in the mountains for three consecutive days. He later revealed he had raced with a broken rib suffered at the Belgian National Championships before the Tour. "It wasn't a bad day. It was three in a row. That's not normal," he said upon getting off the bike.

Yet the time trial remained his untouchable domain. He won his third consecutive world title in Kigali, Rwanda, completing the 40.6 kilometers in under 50 minutes, the only rider to do so. He even caught and passed Pogačar, who had started two and a half minutes ahead. He also claimed the European time trial championship in Étoile-sur-Rhône with a 43-second margin over Filippo Ganna.

## The Transfer That Changed the Board

On August 5, 2025, Evenepoel officially signed with Red Bull-BORA-hansgrohe, leaving Soudal-QuickStep despite having a contract through 2026. The deal, reported at around 6-7 million euros annually as a base salary with performance bonuses, placed him alongside Primož Roglič and Florian Lipowitz in a leadership trio. He brought along teammate Mattia Cattaneo, directeur sportif Klaas Lodewyck, soigneur David Geeroms, and mechanic Dario Kloek.

The most significant strategic decision was the calendar. The team ruled out the 2026 Giro d'Italia due to the absence of long time trials, the terrain where Evenepoel makes the difference. All effort would be concentrated on the spring classics and the Tour de France, with co-leadership alongside Lipowitz in July. Pre-season camps in Mallorca and Germany served to integrate the team's new methodology, with a marked emphasis on high-intensity training and altitude camps.

## The Time Trial Remains Intact, the Mountains Keep Resisting

The February UAE Tour served as a revealing test. Evenepoel dominated the stage 2 time trial, a 12.2-kilometer circuit on Hudayriat Island completed in 13 minutes and 3 seconds at an average of 56.1 km/h, six seconds faster than Josh Tarling. He took the overall lead. But stage 3, finishing atop Jebel Mobrah, exposed the weakness he has carried since the accident: he lost two minutes to Antonio Tiberi and finished tenth overall, 2 minutes and 25 seconds behind Isaac del Toro.

The pattern repeats with unsettling consistency. Evenepoel has no rival in timed efforts, but summit finishes are costing him. In the Emirates, the gap between his time trial dominance and his mountain suffering amounted to over two minutes. For the Ardennes classics, where climbs are short and explosive, this may not be an issue. For the Tour de France in July, the equation is different.

## Catalunya: The Crash That Halted the Momentum

The Volta a Catalunya, held from March 23 to 29, arrived at a moment of confidence for Evenepoel. On the opening stage, he finished second behind Dorian Godon in an uphill sprint in Sant Feliu de Guíxols. Everything indicated his form was still ascending.

Stage 3 changed the script. With 29 kilometers to go, Evenepoel launched an attack from the crosswinds that only Jonas Vingegaard could follow. Together they built a 30-second advantage over the chasing group. Victory seemed within grasp. Less than 500 meters from the finish line, the Belgian hit an unmarked pothole at a roundabout and went over the handlebars. The scrapes on his elbow, back, and hips were not serious, but the loss of time and confidence were. Godon inherited the win.

"I rode into a hole in the road," he explained afterward to CyclingWeekly. The organizers had not marked the obstacle. For a rider who 14 months earlier was on an operating table following a road accident, the psychological resonance of another crash cannot be understated.

On stage 6, the mountains exacted their toll again. Vingegaard attacked with two kilometers to go on a 7% gradient ramp and Evenepoel could not respond. Rather than fighting for his own GC position, he chose to pace teammate Lipowitz. The decision reflected tactical maturity and team commitment, but also current limitations in explosive uphill efforts. Lipowitz finished third overall, Evenepoel fifth, with Vingegaard as the outright winner.

With 73 professional victories, two Olympic golds, three world time trial titles, one world road race title, one Grand Tour, and two monuments, Evenepoel possesses one of the most complete palmares in the current peloton at just 26 years of age. Few riders in history have accumulated as many titles at that age.

## What Lies Ahead: The Ardennes as the Ultimate Test

The immediate targets are the three Ardennes classics: Amstel Gold Race on April 19, Flèche Wallonne on April 22, and Liège-Bastogne-Liège on April 26. Evenepoel won La Doyenne in 2022 and 2023, but did not compete in 2024 and finished 59th in 2025. The question is whether the Evenepoel of January, the one who demolished his rivals in Mallorca and Valencia, can sustain that form on more demanding terrain.

The Catalunya data suggests caution. His time trial form is exceptional, but the ability to respond to attacks on short, steep climbs has not yet returned to 2022-2023 levels. The stage 3 crash adds a variable of physical and mental uncertainty that only the coming weeks will resolve. Against a Pogačar who won Strade Bianche with a 79-kilometer solo breakaway and Milan-San Remo in a sprint finish, the bar is set high.

For Red Bull-BORA-hansgrohe, the first three months present a mixed but promising balance sheet. Evenepoel has shown he can win races with authority when the terrain suits his characteristics. The challenge is closing the mountain gap before July and managing the risks for a body that has suffered two serious accidents in just over a year. The spring of 2026 will tell whether the Mallorca surge was a mirage or the beginning of something bigger.
