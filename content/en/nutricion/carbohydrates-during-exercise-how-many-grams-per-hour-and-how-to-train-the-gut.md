## The Gut as the Bottleneck of Performance

A cyclist riding for more than two hours at moderate to high intensity consumes between 60 and 90 grams of muscle glycogen per hour. Total reserves hover around 400-500 grams in a 70 kg cyclist with a carbohydrate-rich diet. The arithmetic is straightforward: without external intake, glycogen is depleted between the third and fifth hour. When this happens, power drops abruptly. Cyclists call this "bonking"; physiology explains it as glycogen depletion and the consequent reliance on fat oxidation, a process too slow to sustain high intensities.

Asker Jeukendrup, a researcher at the University of Birmingham, published a 2014 review in *Sports Medicine* that reformulated carbohydrate intake recommendations during exercise. Previous guidelines established a uniform range of 30 to 60 grams per hour for any duration and intensity. Jeukendrup proposed a tiered model based on effort duration, with a ceiling of 90 grams per hour for efforts exceeding 2.5 hours, provided different carbohydrate sources were combined.

The limiting factor is not the muscle’s capacity to oxidize glucose, but the intestinal absorption rate. The small intestine absorbs glucose via the SGLT1 transporter, which has a maximum capacity of approximately 60 grams per hour. For decades, those 60 grams were the recommended ceiling. What changed the equation was the discovery that fructose uses a different transporter, GLUT5, and that combining both sugars allows surpassing that barrier.

## Glucose Plus Fructose: The Combination That Broke the 60-Gram Ceiling

Currell and Jeukendrup published a 2008 study in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* comparing three conditions during 120 minutes of cycling followed by a time trial: water, a glucose-only drink at 1.8 g/min, and a glucose-fructose mixture in a 2:1 ratio at the same total rate. Cyclists consuming the mixture completed the time trial 8% faster than those drinking glucose alone, and 19% faster than those drinking water.

Glucose and fructose are absorbed via independent intestinal pathways. When ingested together, both transporters work in parallel, allowing absorption and oxidation rates reaching 90-105 grams per hour, as documented by Jeukendrup (2014). Glucose alone saturates SGLT1 at 60 g/h, and the excess accumulates in the intestine, causing gastrointestinal discomfort. Adding fructose diverts part of the load to GLUT5 and relieves intestinal congestion.

Jeukendrup and McLaughlin published a 2011 review in the Nestlé Nutrition Institute workshop series confirming these findings. The authors noted that intestinal absorption is the limiting factor for exogenous carbohydrate utilization during prolonged exercise, and that multiple transport carbohydrate mixtures represent the most effective strategy to maximize energy availability.

## How Much to Eat Based on How Many Hours You Plan to Ride

Current recommendations from Jeukendrup (2014) scale intake according to effort duration. This scheme recognizes that the digestive cost of processing carbohydrates during exercise is only justified when the duration is sufficient to deplete endogenous stores.

| Effort Duration | Carbohydrate Recommendation | Type of Carbohydrate |
|---|---|---|
| 30-75 minutes | Mouth rinse (no need to swallow) | Any |
| 1-2 hours | Up to 30 g/hour | Single source (glucose) |
| 2-3 hours | Up to 60 g/hour | Glucose or maltodextrin |
| More than 2.5-3 hours | Up to 90 g/hour | Glucose:fructose mix (2:1) |

The mouth rinse recommendation for short efforts deserves attention. Jeukendrup (2014) documented that even in one-hour high-intensity efforts, rinsing the mouth with a carbohydrate solution improves performance without ingestion. Receptors in the oral cavity send signals to the brain that activate regions associated with reward and motivation. The effect is central, not metabolic: the body does not need the carbohydrates, but the brain responds to the promise of energy.

For efforts longer than 2.5 hours, the 90 g/h recommendation is not a starting point but a goal reached progressively. Few cyclists tolerate that intake rate without prior gut training. Attempting to consume 90 grams per hour in a granfondo without practicing it in training guarantees gastrointestinal discomfort.

## The Gut Adapts: Two Weeks Are Enough to Notice the Difference

Jeukendrup published a 2017 review in *Sports Medicine* titled "Training the Gut for Athletes" that established a principle now guiding professional cycling nutrition: the gut is a trainable organ. Gastric emptying, intestinal absorption, and tolerance to intake during effort respond to repeated exposure.

A carbohydrate-rich diet increases the density of SGLT1 transporters in the intestinal wall, allowing more glucose absorption per unit time. This effect is nutrient-specific: consuming more carbohydrates increases the capacity to absorb carbohydrates, but not other nutrients. Jeukendrup (2017) described this adaptation as analogous to muscle training: repeated stimulus generates an adaptive response that expands the organ’s functional capacity.

Miall and colleagues published a 2018 study in the *Scandinavian Journal of Medical Science in Sports* quantifying this adaptation. Eighteen endurance runners completed an intestinal challenge protocol, then underwent two weeks of daily training with carbohydrate supplementation or placebo, and repeated the challenge. The carbohydrate-trained group measurably reduced gastrointestinal discomfort and decreased malabsorption markers. Breath hydrogen levels, an indicator of unabsorbed carbohydrates fermenting in the colon, dropped from 13 ppm to 6 ppm.

## The Concrete Protocol to Train the Gut

Translating the evidence from Jeukendrup (2017) and Miall et al. (2018) into a practical plan requires three elements: frequency, progression, and logging. Gut training is not about suddenly eating more but gradually increasing carbohydrate intake during exercise sessions until reaching the target rate.

The starting point is the cyclist’s current tolerance. Someone who has never eaten during a two-hour ride will start with 30 grams per hour, equivalent to one gel or half a banana every 30 minutes. In two weekly moderate-to-high intensity sessions, intake increases by 10 grams per hour each week. If discomfort appears, the current dose is maintained for another week before progressing. In four to six weeks, most cyclists reach 60-70 g/h without problems.

Reaching 90 g/h requires using glucose-fructose mixtures. A 500 ml bottle with 60 grams of maltodextrin and 30 grams of fructose dissolved, consumed over an hour, provides 90 grams in the recommended 2:1 ratio. Commercial gels with glucose-fructose ratios and sports drinks formulated with multiple transport carbohydrates simplify logistics. Logging each session—recording amount ingested, perceived discomfort, and subjective performance—allows adjusting progression with concrete data.

## Liquid, Gel, or Solid: Format Matters Less Than Quantity

The question of whether gels, drinks, or solid foods are better during rides sparks debate in group outings. Jeukendrup (2014) addressed this clearly: intake format does not significantly affect exogenous carbohydrate oxidation rate, provided total quantity and composition are equivalent. A gel with 30 grams of carbohydrates oxidizes at the same speed as 30 grams of maltodextrin dissolved in water.

Differences between formats are practical, not metabolic. Liquids have the advantage of combining hydration and energy in one gesture. Gels are portable and allow separating hydration—water in a bottle and energy in the jersey pocket. Solid foods, like energy bars, rice, or sandwiches, provide volume and satiety but require chewing and slower gastric emptying.

At high intensities, the stomach tolerates solids worse than liquids. In a time trial or the final kilometers of a climb, gels or isotonic drinks are the safest options. In the early hours of a long stage at controlled pace, solids work well and provide variety that helps maintain intake. The most effective strategy combines formats: carbohydrate-rich liquids as a continuous base, gels for targeted boosts, and solids during lower intensity phases.

## Carbohydrates Do Not Cancel Out Training Adaptations

A recurring concern among cyclists following training plans is that consuming carbohydrates during rides impedes metabolic adaptations. The logic is: if the body receives external glucose, it does not learn to oxidize fats efficiently. Jeukendrup and McLaughlin (2011) addressed this directly and found little or no evidence that carbohydrate supplementation during exercise reduces training adaptations.

Low-intensity sessions designed specifically to improve fat oxidation can be performed with low carbohydrate availability as part of a periodized strategy. But applying carbohydrate restriction to all sessions compromises the quality of high-intensity workouts, where glycogen availability determines the capacity to sustain effort. The current evidence-based sports nutrition stance is clear: periodize carbohydrate intake according to each session’s goal, not restrict it universally.

The concept of "train low, compete high" summarizes this philosophy. Some endurance sessions are done with low carbohydrate availability to stimulate mitochondrial adaptations. Quality sessions, interval training, and competitions are done with high carbohydrate availability to maximize performance and work capacity.

## Personalization as the Final Step

The 60-90 g/h recommendations are averages derived from studies with endurance athletes. Jeukendrup (2014) noted these figures are independent of body weight and training level, simplifying application but also requiring individual adjustments. A 55 kg cyclist may find 90 g/h exceeds their tolerance even with gut training, while an 85 kg cyclist might benefit from even higher rates.

The most reliable way to personalize intake is systematic logging during training. Recording the amount and type of carbohydrates consumed, effort duration and intensity, gastrointestinal discomfort, and perceived performance in the final hours allows identifying the individual optimal range in four to six weeks of structured experimentation. This process requires no laboratory or advanced technology: a notebook, a kitchen scale to weigh intake, and honest cyclist perception suffice to build a data-driven on-road nutrition plan.
