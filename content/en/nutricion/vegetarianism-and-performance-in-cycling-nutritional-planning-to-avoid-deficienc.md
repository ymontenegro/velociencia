## Vegetarianism and Cycling Performance: The Evidence Behind Plants

Two recreational cyclists rode 4,300 kilometers across Canada in 30 days fueled entirely by plants. The case study published by Purcell et al. (2025) in *Physiological Reports* documented that both participants maintained or increased their fat-free mass throughout the challenge, with protein intake exceeding 2.3 grams per kilogram of body weight per day. The male cyclist, aged 41, lost 2.3 kilograms of body fat while gaining 1.4 kilograms of lean mass. These data suggest that a plant-based diet, when rigorously planned, can sustain the physiological demands of ultra-endurance efforts without compromising muscular integrity.

The question of whether vegetarianism is compatible with high-level cycling performance has fueled debate for decades. A meta-analysis by Damasceno et al. (2024) published in the *British Journal of Nutrition*, which pooled eight randomized controlled trials with 188 participants, found a moderate but positive effect of plant-based diets on aerobic performance (effect size: 0.55; 95% CI: 0.29–0.81). A cross-sectional study by Boutros et al. (2020) published in the *European Journal of Clinical Nutrition* compared 28 vegan women with 28 omnivores and found that vegans exhibited an estimated VO₂ max of 44.5 ± 5.2 mL/kg/min versus 41.6 ± 4.6 mL/kg/min in omnivores, and a submaximal time to exhaustion of 12.2 minutes compared to 8.8 minutes. For strength and power, however, the Damasceno meta-analysis found no significant differences between groups.

## Plant Proteins: Combination and Quantity

The most persistent myth about vegetarian diets in athletes is the supposed protein inadequacy. The official position of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, published by Melina, Craig, and Levin (2016) in the *Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics*, states that appropriately planned vegetarian and vegan diets are nutritionally complete and suitable for athletes. When caloric intake is sufficient and a variety of plant sources are consumed throughout the day, essential amino acid requirements are met without the need to combine foods at every single meal.

Endurance cyclists require between 1.2 and 1.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day according to current guidelines. The review by Rogerson (2017) in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition* recommends that vegan athletes increase this figure by an additional 10% to 20% to compensate for the lower digestibility of some plant proteins. In practical terms, a 70-kilogram cyclist should aim for 100–150 grams of protein daily, spread across four to five meals. Legumes such as lentils and chickpeas, soy products like tofu and tempeh, quinoa, hemp seeds, and nuts offer complementary amino acid profiles that, consumed in variety, cover all essential amino acids including leucine, which is key for triggering muscle protein synthesis.

## Iron: Bioavailability and Absorption Strategies

Non-heme iron found in plant foods has an absorption rate of 2% to 20%, compared to 15% to 35% for heme iron from animal sources. This difference requires vegetarian cyclists to pay close attention to both quantity and strategies that improve bioavailability. The study by Nebl et al. (2019) published in *Nutrients* assessed the micronutrient status of vegetarian, lacto-ovo-vegetarian, and omnivorous recreational runners and found that fewer than 30% of participants in each group had low iron stores and none had diagnosed iron deficiency. The result suggests that deficiency is not inevitable but preventable with dietary planning.

Consuming vitamin C-rich foods alongside plant iron sources significantly increases absorption. A practical example: combining lentils with red bell pepper or spinach with lemon juice. Conversely, calcium, tannins from tea and coffee, and phytates from unsoaked whole grains can inhibit absorption when consumed in the same meal. When blood tests reveal ferritin below 30 ng/mL, supplementation with elemental iron at 30–60 mg per day under medical supervision may be necessary, taking the supplement on an empty stomach with vitamin C to maximize absorption.

## Vitamin B12: The Non-Negotiable Supplement

Vitamin B12 is not synthesized in sufficient quantities by any non-fortified plant food. Its deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia, peripheral neuropathy, and cognitive impairment—conditions that directly affect cycling performance and recovery. Rogerson (2017) identifies B12 as the highest-risk nutrient in vegan and vegetarian athletes and recommends systematic supplementation regardless of fortified food intake.

The recommended dose ranges from 250 to 1,000 micrograms daily of cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin. Optimal serum levels for athletes fall between 400 and 700 pg/mL, above the standard clinical threshold of 200 pg/mL. Lab tests should be performed at least every six months, as hepatic B12 stores can take years to deplete and symptoms appear when neurological damage is already underway. Sublingual supplementation or chewable tablets offer good bioavailability and are economically accessible.

## Creatine: The Silent Advantage for Vegetarian Cyclists

Vegetarians have significantly lower muscle creatine concentrations than omnivores because creatine is found almost exclusively in meat and fish. The systematic review by Kaviani, Shaw, and Chilibeck (2020) published in the *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health* analyzed nine studies and concluded that creatine supplementation increases lean mass, muscular strength, mean Wingate power output, and phosphocreatine levels in vegetarians, in some cases surpassing the values of supplemented omnivores.

For cyclists, phosphocreatine is the immediate fuel for high-intensity efforts such as sprints, climbing attacks, and pace changes. The recommended maintenance dose is 3–5 grams daily of creatine monohydrate, with no loading phase required. Creatine monohydrate is the most studied compound with the best long-term safety profile. Unlike other supplements, creatine has decades of research supporting its renal and hepatic safety in healthy individuals.

## Long-Chain Omega-3s: Beyond Flaxseed Oil

Alpha-linolenic acid (ALA) found in flax seeds, chia seeds, and walnuts converts to EPA and DHA with an efficiency below 10%. Long-chain omega-3 fatty acids are critical for resolving post-exercise inflammation, cardiovascular protection, and neurological function. The review by Barnard et al. (2019) in *Nutrients* highlights that endurance athletes following plant-based diets display a favorable cardiovascular profile but warns about the need to ensure preformed EPA and DHA intake.

The alternative for vegetarian and vegan cyclists is microalgae oil supplements (genus *Schizochytrium*), which provide DHA and EPA without animal intermediaries. The recommended dose is 250–500 mg combined EPA and DHA per day, equivalent to what standard fish oil supplements provide. For cyclists with training loads exceeding 15 hours per week or during competition periods, doses up to 2,000 mg daily may offer additional anti-inflammatory benefits, though this recommendation should be individualized.

## Vitamin D and Zinc: The Overlooked Micronutrients

Vitamin D plays a role in muscle contraction, immune function, and bone mineralization. Cyclists who train during office hours or at high latitudes during winter may be deficient regardless of their diet. Nebl et al. (2019) found no differences in vitamin D levels between vegetarian and omnivorous runners, with less than 20% deficiency in each group. Supplementation with 1,000–2,000 IU daily of vitamin D3 is a reasonable preventive measure during months of low sun exposure, adjusted according to serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels.

Zinc is a cofactor for over 300 enzymes involved in protein synthesis, immune function, and wound healing. Plant sources include pumpkin seeds, chickpeas, cashews, and oats. The presence of phytates in these foods partially reduces absorption, so techniques such as soaking, sprouting, and fermentation improve bioavailability. The recommended intake for vegetarian athletes is 50% higher than for omnivores, at approximately 12–16 mg daily for men and 9–12 mg for women.

## From Theory to Plate: Practical Planning

The systematic review by Presti et al. (2024) published in the *Journal of the American Nutrition Association* confirms that plant-based diets do not compromise body composition or athletic performance when caloric intake and macronutrient distribution are adequate. The key lies in planning and monitoring, not in the exclusion of animal foods per se. A vegetarian cyclist who controls caloric intake, distributes protein across multiple meals, supplements B12 and creatine, and undergoes periodic blood tests has all the tools to compete at the same level as an omnivore.

A well-structured plant-based diet offers inherent advantages for endurance cycling: high complex carbohydrate content, greater density of antioxidants and phytonutrients, and a favorable lipid profile for long-term cardiovascular health. Recommended minimum lab panels include complete blood count, serum ferritin, vitamin B12, folic acid, 25-hydroxyvitamin D, and zinc, at a frequency of at least twice a year. With these data in hand, a sports nutritionist can fine-tune supplementation and dietary strategy with precision, turning an ethical or health choice into a science-backed performance strategy.
