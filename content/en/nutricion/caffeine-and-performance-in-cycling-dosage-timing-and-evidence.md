## A 3% Power Improvement Measured in Actual Watts

Caffeine is the most studied legal ergogenic substance in sports. In 2018, Southward, Rutherfurd-Markwick, and Ali published a meta-analysis in *Sports Medicine* that included 46 placebo-controlled studies on caffeine and endurance performance. Caffeine produced an average increase of 3.03% in power output and a 2.22% reduction in time to completion in time trials.

For a cyclist maintaining 250 watts for one hour, that 3% translates to an additional 7.5 watts without any changes in training, nutrition, or equipment. In a 40-kilometer time trial taking 55 minutes, the 2.22% reduction corresponds to 73 seconds saved. These margins separate podium positions from mid-pack finishes in competition.

The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) published its official position on caffeine and sports performance in 2021 in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition*. Guest and colleagues concluded that caffeine has consistent moderate to large benefits on aerobic performance, observed both in trained athletes and recreational individuals.

## The Mechanism Is Not Adrenaline: It’s Adenosine Blockade

For decades, the popular explanation attributed caffeine’s effects to adrenal stimulation. Current research points to a more precise mechanism. Davis and collaborators published a study in 2003 in the *American Journal of Physiology* demonstrating that caffeine primarily acts by blocking adenosine receptors in the central nervous system. Adenosine is an inhibitory neuromodulator that accumulates during prolonged exercise and signals the brain that the body needs rest.

By occupying adenosine receptors without activating them, caffeine prevents the fatigue signal from reaching the brain with its usual intensity. The result is not that muscles work harder, but that the brain perceives less fatigue. Davis et al. found that central administration of caffeine increased time to exhaustion by 60% in experimental models, whereas the same dose administered peripherally had no effect.

This mechanism explains why caffeine improves perceived effort without altering physiological markers of muscle fatigue. Heart rate, lactate, and oxygen consumption remain virtually unchanged with or without caffeine. What changes is how much effort the cyclist perceives at the same intensity, or how much intensity they tolerate before deciding they cannot continue.

## Between 3 and 6 mg/kg: The Evidence-Supported Range

Chen and collaborators published a 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition* specifically on caffeine and cycling time trial performance. They analyzed 15 studies and found that moderate doses of 4 to 6 mg/kg body weight significantly improved performance, whereas low doses of 1 to 3 mg/kg did not reach statistical significance.

For a 70 kg cyclist, the effective range translates to 210 to 420 mg of caffeine. A 250 ml cup of drip coffee contains between 80 and 120 mg of caffeine, depending on the bean and preparation. Two large cups or a double espresso plus a cup of drip coffee reach the lower end of the range. Anhydrous caffeine capsules, available in 100 or 200 mg doses, allow for more precise dosing control.

The ISSN position (Guest et al., 2021) established that doses above 9 mg/kg do not provide additional benefits and increase the incidence of side effects such as anxiety, tachycardia, and insomnia. The principle of “more is not better” applies clearly: the dose-response curve plateaus between 6 and 9 mg/kg, and beyond that, only adverse effects increase.

## Low Doses: The Nuance That Changes Strategy

The claim that only moderate to high doses work deserves a more careful review. Jenkins and collaborators published a 2008 study in the *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism* with 13 cyclists who tested doses of 1, 2, and 3 mg/kg. Results showed that doses of 2 and 3 mg/kg increased performance by 4% and 3% respectively, with the 2 mg/kg dose reaching statistical significance.

This finding has direct practical implications. For a 70 kg cyclist, 2 mg/kg equals 140 mg of caffeine, the content of a large cup of drip coffee. Jenkins et al. also found that these low doses did not affect pain perception or ratings of perceived exertion, suggesting that the mechanism at low doses might partially differ from that observed at higher doses.

The discrepancy between Jenkins (2008) and Chen (2024) results is partly explained by methodology. Chen’s meta-analysis pooled multiple studies with different designs, which can dilute small but real effects. Jenkins’ study used a crossover design with the same subjects, reducing inter-individual variability. The ISSN position acknowledges this tension and states that minimum effective doses can be as low as 2 mg/kg.

## 60 Minutes Before: The Window That Optimizes Plasma Peak

Caffeine takes between 30 and 75 minutes to reach peak blood concentration when ingested as capsules or coffee. Its half-life ranges from 3 to 7 hours, meaning a dose taken 60 minutes before a one-hour time trial maintains elevated levels throughout the effort. The ISSN (Guest et al., 2021) confirms that the most studied and recommended timing is 60 minutes before exercise.

For longer efforts, the strategy changes. In a 4- or 5-hour stage, a single dose at the start loses effect in the second half. Splitting intake, with a dose at the start and a second mid-race, maintains more stable plasma levels. Caffeine gels and gums offer faster absorption than capsules or coffee, with onset of effect in 15 to 20 minutes depending on buccal absorption.

In stage races, timing acquires another dimension. Caffeine consumed in the afternoon can affect sleep quality that night. A cyclist taking 400 mg of caffeine for a stage finishing at 5 p.m. may have difficulty falling asleep before midnight. The ISSN notes that genetic variations in the CYP1A2 gene determine metabolism speed and thus individual sensitivity to caffeine’s effects on sleep.

## Genetic Variation Explains Why Your Riding Partner Responds Differently

Not all cyclists obtain the same benefit from caffeine. The ISSN (Guest et al., 2021) documented that variations in the CYP1A2 gene divide the population into fast and slow metabolizers. Fast metabolizers process caffeine more quickly, experience peak performance earlier, and suffer fewer side effects. Slow metabolizers maintain elevated plasma levels longer, which may translate into greater anxiety and lower net benefit.

A study cited by the ISSN found that in a sample of cyclists completing a 10 km time trial, some improved their time by up to 6.8% with caffeine, others experienced no change, and a subgroup worsened performance by 13.7%. Individual variation is so wide that the universal recommendation to take caffeine before competing needs to be individualized through trial and error in training.

Polymorphism in the ADORA2A gene, which encodes the adenosine A2A receptor, adds another layer of variability. Cyclists with certain variants of this gene report greater anxiety with caffeine, which can counteract the ergogenic benefit. Controlled self-experimentation, testing different doses in training sessions and recording performance, perceived effort, and sleep quality, is the most accessible tool to calibrate individual response.

## Tolerance and Withdrawal: The Dilemma of the Habitual Consumer

The cyclist who drinks three coffees daily for years faces the question of whether caffeine still works as an ergogenic aid or if tolerance has eliminated the benefit. The evidence is nuanced. Tolerance develops for cardiovascular and diuretic effects of caffeine within days, but tolerance to ergogenic effects appears incomplete according to the ISSN review.

Controlled studies have shown that habitual caffeine consumers still gain performance improvements with acute doses, although the magnitude of effect may be smaller than in occasional consumers. The strategy of withdrawing caffeine for 7 to 14 days before competition to resensitize adenosine receptors is common practice in professional cycling. However, withdrawal days may be accompanied by headache, fatigue, and decreased training performance.

The ISSN does not recommend a specific pre-competition withdrawal protocol due to lack of robust evidence. What it does establish is that a gradual reduction over 2 to 4 days minimizes withdrawal symptoms without requiring prolonged abstinence. For the recreational cyclist, maintaining moderate consumption and reserving high doses for competitions or key training sessions represents a reasonable balance between tolerance and performance.

## Coffee, Capsules, or Gels: Form Matters Less Than It Seems

The question of whether coffee works as well as pure caffeine sparked debate for years. Recent studies have closed that discussion. The ISSN (Guest et al., 2021) concluded that coffee, caffeine gels, gums, and capsules produce comparable ergogenic effects when the caffeine dose is equivalent. Additional coffee compounds, such as chlorogenic acids, do not appear to significantly attenuate or enhance the ergogenic effect.

The advantage of capsules and gels is dosing precision. Knowing exactly 200 mg was ingested eliminates uncertainty from variability between coffee preparations. The advantage of coffee is its accessibility, lower cost, and the ritual many cyclists value as part of their pre-competition preparation.

For efforts where the stomach is already compromised by carbohydrate intake and exercise stress, caffeine gums offer an absorption route that bypasses the gastrointestinal tract. Buccal mucosa absorbs caffeine from gum in approximately 15 minutes, faster than capsules or coffee, which require gastric emptying and intestinal absorption.
