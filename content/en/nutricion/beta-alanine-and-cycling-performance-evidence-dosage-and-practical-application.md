## A Muscular Buffer Built Gram by Gram

Four weeks of beta-alanine supplementation can increase muscle carnosine concentrations by up to 80%. Carnosine is a dipeptide that acts as an intracellular buffer of hydrogen ions (H+), the particles that accumulate during high-intensity efforts and cause the drop in muscle pH associated with fatigue. When a cyclist launches an attack on a two-minute climb or contests an intermediate sprint, muscle acidosis limits how long that power output can be sustained. Increasing carnosine stores expands that window.

Carnosine synthesis in muscle is limited by the availability of beta-alanine, not histidine, the other amino acid that composes it. According to Trexler and colleagues in the official position of the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) published in 2015 in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition*, chronic beta-alanine supplementation can raise intramuscular carnosine concentrations between 20% and 80%, depending on dose and loading duration. This increase does not occur overnight: weeks of daily intake are required to saturate the muscle.

## What the Most Cited Meta-Analysis in the Field Says

Hobson and collaborators published a meta-analysis in 2012 in *Amino Acids* that included 15 placebo-controlled studies on beta-alanine and sports performance. The main conclusion was that supplementation significantly improved performance in efforts lasting between 60 and 240 seconds. Within this time range, carnosine’s buffering capacity has its greatest physiological relevance: the effort is intense enough to generate pronounced acidosis but long enough for progressive H+ accumulation to be a real limiting factor.

For efforts shorter than 60 seconds, acidosis does not become the determining factor of performance. For efforts longer than 4 minutes, the aerobic system assumes an increasing proportion of the energy demand, and the contribution of the carnosine buffer becomes diluted. Hobson et al. calculated a medium effect size of 0.18, which practically translates into a modest but statistically consistent improvement. In cycling, this improvement can represent the difference between holding onto an attack or dropping off the group in the last 500 meters of a climb.

The ISSN-recommended dose ranges between 3.2 and 6.4 grams daily during a loading period of 4 to 12 weeks. Higher doses accelerate carnosine accumulation but also increase the incidence of the most well-known beta-alanine side effect: paresthesia.

## Tingling Skin: Uncomfortable but Harmless

Paresthesia is a tingling or itching sensation that appears on the face, neck, and hands between 15 and 30 minutes after ingesting beta-alanine. It is dose-dependent: a single 1.6-gram dose triggers it more frequently and intensely than two 0.8-gram doses spaced several hours apart. According to the ISSN position (Trexler et al., 2015), paresthesia is a benign side effect that poses no health risk and disappears completely within 60 to 90 minutes.

The strategy to minimize it consists of fractionating the daily dose into 0.8 to 1.6-gram servings distributed throughout the day. Sustained-release formulations also reduce the incidence and intensity of tingling. Importantly, paresthesia is not related to the supplement’s efficacy: feeling more tingling does not mean beta-alanine is working better, nor does its absence indicate it is not working.

## Category A According to ISSN: Solid Evidence with Nuances

The ISSN position classified beta-alanine as a Category A supplement for high-intensity efforts between 60 and 240 seconds. This classification places it on the same level as caffeine, creatine, and dietary nitrates—the four ergogenic supplements with the strongest scientific backing for sports performance. Trexler et al. (2015) specified that the benefit is more pronounced in subjects with lower training levels, possibly because elite athletes already have higher baseline carnosine concentrations.

In road cycling, efforts of 1 to 4 minutes are not the norm in a flat stage but appear at decisive moments. An intermediate sprint, an attack at the base of a climb, a response to a breakaway, or the final sprint of a hilly stage demand power outputs sustained within this time range. Beta-alanine will not improve the ability to ride at a steady pace for five hours but can extend the capacity to respond during the moments when the race is decided.

For purely aerobic efforts longer than 10 minutes, evidence does not show direct benefits from beta-alanine supplementation. Hobson et al.’s (2012) meta-analysis found effects did not reach statistical significance when exercise duration exceeded 4 minutes. The practical application for the cyclist is clear: beta-alanine is a tool for explosive moments within a predominantly aerobic sport, not a general endurance enhancer.

## Synergy with Sodium Bicarbonate: Two Buffers Are Better Than One

Tobias and collaborators published a 2013 study in *Amino Acids* evaluating the combination of beta-alanine with sodium bicarbonate during high-intensity exercise. The physiological logic is straightforward: carnosine acts as an intracellular buffer and bicarbonate as an extracellular buffer. Combined, they attack acidosis from both fronts.

Results showed the combination produced superior improvements compared to each supplement alone in high-intensity work capacity tests. Sodium bicarbonate is typically administered at 0.3 g/kg body weight 60 to 90 minutes before effort. For a 70-kg cyclist, this equals 21 grams of bicarbonate, a quantity that frequently causes gastrointestinal discomfort such as nausea, bloating, and diarrhea.

Bicarbonate tolerability is the limiting factor of this combination. Not all cyclists tolerate effective doses, and gastrointestinal issues during competition can negate any ergogenic benefit. Beta-alanine, requiring chronic loading and not causing gastrointestinal effects, offers a more reliable base upon which some cyclists can experiment by adding bicarbonate in training before using it in competition.

## Beta-Alanine Versus Other Ergogenics: A Comparative Perspective

Caffeine produces 2% to 4% improvements in endurance performance according to available evidence, with effects manifesting from a single dose and covering a broad range of effort durations. Dietary nitrates, typically administered as beetroot juice, improve oxygen consumption efficiency and benefit moderate to high-intensity efforts. Creatine increases phosphocreatine stores and improves maximal intensity efforts under 30 seconds.

Beta-alanine occupies a specific niche among these supplements. Its advantage is acting in the 1 to 4-minute range where neither creatine (too short for its effect) nor caffeine alone (broader effect) offer their maximum benefit. Its disadvantage is requiring weeks of loading to reach effective carnosine concentrations, unlike caffeine or nitrates which work with a single acute dose.

For cyclists already using caffeine as their primary ergogenic aid, adding beta-alanine represents an additional layer of performance in anaerobic efforts, not a replacement. The ISSN (Trexler et al., 2015) found no negative interactions between beta-alanine and other ergogenic supplements, allowing combinations tailored to the specific demands of each competition type. A criterium with repeated sprints benefits more from the beta-alanine and caffeine combination than a flat 40-kilometer time trial where caffeine alone is likely sufficient.

## Practical Protocol for Cyclists

The loading phase requires between 3.2 and 6.4 grams daily of beta-alanine for a minimum of 4 weeks. Splitting the dose into 4 servings of 0.8 to 1.6 grams distributed with meals minimizes paresthesia and maintains steady absorption. Saunders et al. (2017) documented that muscle carnosine concentrations continue increasing up to week 12, so starting loading at the beginning of the preseason maximizes the available benefit when competitions arrive.

After the loading phase, a maintenance dose of 1.2 to 1.6 grams daily preserves elevated carnosine concentrations. Without supplementation, levels return to baseline over 6 to 15 weeks. Beta-alanine does not require cycling or rest periods, and the ISSN found no long-term adverse effects in reviewed studies, some lasting up to 24 weeks.

The cyclist seeking to optimize performance in explosive moments has in beta-alanine a supplement with robust scientific backing and a favorable safety profile. It is not a magic bullet that will transform a rouleur into a sprinter but can extend by seconds the capacity to sustain supra-threshold power in the moments where races are won or lost.
