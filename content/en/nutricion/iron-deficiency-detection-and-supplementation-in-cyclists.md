## The mineral behind aerobic performance

Iron is the central component of hemoglobin, the molecule that carries oxygen from the lungs to working muscles. A cyclist with insufficient iron stores produces less hemoglobin, transports less oxygen, and generates less aerobic power. The relationship is direct and quantifiable. Hinton (2014) estimated that a 1 g/dL drop in hemoglobin concentration reduces VO2max by 5% to 7%. In a cyclist with a VO2max of 65 ml/kg/min, that translates to a loss of 3-4 ml/kg/min — a decline that can mean the difference between staying in the lead group and getting dropped on the first climb.

The problem is that iron deficiency in its early stages produces no obvious symptoms. The cyclist notices that training sessions feel harder than usual, that recovery takes longer, and that intervals once completed with ease are now unsustainable. Many attribute this to overtraining or accumulated fatigue when the actual cause is a silent mineral deficit.

## Why cyclists lose iron

Endurance athletes operate with a chronically compromised iron balance driven by multiple simultaneous mechanisms. Peeling et al. (2008) identified the primary pathways of loss. Foot-strike hemolysis destroys red blood cells in the capillaries of the feet with every pedal stroke — an effect less pronounced than in runners but still present. Sweating during prolonged efforts eliminates between 0.3 and 0.5 mg of iron per liter of sweat, accumulating significant losses across 3- to 5-hour training rides. Gastrointestinal microbleeding, aggravated by frequent use of anti-inflammatory drugs such as ibuprofen, adds another route of loss.

Hepcidin, the hormone that regulates iron absorption, rises during the 3-6 hours following intense exercise according to Peeling et al. (2009). This surge temporarily blocks intestinal iron absorption, creating a paradox: the moment when a cyclist most needs to replenish iron (post-training) is precisely when the body absorbs it least. In premenopausal women, menstrual losses add 15-30 mg per cycle, which explains the higher prevalence of deficiency among female cyclists.

## Prevalence: a more common problem than most assume

Sim et al. (2019) conducted a systematic review of iron deficiency prevalence in athletes and found alarming figures. Deficiency without anemia (low ferritin with normal hemoglobin) affects a substantial percentage of athletes, with marked differences between sexes and disciplines.

Female cyclists show a prevalence of 30-40%, more than double that of swimmers and significantly higher than the general female population. In men, cycling shows a prevalence of 12-18%, three times the rate of the general male population. Track and field exceeds cycling in both sexes, likely due to the greater hemolysis associated with the repeated impact of running.

## Diagnostic markers and optimal values

Diagnosing iron deficiency in athletes cannot rely on a single parameter. Clenin et al. (2015) proposed a comprehensive panel that includes serum ferritin, hemoglobin, transferrin saturation, and soluble transferrin receptor. Ferritin is the most widely used marker because it reflects total iron stores, but it has an important limitation: it is an acute-phase protein that rises with inflammation, recent exercise, and infections, which can mask a real deficiency.

| Marker | Optimal for athletes | Deficiency | Anemia |
|--------|---------------------|------------|--------|
| Serum ferritin | > 50 ng/mL | < 30 ng/mL | < 15 ng/mL |
| Hemoglobin (male) | 14.5–17.5 g/dL | Normal | < 13.0 g/dL |
| Hemoglobin (female) | 12.5–15.5 g/dL | Normal | < 12.0 g/dL |
| Transferrin saturation | 20–50% | < 16% | < 16% |
| Soluble transferrin receptor | < 28 nmol/L | > 28 nmol/L | > 28 nmol/L |

Optimal values for athletes are more stringent than reference ranges used by laboratories for the general population. Burden et al. (2015) demonstrated that cyclists with ferritin between 30 and 50 ng/mL already showed a measurable reduction in oxygen-carrying capacity, even though their values fell within the "normal" lab range. This is why the recommendation for endurance athletes is to maintain ferritin above 50 ng/mL, ideally between 50 and 100 ng/mL.

## Oral versus intravenous supplementation

Once a deficiency is confirmed, two treatment pathways exist. Oral supplementation with ferrous sulfate (325 mg/day, equivalent to 65 mg of elemental iron) is the first-line treatment. It is accessible, affordable, and effective. The issue is speed and tolerability. McCormick et al. (2020) documented that oral repletion takes between 8 and 12 weeks to normalize ferritin, and gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, constipation, abdominal pain) affect 30-50% of athletes, reducing adherence.

Intravenous supplementation with ferric carboxymaltose (Ferinject) allows administration of 500-1000 mg of iron in a single 15-minute session. Garvican-Lewis et al. (2018) demonstrated that a 500 mg IV dose raised ferritin to optimal levels within 2-4 weeks, with significantly better tolerance than the oral route. IV infusion is particularly useful when competition is imminent and there is not enough time for a full oral cycle, or when the deficiency is severe (ferritin < 15 ng/mL).

The difference in recovery kinetics is stark. The intravenous route produces a ferritin peak at weeks 3-4 that then stabilizes as iron is distributed toward hemoglobin and myoglobin production. The oral route climbs in a linear, gradual fashion, reaching the 50 ng/mL threshold only between weeks 10 and 12. In both cases, improvements in aerobic performance are typically noticed before ferritin normalizes, because the production of new red blood cells begins just a few weeks after treatment starts.

## Strategies to optimize oral absorption

If the oral route is chosen, several factors modulate absorption. Moretti et al. (2015) demonstrated that taking iron every 48 hours rather than daily improved fractional absorption by 35-40%, because hepcidin levels induced by a dose take approximately 24 hours to normalize. Taking iron on alternate days at a higher dose can be more effective than smaller daily doses.

Vitamin C enhances absorption by reducing ferric iron (Fe3+) to ferrous iron (Fe2+), the form the intestine preferentially absorbs. Taking the supplement with 200 mg of vitamin C (a glass of orange juice) doubles absorption according to Hallberg et al. (1989). Conversely, calcium, tannins from tea and coffee, and phytates from whole grains inhibit absorption. The practical recommendation is to take iron on an empty stomach, accompanied by vitamin C, and separated by at least two hours from dairy products, coffee, and tea.

The time of day also matters. Peeling et al. (2017) recommended taking iron in the morning before training, not after, to avoid the post-exercise hepcidin elevation window. If the cyclist trains in the morning, the best option is to take it upon waking with at least a 30-minute gap before eating and training.

## Ongoing monitoring

Iron deficiency is recurrent in endurance athletes. Even after normalizing stores, continuous losses from sweating, hemolysis, and menstruation can progressively erode ferritin. Peeling et al. (2008) recommended blood tests every 3-4 months during the competitive season, with particular attention to high-volume training periods and the weeks following altitude training camps.

Altitude deserves special mention. Altitude training camps stimulate erythropoietin production and the generation of new red blood cells, a process that consumes iron at a massive rate. Govus et al. (2015) demonstrated that cyclists who arrived at altitude with ferritin below 40 ng/mL failed to achieve the expected increases in hemoglobin and red cell mass. Supplementing iron before and during altitude stays is a near-universal recommendation among sport physiologists.

## Beyond hemoglobin

Iron does not participate solely in oxygen transport. It is an essential component of mitochondrial electron transport chain enzymes, muscle myoglobin, and antioxidant enzymes. Haas and Brownlie (2001) demonstrated that iron deficiency without anemia (low ferritin but normal hemoglobin) already impairs mitochondrial function and muscle oxidative capacity, reducing metabolic efficiency even when blood oxygen transport appears adequate.

This explains why cyclists with "normal" hemoglobin but low ferritin report fatigue, poor performance, and impaired recovery. Iron deficiency is not merely a hemoglobin problem. It is a systemic metabolic issue that affects energy production at the mitochondrial level. Detecting it early, treating it appropriately, and preventing it through regular monitoring is one of the nutritional interventions with the greatest impact on aerobic performance available to any cyclist.
