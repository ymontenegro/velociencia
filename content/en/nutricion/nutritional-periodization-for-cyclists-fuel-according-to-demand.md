## Fuel for the Work Required, Not for the Calendar Day

Impey and colleagues published in 2018 in *Sports Medicine* the theoretical framework that reshaped how endurance athletes think about carbohydrates. The concept can be summarized in one phrase: "fuel for the work required." Carbohydrate intake should not be constant throughout the week nor follow fixed rules; rather, it should be adjusted session by session according to the training goal. A 90-minute low-intensity recovery ride does not require the same glycogen load as a threshold interval series.

This approach broke with the tradition of prescribing a fixed amount of carbohydrates per kilogram of body weight for every training day. Impey et al.'s model proposes that some sessions be deliberately performed with low carbohydrate availability to stimulate specific cellular adaptations, while others are done with high availability to sustain work quality and intensity. Nutritional periodization is not about eating less: it is about eating differently depending on the goal of each session.

The physiological basis of the model rests on the activation of AMPK and PGC-1α signaling, two master regulators of mitochondrial biogenesis. When muscle works with low glycogen stores, these pathways are activated more intensely than when working with full stores. The result, after weeks of periodized training, is muscle with greater mitochondrial density and enhanced capacity to oxidize fats as fuel.

## Train Low, Compete High: The Strategy That Separates Adaptation from Performance

The concept "train low, compete high" encapsulates the operational logic of nutritional periodization. Training with low glycogen availability enhances mitochondrial adaptations and fat utilization capacity. Competing with full glycogen stores maximizes performance because carbohydrate availability is the limiting factor for sustainable intensity in competition efforts.

Burke and colleagues systematized this strategy in the IAAF consensus recommendations published in 2019 in the *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*. Carbohydrate intake varies according to the season phase and session type. In the base phase, dominated by low-intensity volume, an intake of 3 to 5 grams of carbohydrates per kilogram of body weight covers demands without saturating stores. In the build phase, with higher intensity sessions, the range increases to 5 to 7 g/kg. In competition, where every watt counts, the recommendation reaches 8 to 12 g/kg in the days before and during the event.

For a 70 kg cyclist, these figures translate into concrete ranges. In the base phase, between 210 and 350 grams of carbohydrates daily. In the build phase, between 350 and 490 grams. In competition, between 560 and 840 grams. The difference between the lower end of the base phase and the upper end of competition is four times more carbohydrates, a variation reflecting how different the metabolic demand is between riding three hours at conversational pace and contesting a mountain stage.

| Season Phase | CHO (g/kg/day) | CHO for 70 kg (g/day) | Main Objective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base | 3–5 | 210–350 | Aerobic adaptation, fat oxidation |
| Build | 5–7 | 350–490 | Sustain intensity, interval quality |
| Competition | 8–12 | 560–840 | Max performance, full stores |

## Sleep Low: The Most Aggressive Version of Train Low

Marquet and colleagues published in 2016 in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* a study that took the train low concept one step further. The "sleep low" protocol involves performing a high-intensity session in the afternoon, having a very low carbohydrate dinner, sleeping with partially depleted glycogen stores, and performing a low to moderate intensity session the next morning in a fasted state or with minimal carbohydrate intake.

The results were striking. After three weeks, the sleep low group improved their performance in a supramaximal cycling test by 3.2% more than the control group, which consumed the same total amount of carbohydrates but distributed evenly. Both groups ingested exactly the same calories and grams of carbohydrates by the end of each day. The only difference was when they consumed them relative to training sessions.

Marquet’s protocol demonstrated that the timing of carbohydrate intake can be as relevant as the total amount. The afternoon high-intensity session partially depletes muscle glycogen stores. The low-carb dinner prevents replenishment overnight. The morning session is performed in a low-availability state that amplifies molecular signaling for mitochondrial adaptations. The cyclist trains with the tank half-empty when the goal is adaptation, and fills it when the goal is performance.

## Risks That Aggressive Periodization Cannot Ignore

Training with low energy availability has a limit that must not be crossed. LEA (Low Energy Availability) occurs when caloric intake does not cover the energy expenditure of exercise plus the organism’s basal needs. If nutritional periodization is combined with caloric restriction, whether intentional for weight loss or accidental due to poor planning, the cyclist can fall into RED-S syndrome (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport).

RED-S affects endocrine, bone, immune, cardiovascular, and psychological function. In cyclists, it manifests as decreased testosterone, bone density loss, increased incidence of respiratory infections, mood disturbances, and paradoxically, a decline in performance that the restriction sought to improve. The line between "train low" and "undertrained and underfueled" can be thinner than it seems, especially in cyclists combining high training volume with body composition goals.

Burke et al. (2019) make an explicit recommendation on this point: train low sessions must be balanced with train high sessions within the same week. Total energy balance must remain neutral or slightly positive. Reducing carbohydrates around a specific session does not equate to reducing total intake. A cyclist who does sleep low on Tuesday and Thursday must ensure that on Wednesday and Friday they consume enough energy to cover the accumulated metabolic debt.

## From Theory to the Peloton: Periodization in Elite Athletes

Stellingwerff published in 2012 in the *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism* a case study documenting the practical application of nutritional periodization in elite endurance athletes. The work showed that high-performance athletes were already intuitively manipulating their carbohydrate intake before the science formalized the concept, consuming less on easy volume days and more on key sessions and competition days.

What Stellingwerff contributed was the precise quantification of these variations. Documented athletes consumed between 3 and 4 g/kg on recovery days and between 8 and 10 g/kg on competition or quality session days. The variation was neither arbitrary nor based on feelings: it responded to a nutritional plan integrated with the training plan, where each session had a defined metabolic goal and corresponding nutritional prescription.

Transferring this to amateur cycling requires simplifying the model without losing its essence. A cyclist training five days a week can apply train low in two low-intensity sessions (recovery rides, technique sessions) and ensure high carbohydrate availability in the two or three quality sessions (intervals, tempo, long rides at pace). They do not need to weigh every gram of rice or calculate exact glycogen percentages: they need to understand that the easy session on Tuesday and the interval series on Thursday require different fuels.

## Practical Recommendations to Implement Periodization

The first step is to classify training sessions by objective. High-intensity sessions (threshold intervals, VO2max, sprints) require high carbohydrate availability before, during, and after. Low to moderate intensity sessions (zone 2 rides, active recovery) are candidates to be performed with low availability. Long endurance sessions present a mixed case: start with moderate availability and allow progressive glycogen depletion during the latter hours to activate adaptation pathways.

The second step is to adjust intake around each session, not the entire day. A reduced carbohydrate breakfast before a morning zone 2 ride is an application of train low. A load of 80 to 100 grams of carbohydrates in the two hours prior to a threshold interval series is an application of train high. The dinner after the interval session should replenish consumed glycogen with generous amounts of rice, pasta, potato, or bread.

The third step, and most frequently omitted, is to monitor warning signs of LEA. Persistent fatigue that does not improve with rest, performance decline over weeks, sleep disturbances, irritability, loss of menstruation in female cyclists, or increased frequency of colds are indicators that energy balance has tipped too far into deficit. At that point, priority shifts from periodization to restoring energy status by increasing total intake until symptoms resolve.

Nutritional periodization is not a restrictive diet with a scientific name. It is a carbohydrate distribution strategy that respects the demands of each session and protects the cyclist’s health. Applied with planning, it allows extracting more adaptation from every hour of training without sacrificing the ability to perform when the race demands it.
