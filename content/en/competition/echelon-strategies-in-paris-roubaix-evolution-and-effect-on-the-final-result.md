## Echelon Strategies in Paris-Roubaix: Evolution and Impact on the Final Outcome

The 2022 Paris-Roubaix was marked by one of the most decisive tactical demonstrations of the last decade. Dylan van Baarle, shielded by the collective work of Ineos Grenadiers, triumphed solo after a race where echelons fragmented the peloton long before the cobblestone sectors decided anything. The edition reached an average speed exceeding 45 km/h, a figure that reflects not only the riders’ power but also the aerodynamic efficiency that a well-executed echelon formation can provide to a reduced group. That day, the crosswind was as much a protagonist as the cobblestones of northern France themselves.

Echelons are diagonal line formations that teams adopt when the wind blows from the side. Instead of riding in single file, cyclists stagger diagonally so that each rider is protected by the teammate ahead and slightly to the side, taking advantage of the low-pressure zone generated by the preceding rider. This arrangement occupies the entire width of the road, and when a full team executes the maneuver at sufficient speed, rivals who fail to join the formation are exposed to the wind and lose contact with the front group. In a nearly 260-kilometer race like Paris-Roubaix, that separation can be decisive.

### The Science Behind Aerodynamic Protection

The research by Blocken, Defraeye, Koninckx, and others, published in the *Journal of Wind Engineering and Industrial Aerodynamics* in 2018, quantified through computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations and wind tunnel testing the reduction of aerodynamic drag within a cycling peloton. Their results demonstrated that a rider positioned inside a compact group can experience drag reductions of up to 40 to 50 percent compared to riding solo, and that this figure varies considerably depending on the position within the formation and the wind angle. Under crosswind conditions, the echelon formation becomes the optimal configuration to distribute protection among group members.

An earlier study by Defraeye and collaborators, published in the *Journal of Biomechanics* in 2010, had already established through CFD analysis and full-scale tests that the relative position between cyclists drastically modifies the drag coefficient. Their measurements confirmed that even small lateral spacing variations alter aerodynamic benefit, explaining why precision in positioning within an echelon is so critical. Barry, Burton, and Sheridan expanded on these findings in 2015 in *Sports Engineering*, analyzing drag interactions between cyclists in team formations and confirming that collective coordination multiplies the individual advantage each rider gains from drafting.

These studies dismantle the notion that echelons are merely an instinctive reaction to the wind. It is a maneuver requiring precise understanding of airflow physics, specific training to maintain exact positions at high speed, and constant communication between riders and sports directors via race radios.

### Paris-Roubaix 2022: Ineos Grenadiers’ Masterclass

The 2022 edition stands as the most eloquent case study on the power of echelons in this classic. Ineos Grenadiers planned their race around the wind-exposed sectors between Troisvilles and the first significant cobblestone stretch. With sustained crosswinds, the British team launched a coordinated echelon that split the peloton into several groups. Riders like Wout van Aert and Mathieu van der Poel, two of the top favorites, were caught in the second group and lost precious time that compromised the rest of their race.

Van Baarle capitalized on his teammates’ work to conserve energy in the kilometers leading up to the toughest cobblestone sectors. When he attacked solo with over 50 kilometers to go, he did so with reserves that his rivals had already spent battling the wind in disorganized groups. The Dutchman’s victory was not just a testament to his legs; it was the direct result of an echelon strategy that neutralized the competition before the race reached its decisive cobbled phase.

Tom Boonen, three-time Paris-Roubaix winner and regular classics analyst, has repeatedly stated that “in northern France, the race is won or lost on the asphalt kilometers before the cobbles, when the crosswind allows the strongest to separate themselves from those poorly positioned.” The 2022 edition proved this maxim with a clarity rarely seen.

### From 2019 to 2024: Tactical Evolution Edition by Edition

The 2019 Paris-Roubaix presented a different scenario. Philippe Gilbert won with a bold solo attack, and although crosswinds were present in some sections, the race was defined more by the cobbles than by echelons. Gilbert demonstrated that under moderate wind conditions, individual skill on the cobbles can prevail over collective tactics. However, teams were already beginning to incorporate meteorological data into their race plans more systematically.

The 2021 edition, held in October due to the pandemic, brought atypical weather conditions for the race. Sonny Colbrelli won under rain in a day where mud and slips displaced wind as the determining factor. Echelons had little protagonism, but this exception reinforced a known lesson: echelon tactics depend entirely on atmospheric conditions, and their absence does not diminish strategic importance when wind does appear.

In 2023, Mathieu van der Poel demonstrated absolute dominance over the cobbles with a crushing victory. Crosswinds played a lesser role compared to 2022, and the Dutchman imposed his physical superiority on the cobbled sectors. Attempts by some teams to force echelons on open stretches did not have the expected effect, partly because the gusts were not sustained enough to definitively fragment the peloton.

The 2024 edition confirmed Van der Poel as the race dominator with his second consecutive win. Rival teams tried to replicate Ineos’s 2022 strategy, seeking to provoke echelons in the kilometers before key sectors. However, Alpecin-Deceuninck’s ability to keep their leader always on the peloton’s front line neutralized those attempts. Patrick Lefevere, historic director of Soudal Quick-Step, acknowledged after the race that “against a rider like Van der Poel, echelons only work if you manage to leave him out, and that’s almost impossible when his team protects him like that.”

### The Role of Technology and Meteorology

WorldTour teams have progressively integrated technological tools in classics preparation. GPS devices allow post-race analysis of exact speed on each segment and riders’ positions at the moment echelons form. These data, combined with meteorological records, enable performance analysts to identify patterns: at which kilometers crosswinds exceed certain thresholds, how long an echelon typically lasts before the peloton regroups, and what minimum speed the front group needs for the separation to be irreversible.

Meteorology has become a planning variable that sports directors consult with the same seriousness as the altitude profile. Teams like Ineos, Soudal Quick-Step, and Jumbo-Visma—now Visma-Lease a Bike—work with specialized meteorological services that provide wind forecasts segmented by route section. This information conditions decisions such as team composition, role distribution, and even the exact moment to launch the echelon maneuver.

The contrast between the 2022 race, where crosswinds transformed the event, and 2023, where their absence allowed a more conventional race, illustrates how much this external variable determines the tactical relevance of echelons. It is not a strategy that can be applied in every edition; its effectiveness depends on the conjunction of team preparation and favorable atmospheric conditions.

### What Echelons Reveal About Modern Cycling

The evolution of echelons in Paris-Roubaix reflects a broader transformation in professional cycling. The race is no longer decided exclusively by individual endurance on the cobbles or brute power in the final kilometers. The integration of aerodynamic data, studies like those by Blocken and Defraeye, applied meteorology, and GPS analysis have turned tactical planning into a factor that can equal or surpass the difference in legs between riders.

Echelons are the most visible expression of this professionalization. In a classic where the wind blows from the side, a team that executes this maneuver precisely can eliminate objectively stronger rivals before the race reaches the decisive sectors. Van Baarle’s 2022 victory was not an anomaly; it was proof that in the Hell of the North, collective intelligence and wind reading can weigh as much as legs on the cobbles.
