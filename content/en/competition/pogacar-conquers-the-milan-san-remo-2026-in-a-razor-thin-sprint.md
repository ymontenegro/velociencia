La Classicissima delivered one of the most dramatic editions ever witnessed this Saturday. 298 kilometers from Pavia to San Remo, six and a half hours of racing, a massive crash at the most inopportune moment, and a finish decided by the thickness of a tire. Tadej Pogačar, the world champion, claimed the victory that had eluded him most in recent years. He did so bleeding from his left elbow, with his jersey torn but his determination intact.

## The Trap Before the Cipressa

All tactical plans shattered about five kilometers before the base of the Cipressa, with 32 kilometers still to go. A crash on the coastal road took down half the peloton and left unexpected names on the asphalt: Pogačar, Wout van Aert, Matteo Jorgenson, Biniam Girmay, and Giulio Pellizzari all went down. The Slovenian rose with a marked face and a shredded jersey, while his direct rivals also tried to recover.

The situation was unprecedented. The big favorite arrived at the decisive climb with visible scrapes and without part of his team, after Jan Christen abandoned due to an earlier crash more than 200 kilometers out. UAE Team Emirates-XRG thus lost a key piece for the work on the Cipressa. Even so, Brandon McNulty and Isaac del Toro maintained enough structure for Pogačar to rejoin on the left side of the group.

## Cipressa: Pogačar’s Carnage

Anyone expecting the crash to slow the world champion was completely mistaken. Pogačar reached the front of the peloton with four kilometers of climbing remaining and launched his first acceleration. McNulty had already imposed a fierce pace at the base, but it was Pogačar himself who turned pressure into selection. Three consecutive pace changes shattered what was left of the group.

Tom Pidcock demonstrated why he is among the best cyclists on the planet. Every time Pogačar accelerated, the Brit closed the gap with a coldness reminiscent of his best cyclocross days. Van der Poel also held on, though visibly more strained. The Cipressa took its toll on the rest: pure sprinters like Girmay were dropped without remedy, and the lead group was reduced to just over twenty riders before the summit.

## Poggio: Where the Best Separated

The descent of the Cipressa was Pidcock’s territory. The former Olympic mountain bike champion descended with a fluidity no one else matched, taking every corner on the cleanest possible line. Van der Poel used this section to feed, aware that the Poggio would decide everything.

And the Poggio delivered on its promise. Pogačar attacked from the first meters of the climb with an intensity that left no room for doubt. A first surge distanced half the reduced group. A second put Van der Poel on the ropes. The Dutchman, two-time winner of this race and defending champion after his 2025 victory, couldn’t follow the Slovenian’s third acceleration and fell back before the summit. The throne of the Poggio, which seemed rightfully his, went to Pogačar.

Only Pidcock survived. The Pinarello-Q36.5 rider stuck to Pogačar’s rear wheel as if his life depended on it, and together they crested the Poggio with a few seconds’ advantage over the Van der Poel group. The descent to San Remo was a shared vertigo: two men alone, at over 80 km/h, knowing the race would be decided in the last 300 meters of Via Roma.

## Via Roma: Half a Tire’s Difference

Pogačar launched a long sprint. At 250 meters from the line, he took the initiative down the center of Via Roma with the aerodynamic position he has perfected in recent years. Pidcock positioned himself in the slipstream, waiting for the moment to strike. At 100 meters, he changed lines and accelerated with all he had. For an eternal instant, the two bikes were parallel.

The photo finish confirmed what the human eye could not discern: Pogačar by half a tire. The official time was 6 hours, 35 minutes, and 49 seconds. Behind, Van Aert led the chasing group to complete the podium four seconds back, proving that the earlier crash had not cost him legs, only position.

## The Race of the Fallen and the Survivors

The 2026 edition of La Primavera will be remembered for chaos. Before the main crash approaching the Cipressa, an accident 55 kilometers from the start had already eliminated riders from INEOS Grenadiers and UAE. The narrow Ligurian coastal roads, combined with the nervousness of 200 riders fighting for position, became a minefield.

The teams that read the race best were UAE and Pinarello-Q36.5. The former maintained structure despite losing riders, with McNulty and Del Toro doing enormous work before the Cipressa. The latter protected Pidcock impeccably until the decisive moment, allowing him to arrive fresh for the final mano a mano. Visma-Lease a Bike paid dearly for Van Aert’s crash, losing contact at a critical moment and only able to claw back ground in the subsequent chase.

## The Primavera Pogačar Needed

Tadej Pogačar now has four of the five Monuments in his palmarès. Milan-San Remo was one of the two missing pieces, the race that had resisted him during seasons when he won practically everything else. Only Paris-Roubaix remains to complete the set. His 2026 victory came in the hardest possible way: with a battered body, without part of his team, and facing a rival who didn’t give him even half a meter of respite.

Tom Pidcock confirms himself as the most uncomfortable rival Pogačar can find in the classics. His versatility across road, mountain, and cyclocross gives him a reactive capacity few peloton riders can match. Van der Poel, meanwhile, is left with the frustration of not being able to follow on the Poggio, although his eighth place after the crash speaks to a class that never fully disappears.

The classics season is now launched. The next chapter will be written on the Flanders walls on April 5 and on the Roubaix cobbles a week later. Pogačar arrives with his hunger sated in San Remo but with the confidence that he can win anything, under any circumstance.
