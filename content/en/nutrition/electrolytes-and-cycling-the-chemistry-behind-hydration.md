## Electrically Charged Minerals

Electrolytes are minerals that, when dissolved in water, conduct electrical impulses. In a cyclist's body, four electrolytes have direct roles in performance: sodium, potassium, magnesium, and calcium. Sodium regulates blood plasma volume and intestinal water absorption. Potassium is involved in muscle contraction and heart rhythm. Magnesium participates in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including ATP production, according to Volpe’s (2013) review in the *American Journal of Lifestyle Medicine*. Calcium activates muscle fibers to contract.

Sweat carries these minerals out of the body. Unlike water, which the body signals to replenish through thirst, electrolyte loss does not generate such a clear signal. A cyclist can drink enough fluid yet still experience performance decline if sodium and potassium replacement is insufficient.

## Sodium: The Electrolyte Lost the Most

Sodium concentration in sweat varies between 200 and 1500 mg per hour depending on individual genetics, heat acclimatization level, and exercise intensity. A study by Baker et al., published in the *Journal of Sports Sciences*, measured sodium loss rates in endurance athletes and found differences up to sevenfold between subjects. A heat-acclimated cyclist may lose 300 mg of sodium per hour, while another unacclimated under the same environmental conditions can exceed 1500 mg.

Normal blood sodium concentration ranges between 135 and 145 mmol/L. When it falls below 135 mmol/L, hyponatremia occurs. On a four-hour ride at 30 °C, a cyclist sweating at 1.5 liters per hour and drinking only water can dilute plasma sodium to dangerous levels.

## Hyponatremia: The Risk of Drinking Too Much Water

Exercise-associated hyponatremia is not a minor issue. Tamara Hew-Butler, a researcher at Wayne State University, has documented fatal cases in endurance athletes who consumed large volumes of water without sodium replacement. A quantitative analysis published in the *Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine* reported that symptomatic hyponatremia appears most frequently in events lasting over four hours when participants consume fluids exceeding their sweat rate.

Initial symptoms include nausea, headache, and confusion. In severe cases, cerebral edema can cause seizures and death. The paradox is that these symptoms are easily confused with dehydration, leading some cyclists to drink even more water, worsening the problem. The difference between dehydration and hyponatremia is not academic: treatment is opposite. The former requires more fluid; the latter requires fluid restriction and sodium.

## What Sports Drinks Contain

Not all electrolyte sources are equivalent. Composition varies among water, commercial sports drinks, and effervescent electrolyte tablets. These differences affect both absorption and the actual amount of sodium available.

| Source | Sodium (mg/500 ml) | Potassium (mg/500 ml) | Carbohydrates (g/500 ml) | Osmolarity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Plain Water | 0 | 0 | 0 | Hypotonic |
| Gatorade | 230 | 60 | 36 | Isotonic |
| Precision Hydration PH 1500 | 750 | 0 | 0 | Hypotonic |
| SIS GO Electrolyte | 250 | 60 | 36 | Isotonic |
| Nuun Sport Tablet | 150 | 50 | 2 | Hypotonic |
| Oral Rehydration Solution (WHO) | 360 | 195 | 13.5 | Hypotonic |

Isotonic drinks combine sodium with carbohydrates, which promotes intestinal absorption via the sodium-glucose cotransport mechanism. Electrolyte tablets without carbohydrates are useful when the cyclist obtains energy from solid foods and only needs mineral replacement. The WHO formula, originally designed to treat dehydration from diarrhea, contains the highest potassium concentration and is effective as a low-cost option.

## Replacement Protocols According to Conditions

The amount of sodium per hour depends on three variables: intensity, ambient temperature, and individual sweat rate. General recommendations from scientific literature establish ranges that should be adjusted through personal testing.

| Condition | Intensity | Recommended Sodium (mg/hour) |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature < 20 °C | Moderate | 200 - 400 |
| Temperature < 20 °C | High | 300 - 600 |
| Temperature 20-30 °C | Moderate | 400 - 700 |
| Temperature 20-30 °C | High | 500 - 1000 |
| Temperature > 30 °C | Moderate | 600 - 1000 |
| Temperature > 30 °C | High | 800 - 1500 |

These values come from studies such as Shirreffs and Sawka (2011), which recommend individualizing sodium intake according to the concentration of this mineral in each athlete’s sweat. A cyclist who notices white salt stains on their jersey after riding is a salty sweater and likely falls at the higher end of the range.

## Magnesium and Cramps: What the Evidence Says

Popular belief associates muscle cramps with magnesium deficiency. Scientific evidence does not conclusively support this relationship. A review by Zhang et al., published in *Nutrients* (2017), analyzed available studies on magnesium supplementation and sports performance. The authors found supplementation benefits individuals with prior deficiency but does not improve performance or prevent cramps in people with normal levels.

Exercise-associated cramps have a more complex origin. The current hypothesis, supported by Martin Schwellnus from the University of Cape Town, points to neuromuscular alteration caused by fatigue, not specific electrolyte deficits. Magnesium plays important roles in post-exercise recovery and sleep quality, but taking it as an anti-cramp during a ride lacks solid backing.

The recommended daily intake of magnesium for active adults is around 400-420 mg for men and 310-320 mg for women. Foods such as almonds, spinach, and dark chocolate provide significant amounts. Supplementation makes sense when the diet is deficient, not as a specific anti-cramp strategy.

## How to Calculate Your Sweat Rate

Knowing how much you sweat allows you to adjust both fluid volume and electrolyte amounts. The weighing method is simple and requires a scale with at least 100-gram precision.

The protocol consists of weighing yourself without clothes before a one-hour ride in controlled conditions. During that hour, all fluid consumed in milliliters is recorded. At the end, weigh yourself again without clothes and dry. The weight difference in grams, plus the fluid ingested, equals the sweat rate in milliliters per hour. A cyclist weighing 75.0 kg before, 74.2 kg after, and who drank 500 ml during the ride has a sweat rate of 1300 ml/hour.

To estimate sodium loss more precisely, sweat tests exist that measure sodium concentration via adhesive patches. Brands like Precision Hydration and Gatorade Sports Science Institute offer this service. The result helps place the cyclist in a range and select the appropriate electrolyte concentration for each ride.

## Potassium and Calcium: The Other Two

Potassium is lost in sweat in smaller amounts than sodium, between 100 and 250 mg per hour. Its main function is maintaining the electrical potential of cell membranes, which directly affects cardiac muscle contraction. Most sports drinks contain modest potassium amounts. A banana provides about 400 mg and is a practical source during feed zone stops.

Calcium is lost in sweat at a rate of 20 to 60 mg per hour. It is usually not replaced during exercise because bone reserves act as a buffer. However, in cyclists with sustained high-volume training over months, accumulated calcium loss may contribute to lower bone density, a problem documented in professional road cyclists.

## Individualization as a Rule

No electrolyte protocol works the same for everyone. Genetic variables that determine sodium concentration in sweat, heat acclimatization level, body weight, and effort duration create unique combinations. A cyclist riding three hours on the Chilean coast at 28 °C has different needs than one training two hours in the high plateau at 12 °C. Sweat testing, recording weight before and after each ride, and observing symptoms such as premature fatigue or salt marks on clothing are accessible tools to adjust replacement strategy.
