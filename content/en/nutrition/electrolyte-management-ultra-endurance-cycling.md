## Electrolyte Management During Ultra-Endurance Cycling Races: Balancing Hydration and Muscle Function

A cyclist pedaling for eight, twelve, or even twenty-four hours can lose between 0.5 and 2.5 liters of sweat per hour, as documented by the American College of Sports Medicine’s official position published by Sawka et al. in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* (2007). Each liter of sweat carries between 230 and 1700 mg of sodium—amounts that, without adequate replacement, compromise muscle contraction, nerve transmission, and overall performance. Ultra-endurance cycling races represent the most extreme scenario for electrolyte balance: the prolonged effort multiplies cumulative losses and narrows the nutritional margin for error, turning every sip into a physiological decision.

Scientific interest in electrolyte management in endurance sports has grown over the past two decades. The review by Baker and Jeukendrup published in *Comprehensive Physiology* (2014) established that the optimal composition of replacement drinks must reflect individual sweat losses rather than follow generic formulas. This shifted the focus of sports nutrition: it is no longer just about drinking water but about replenishing specific minerals in calibrated proportions according to the athlete, intensity, and climate.

### Physiology of Electrolytes in Prolonged Exercise

Sodium is the predominant electrolyte in extracellular fluid, and its concentration determines plasma volume and the osmotic pressure that supports perfusion of active tissues. Sawka et al. (2007) demonstrated that sodium loss through sweating reduces plasma volume, elevates heart rate, and decreases cardiac output—a mechanism that explains the progressive decline in power during prolonged efforts without replacement. Potassium, the main intracellular cation, maintains the resting membrane potential of muscle and nerve fibers. Its depletion alters cellular repolarization, facilitating involuntary contractions and muscle weakness.

Calcium acts as a direct signal for muscle contraction by being released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum and binding to troponin, enabling the interaction between actin and myosin. Magnesium participates in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including the ATP hydrolysis necessary for muscle relaxation post-contraction. The review by Shirreffs and Sawka in the *Journal of Sports Sciences* (2011) emphasized that these four electrolytes operate as an integrated system: a deficit in any one generates compensations that overload the others, producing a cascading effect on neuromuscular function.

### Muscle Cramps: Beyond the Dehydration Myth

For decades, muscle cramps associated with exercise were assumed to be a direct consequence of dehydration and electrolyte loss. The review by Maughan and Shirreffs published in *Sports Medicine* (2019) presented a more complex picture. Evidence suggests that exercise-induced cramps have a multifactorial origin where neuromuscular fatigue, electrolyte disturbances, and individual factors such as genetic predisposition and injury history converge.

Bergeron (2008), in an analysis published in *Current Sports Medicine Reports*, found that athletes with higher sodium concentrations in sweat had a greater incidence of cramps during prolonged competitions in heat. This does not exclude the neuromuscular component but confirms that sodium deficit acts as an aggravating factor in a context of accumulated fatigue. In ultra-endurance races, where both elements—extreme fatigue and prolonged mineral loss—coexist for hours, the risk of cramps is synergistically amplified. The most supported preventive strategy in the literature combines individualized electrolyte replacement with effort intensity management.

### Optimal Drink Composition and Replacement Protocol

Baker and Jeukendrup (2014) established reference ranges for sports drink composition during prolonged exercise: 300 to 800 mg of sodium per liter, 75 to 200 mg of potassium per liter, and a carbohydrate concentration of 6 to 8 percent to optimize intestinal absorption via sodium-glucose cotransport. These values represent a starting point that must be adjusted according to each athlete’s sweat rate and sodium concentration, variables measurable through standardized sweat tests.

The joint position of the ACSM, the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, and Dietitians of Canada, published by Thomas et al. (2016), recommends that endurance athletes consume between 400 and 800 milliliters of fluid per hour during exercise, adapting volume to limit body mass loss to less than 2 percent. In ultra-endurance cycling, where efforts exceed six hours, this recommendation should be complemented with planned intakes every 15 to 20 minutes rather than relying on thirst sensation, which diminishes with fatigue and environmental conditions.

Magnesium supplementation during the race warrants particular attention. Shirreffs and Sawka (2011) noted that although magnesium losses through sweat are relatively low (between 0.2 and 1.5 mmol per liter), cumulative depletion in efforts longer than six hours can compromise muscle relaxation and ATP production. Doses of 200 to 400 mg of elemental magnesium spread throughout the event, preferably as citrate or bisglycinate due to their higher bioavailability, represent a literature-backed strategy without significant risk of gastrointestinal discomfort.

### Environmental Conditions and Individual Variability

Baker (2017) published an exhaustive review in *Sports Medicine* on variability in sweat rate and sodium concentration among athletes. His data showed that sodium concentration in sweat ranges between 10 and 90 mmol per liter (230 to 2070 mg per liter), with interindividual variability exceeding the effect of exercise intensity or ambient temperature. This means two cyclists competing in the same race, at the same pace and under the same sun, can have sodium replacement needs differing by a factor of three or more.

In extreme heat environments, Casa et al. (2015) documented in the official position of the National Athletic Trainers' Association that sweat rates can reach 2.5 liters per hour in acclimated athletes. Heat acclimation—a process requiring 10 to 14 days of progressive exposure—modifies both sweat volume and composition: acclimated athletes produce more diluted sweat with lower sodium concentration per liter but greater total volume. This phenomenon requires recalibrating replacement strategies after each acclimation period, as previous formulas may be insufficient in volume or excessive in salt concentration.

Altitude adds another relevant variable for ultra-endurance cycling. The lower partial pressure of oxygen increases ventilation and insensible water losses, while altitude-induced diuresis accelerates electrolyte excretion. Although specific data for ultra-endurance cycling at altitude are limited, Sawka et al. (2007) recommended increasing fluid and sodium intake by 10 to 20 percent compared to sea level conditions during the first days of exposure above 2500 meters.

### Common Errors and Practical Applications

One of the most documented errors in ultra-endurance is overhydration with pure water, which dilutes plasma sodium concentration and can cause hyponatremia, a potentially fatal condition. Sawka et al. (2007) were categorical in stating that drinking above sweat rate without sodium replacement constitutes a real risk, especially in slower cyclists who have greater access to aid stations. Exercise-associated hyponatremia presents symptoms ranging from nausea and confusion to seizures and cerebral edema, and its incidence has paradoxically increased in the era of aggressive hydration.

Another common mistake is relying exclusively on commercial supplements without knowing the actual composition of each product. Thomas et al. (2016) emphasized that athletes must read labels and calculate the amount of sodium, potassium, and other electrolytes per dose, comparing it with their estimated needs. An energy gel, for example, can contain between 20 and 200 mg of sodium depending on the brand—a tenfold difference that can accumulate significantly over twelve hours of racing.

Pre-competition planning is as relevant as execution during the event. Conducting a sweat test during training that simulates race conditions allows estimation of sweat rate and individual sodium concentration. Baker (2017) described accessible protocols including measuring body weight before and after training, corrected for fluid intake, as a reliable method to calculate total losses. With these data, the cyclist and their nutrition team can design a specific replacement plan that minimizes both dehydration and hyponatremia risk.

### Final Considerations for the Ultra-Endurance Cyclist

Electrolyte management in ultra-endurance races admits no improvisation. The accumulated evidence over the past two decades—from the ACSM position stand of 2007 to the most recent reviews on cramps and sweat composition—converges on one message: replacement must be individualized, based on measured data rather than generic estimates. Sodium is the priority electrolyte due to its high concentration in sweat and central role in plasma volume, but potassium, magnesium, and calcium complete a system that functions as an interdependent unit.

Every ultra-endurance race is a physiological experiment where the cyclist tests their nutritional strategy under conditions that are never exactly the same. Training nutrition with the same discipline as power and cadence is not a luxury but a competitive necessity. The data exist, measurement tools are accessible, and the difference between a good result and a dropout due to cramps or hyponatremia can come down to a few hundred milligrams of sodium per hour.
