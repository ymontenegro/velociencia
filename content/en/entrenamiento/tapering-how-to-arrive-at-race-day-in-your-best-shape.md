## The Week Nobody Wants to Do

There are ten days left until the season's target race. The cyclist has accumulated months of training, thousands of kilometers, dozens of interval sessions. Emotional logic dictates that they should keep training hard to reach peak form. Physiology says exactly the opposite. Reducing training load in the weeks leading up to a competition—a process known as tapering—is one of the interventions with the strongest scientific evidence in endurance sports, and one of the hardest to accept psychologically.

According to Bosquet, Montpetit, Arvisais, and Mujika (2007) in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, a meta-analysis of 27 controlled studies concluded that a two-week taper in which training volume is exponentially reduced by between 41% and 60%, without modifying intensity or frequency, is the most efficient strategy to maximize performance gains. The average improvement was 3%, with a typical range between 0.5% and 6%. In cycling, a 3% improvement in power can represent several minutes in a one-hour time trial.

## What Happens Inside the Body When You Stop Pushing Hard

Tapering is not passive rest. It is a calculated reduction of training stress that allows the body to complete adaptive processes that were underway but blocked by accumulated fatigue. According to Mujika and Padilla (2003) in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, physiological adaptations during a taper span multiple systems: increased plasma volume and hemoglobin concentration, normalization of hormonal markers (testosterone, cortisol), restoration of muscle and liver glycogen, and improved contractility of muscle fibers.

According to Neary, Martin, and Quinney (2003) in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, a 7-day taper in trained cyclists produced measurable changes in the metabolic properties of individual muscle fibers. Type I fibers increased their oxidative enzyme activity, while type II fibers showed greater glycolytic capacity. The combined result was a 5.4% improvement in performance in a 20-kilometer time trial. These adaptations do not occur during heavy training. They occur when part of the load is withdrawn and the body can allocate resources to repair and enhance what training has controlledly damaged.

## Three Variables: Volume, Intensity, Frequency

The taper equation has three components, and the evidence is clear about which should change and which should remain. Total training volume is reduced between 40% and 60%. Intensity is maintained or even slightly increased, with short high-power intervals included up to the last days before competition. Training frequency is marginally reduced, by no more than 20%.

According to Mujika and Padilla (2003), maintaining intensity is the most critical variable of tapering. Athletes who reduce volume but preserve high-intensity interval sessions during the unloading period achieve greater improvements than those who simply reduce everything equally. The reason is that high-intensity stimuli preserve neuromuscular and cardiovascular adaptations gained during months of training, while volume reduction allows tissue recovery. A cyclist in taper does not stop doing intervals: they do fewer total kilometers but maintain 2 or 3 quality efforts per week.

## Progressive Taper vs Step Taper

Not all tapers are equal. The evidence distinguishes two main formats. The step taper reduces volume abruptly to a fixed level and maintains it constant throughout the unloading period. The progressive taper reduces volume gradually, either linearly or exponentially, over the days before competition.

According to Bosquet et al. (2007), nonlinear progressive tapers—in which volume reduction is greater at the end of the period than at the beginning—produce better results than step tapers. Rapid exponential reduction, where volume drops sharply in the first days and stabilizes at a very low level at the end, was the format associated with the greatest performance gains in the meta-analysis. The explanation is that the step taper may reduce volume too early, causing partial detraining, or too late, not allowing full supercompensation.

| Taper Type | Volume Reduction | Pattern | Performance Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Step | 50% from day 1 | Constant | Moderate improvement (1-2%) |
| Linear Progressive | Gradual, equal each day | Linear | Moderate-high improvement (2-3%) |
| Rapid Exponential Progressive | Greater at start, minimal at end | Exponential | Maximum improvement (3-6%) |
| Slow Exponential Progressive | Gradual accelerated | Gentle exponential | High improvement (2-4%) |

## Overload Before the Taper: The Compressed Protocol

A variant gaining traction in recent research is combining a short overload phase followed by tapering. According to Rønnestad and Vikmoen (2019) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, an 11-day compressed protocol—6 days of daily high-intensity training followed by 5 days of step taper—produced improvements of 4.0% in VO2max and 5.0% in peak 1-minute power in elite cyclists. The control group, which performed a conventional taper without prior overload, improved only 0.8% and 0.9% respectively.

The proposed mechanism is that concentrated overload deliberately amplifies functional fatigue, creating a state of acute overtraining from which the body recovers during the subsequent taper. The resulting supercompensation is greater than that produced by a conventional taper because the "gap" between fatigue and recovery is deeper. Wang, Wang, Gao, and Zhong (2023) published in *PLoS One* a meta-analysis of 14 studies confirming significant improvements in time trial performance and time to exhaustion after tapering periods, reinforcing the evidence base for planned volume reduction before competitions.

This protocol is not for everyone. It requires the capacity to absorb a week of very high load without injury, something only viable for cyclists with a solid training base and experience managing fatigue. For an amateur training 8 to 10 hours per week, a moderate version could consist of one week at 120% of usual volume followed by 10 days of progressive reduction.

## Two Weeks Before the Race: A Concrete Plan

For an amateur cyclist preparing for a Gran Fondo, a sportive, or a target time trial, the evidence suggests the following 14-day taper scheme with exponential volume reduction and intensity maintenance.

| Day | Volume (% of usual) | Typical Session |
|-----|---------------------|-----------------|
| -14 | 85% | Reduced long ride + 3×5min at 100% FTP |
| -13 | 80% | Aerobic ride |
| -12 | 75% | Intervals: 4×4min at 105% FTP, 3min recovery |
| -11 | 70% | Easy ride |
| -10 | 65% | Rest |
| -9  | 60% | Short ride + 3×3min at 105% FTP |
| -8  | 55% | Short aerobic ride |
| -7  | 50% | Intervals: 3×3min at 110% FTP, 3min recovery |
| -6  | 45% | Very easy ride |
| -5  | 40% | Rest |
| -4  | 40% | Openers: 3×1min at 120% FTP + easy ride |
| -3  | 35% | Easy 45min ride |
| -2  | 30% | Rest or very easy 30min |
| -1  | 20% | Activation: 20min easy + 2×30s max sprints |
| 0   | —   | Competition |

Volume progression drops from 85% to 20% in an accelerated fashion. Intensity sessions appear every 2 or 3 days until four days before competition, with short efforts maintaining neuromuscular activation without causing residual fatigue. The last two days are minimal volume, with a brief activation session the day before including short sprints to "wake up" the neuromuscular system.

## The Mind Doesn’t Help: Why It’s So Hard to Reduce

The biggest barrier to executing an effective taper is not physiological but psychological. The cyclist who has trained 12 to 15 hours per week for months experiences anxiety when volume drops to 6 hours. Thoughts of lost fitness arise, that rivals will be training more, that legs feel "heavy" or "asleep." That sensation of heavy legs during the first days of taper is normal and expected: it reflects ongoing muscle repair, not a loss of fitness.

Evidence shows that aerobic fitness does not significantly deteriorate in the first two weeks of volume reduction as long as intensity is maintained. VO2max remains stable for at least 14 to 21 days with reduced load. Lactate threshold power can even improve during taper if quality stimuli are preserved. What is lost with a taper that is too long—more than three weeks—is metabolic efficiency and some peripheral adaptations, which is why evidence places the optimal range between 8 and 14 days for cyclists.

## The Mistake of Doing Nothing the Last Week

The opposite extreme also fails. A cyclist who stops training completely the 7 days before competition arrives on race day with rested legs but a dulled neuromuscular system. Muscle activation, intermuscular coordination, and the ability to produce power quickly deteriorate without regular stimuli. According to Mujika and Padilla (2003), training frequency should not be reduced by more than 20% during taper, and sessions should include brief efforts at intensities equal to or higher than competition.

The optimal taper is an exercise in precision, not inactivity. It is about reducing total stress on the organism while maintaining signals that preserve acute performance capacity. The cyclist who arrives on race day having executed a disciplined taper—with fewer kilometers but quality intervals until the end—will have fresh legs and a neuromuscular system ready to respond. That 2% to 6% margin documented by science is not gained by training more in the last days. It is gained by trusting accumulated work and giving the body just enough time to express it.
