## The Window Most Waste

Rønnestad and colleagues demonstrated that 25 weeks of heavy strength training improve average power by 7% without increasing body weight. But these gains are only built when there is room for recovery. November to January in the northern hemisphere, May to July in the southern hemisphere: these are the months when cycling volume drops to a minimum, competitions are distant, and the body can absorb a stimulus different from pedaling. The preseason is the only time of year when a cyclist can train maximal strength with the frequency and intensity necessary to produce real neuromuscular adaptations. Trying to squeeze in heavy squat sessions during the competition phase, when the body is already absorbing 15 weekly hours of cycling, is a recipe for injury or overtraining.

The physiological logic is straightforward. When cycling volume is low, limited to rides in Z1-Z2 (<75% FTP) of 3 to 5 hours per week, the musculoskeletal system has recovery capacity available. That capacity allows tolerating gym loads that would be unsustainable during the season. The cyclist who takes advantage of these months to build strength enters the Build phase with a more powerful engine without having sacrificed their aerobic base.

## What the Evidence Says

Rønnestad et al. (2010) published one of the most influential studies on strength training in cycling in the *European Journal of Applied Physiology*. Over 25 weeks, a group of well-trained cyclists combined heavy strength training with their usual cycling program. The strength group improved average power by 7% compared to the control group that only pedaled. Power in 5-minute efforts increased by 8.5%. And the data that debunks the most widespread myth: there was no significant increase in body mass. The cyclists got stronger without getting heavier.

Five years later, Rønnestad et al. (2015) published a follow-up in the *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports* answering the logical question: what happens when preseason ends? Cyclists who maintained a single weekly strength session during the competitive season preserved the power gains obtained in preseason. Those who abandoned the gym completely progressively lost those improvements over 12 weeks. The message was clear: build strength in preseason and maintain it with one session per week during the season.

## Four Phases, One Progression

Strength periodization in preseason follows a sequence of four phases that progressively prepare the neuromuscular system. Skipping phases or starting directly with heavy loads is a fast track to knee or lower back injury. Each phase has a specific physiological goal and a minimum duration for adaptations to consolidate.

The first phase is anatomical adaptation, lasting 2 to 3 weeks. The goal is to prepare tendons, ligaments, and connective tissue for the loads to come. Work is done with sets of 15 repetitions at 50-60% of estimated maximum, prioritizing technique and full range of motion. A cyclist who hasn’t stepped into a gym for months needs these weeks for their knees, hips, and lower back to get accustomed to bearing external load.

The second phase is functional hypertrophy, lasting 3 to 4 weeks. Here volume increases to 3-4 sets of 8 to 12 repetitions at 65-75% of maximum. The goal is not to gain muscle size like a bodybuilder, but to increase the cross-sectional area of type IIa fibers—the fast, fatigue-resistant fibers that contribute to sustained power in cycling. Muscle mass increase in this phase is minimal if nutrition is kept at maintenance calories.

The third phase is maximal strength, the core of the program, extending 4 to 6 weeks. Loads rise to 80-90% of maximum, repetitions drop to 3-6 per set, and sets increase to 4-5. This is where neuromuscular adaptations occur that improve the ability to recruit motor units efficiently. The cyclist is not trying to lift record weights; they are teaching their nervous system to activate more muscle fibers in a coordinated way.

The fourth phase is power conversion, lasting 2 to 3 weeks. Load decreases to 60-70% but execution speed increases. Sets of 6 to 8 repetitions performed explosively, emphasizing the fast concentric phase. This phase translates the maximal strength acquired into power applicable to the pedal: the ability to generate force at high velocity, exactly what happens in a sprint, a group attack, or a tempo climb.

## The Exercises That Move the Needle

Not all gym exercises have the same transfer to cycling. Multi-joint movements involving the posterior chain and knee extension show the highest correlation with pedal power. The back squat is the king exercise due to its simultaneous activation of quadriceps, glutes, and hamstrings in a pattern that partially replicates pedaling mechanics during the push phase.

Deadlifts work the posterior chain integrally—from hamstrings to spinal erectors—strengthening the cyclist’s ability to maintain a stable position on the bike during prolonged efforts. Leg press allows working with heavy loads in a knee extension pattern with less technical demand than the squat, useful for cyclists with ankle or hip mobility limitations. Unilateral step-ups correct strength asymmetries between legs, a common problem in cyclists who pedal thousands of kilometers yearly with a dominant leg.

Hip thrusts isolate the glutes, the most powerful muscle group in the body and the main force generator in pedaling between the 12 and 5 o’clock positions of the pedal stroke. Calf raises complete the lower chain work, strengthening the gastrocnemius and soleus muscles that transmit force to the pedal through the ankle.

## 8-Week Plan

The following plan covers the four periodization phases with a progression designed for a cyclist who has access to a gym 2-3 times per week during preseason. Cycling sessions during this period are low intensity, limited to Z1-Z2, aiming to maintain the aerobic base without competing with recovery from strength work.

| Week | Phase               | Main Exercises                                   | Sets x Reps | Load (% 1RM) | Bike Sessions     |
|-------|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| 1     | Anatomical Adaptation | Squat, leg press, step-up, hip thrust, calves  | 3x15        | 50%          | 3x Z1 (1h)        |
| 2     | Anatomical Adaptation | Squat, deadlift, leg press, step-up, hip thrust, calves | 3x15 | 55-60%       | 3x Z1 (1h)        |
| 3     | Hypertrophy          | Squat, deadlift, leg press, step-up, hip thrust | 3x12        | 65%          | 3x Z1-Z2 (1h15)   |
| 4     | Hypertrophy          | Squat, deadlift, leg press, step-up, hip thrust | 4x10        | 70%          | 3x Z1-Z2 (1h15)   |
| 5     | Hypertrophy          | Squat, deadlift, leg press, hip thrust          | 4x8         | 75%          | 2x Z1-Z2 (1h30)   |
| 6     | Maximal Strength     | Squat, deadlift, leg press, hip thrust          | 4x5         | 82%          | 2x Z1-Z2 (1h30)   |
| 7     | Maximal Strength     | Squat, deadlift, leg press, hip thrust          | 5x4         | 85-88%       | 2x Z1-Z2 (1h30)   |
| 8     | Power Conversion     | Explosive squat, explosive step-up, hip thrust, box jumps | 3x6 | 60-65% (explosive) | 3x Z1-Z2 (1h30) |

Gym sessions should be scheduled with at least 6 hours separation from bike sessions. If the cyclist trains on the bike in the morning, the strength session goes in the afternoon, or vice versa. In weeks where recovery becomes challenging, it is preferable to drop a bike session rather than a strength session, since in preseason the priority is the strength stimulus.

## The Compatibility Everyone Worries About

The most common question among cyclists considering the gym is whether lifting weights will make them heavier. Rønnestad addressed this issue directly in his 2010 study. Cyclists in the strength group showed no significant increase in body mass after 25 weeks of combined training. The physiological reason is that the primary adaptations to strength training in well-trained cyclists are neuromuscular, not hypertrophic. The nervous system learns to recruit more motor units and coordinate their activation more efficiently, without needing to build additional muscle in amounts relevant to the scale.

The concurrent aerobic training volume also limits hypertrophy. The interference effect described by Hickson in 1980, where simultaneous endurance training attenuates muscle mass gains, works in the cyclist’s favor here. Molecular signals activated by bike sessions (AMPK, PGC-1α) partially inhibit protein synthesis pathways (mTOR) that would produce hypertrophy in a pure strength program. The net result is a cyclist with greater force production capacity per kilogram of body weight—exactly what is needed to climb mountains faster.

## After Preseason

The costliest mistake is quitting the gym when the season starts. Rønnestad et al. (2015) showed that a single weekly maintenance session, with 2-3 sets of 4-6 reps at 80-85% of maximum in squat and deadlift, is enough to preserve strength gains throughout the competitive season. This session can be placed on an active recovery day, away from key interval sessions, and should not last more than 30-40 minutes including warm-up.

Cyclists who maintained that weekly session in Rønnestad’s study showed superior performance in 5-minute and 30-minute power tests at season’s end compared to those who dropped strength training completely. The difference was statistically significant and practically relevant: this is not a theoretical margin but real watts that manifest on the road. Building strength in preseason takes weeks of disciplined work. Maintaining it requires one session per week and the will not to abandon the gym when the bike starts demanding more hours.
