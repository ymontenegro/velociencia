## One Stimulus per Block, Not Ten

In 2004, Israeli physiologist Vladimir Issurin published the results of a decade-long study with Olympic athletes that transformed the way training is planned in cyclic sports. His proposal was counterintuitive: instead of training endurance, strength, speed, and power simultaneously over weeks, concentrate on a single type of stimulus in short blocks of 2 to 4 weeks. Athletes who followed this model in Issurin’s initial studies improved between 3% and 5% more than those who maintained traditional periodization, according to data published in *Journal of Sports Medicine and Physical Fitness*.

The traditional model, inherited from Matveyev’s Soviet school in the 1960s, distributes multiple physical qualities across long mesocycles. A typical cyclist trains endurance, threshold, VO2max, and sprint within the same week, gradually rotating emphasis. Block periodization breaks with this logic. Each 2 to 4-week block targets a single primary adaptation, and volume dedicated to other qualities is reduced to the minimum necessary to maintain them. The physiological argument is that molecular adaptation signals compete when trained simultaneously, and concentrating the stimulus allows for a more potent adaptive response.

## Accumulate, Transmute, Realize: Three Phases with One Goal Each

The classic sequence of the block model is divided into three phases with distinct functions. The accumulation block lasts 2 to 3 weeks and prioritizes building aerobic base. Volume is high—between 15 and 20 hours per week for an advanced amateur—and intensity is predominantly kept below 80% of FTP. Key sessions are long rides of 3 to 5 hours at conversational pace, aiming to maximize mitochondrial adaptations and metabolic efficiency.

The transmutation block, also 2 to 3 weeks, reduces volume to 12–16 hours per week and raises intensity toward the threshold zone, between 85% and 105% of FTP. This phase includes sweet spot intervals, tempo sets, and sustained work near lactate threshold. According to Rønnestad et al. (2016) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, this concentrated threshold work block produced a 4.6% improvement in 40-minute power compared to 1.8% in cyclists who distributed the same stimulus over a longer period.

The realization block closes the sequence with 1 to 2 weeks of low volume—8 to 12 hours per week—and high-intensity efforts: sprints, supra-threshold intervals, and race simulations. Total volume is deliberately reduced to allow supercompensation, the physiological process by which performance peaks after a recovery period.

## FTP +8.2% in 12 Weeks Compared to the Traditional Model

Evidence in cycling has grown steadily over the last decade. According to Breil et al. (2010) in *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, an 11-day concentrated HIIT block in cross-country skiers produced a 6% increase in VO2max and 8% in peak power—adaptations that a control group with distributed training did not achieve in the same period. This study was among the first to demonstrate that load concentration also works in endurance sports, not just strength disciplines.

According to Rønnestad et al. (2023) in *International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance*, a direct 12-week comparison between block periodization and traditional periodization in sub-elite cyclists revealed significant differences in the three main measured variables.

| Variable | Blocks | Traditional |
|----------|---------|------------|
| FTP Improvement | +8.2% | +4.7% |
| VO2max Improvement | +6.1% | +4.3% |
| 40km TT Improvement | -3.4% | -1.8% |

Cyclists in the block group trained the same total hours as the traditional group. The difference was not volume but organization: concentrating threshold work in 3 consecutive weeks produced adaptations that 12 weeks of mixed stimuli did not match. According to García-Pallarés et al. (2010) in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, elite kayakers who switched from traditional to block periodization for two consecutive seasons improved their VO2max by 8.6% and lactate threshold power by 9.2%, unusual values for athletes already near their physiological ceiling.

Not all studies are so conclusive. According to Grgic et al. (2018) in *Sports Medicine*, a meta-analysis of 14 studies found that differences between block and traditional periodization were statistically significant but modest in magnitude. The authors noted that the block model’s advantage amplifies in athletes with more years of training and diminishes in beginners, for whom almost any organized stimulus produces improvements.

## Why It Works Better When You Have Little Time

Block periodization has a practical advantage rarely mentioned in studies but decisive for amateur cyclists balancing work, family, and fragmented schedules. Concentrating on a single stimulus per block radically simplifies planning. Instead of trying to fit endurance, threshold, VO2max, and sprint sessions in the same week—requiring at least 5 or 6 distinct workouts—the cyclist dedicates 3 weeks to long, easy rides, then 3 weeks to threshold intervals, then tapers for competition.

This simplicity reduces cognitive load and facilitates adherence. An accumulation block can coincide with holidays or periods of lower work demands, when there is time for long rides. A transmutation block, with shorter but intense sessions, fits better into demanding workweeks. Block periodization allows aligning training demands with life realities outside the bike—something the traditional model, with its constant weekly variety requirement, complicates.

Recovery is also managed more clearly. Each block ends with unloading days that allow the cyclist to absorb accumulated load before switching stimulus. In the traditional model, fatigue accumulates more diffusely and the risk of chronic overtraining increases when the cyclist neglects unloading periods, precisely because there is no clear phase boundary.

## Nine Weeks to Peak: A Pre-Gran Fondo Plan

For an amateur cyclist with 10 to 14 hours weekly availability, a 9-week block plan aimed at a Gran Fondo could be structured as follows.

### Weeks 1-3: Accumulation

| Day | Session | Intensity | Duration |
|-----|---------|-----------|----------|
| Monday | Rest | — | — |
| Tuesday | Aerobic ride | Z2 | 1h 30min |
| Wednesday | Ride with 4×8min sweet spot | Z2/Z3 | 2h |
| Thursday | Active recovery | Z1 | 1h |
| Friday | Aerobic ride | Z2 | 1h 30min |
| Saturday | Long ride | Z2 | 4h |
| Sunday | Medium ride | Z2 | 2h 30min |

### Weeks 4-6: Transmutation

| Day | Session | Intensity | Duration |
|-----|---------|-----------|----------|
| Monday | Rest | — | — |
| Tuesday | Intervals 3×15min at 95% FTP | Z3/Z4 | 1h 30min |
| Wednesday | Aerobic ride | Z2 | 1h 30min |
| Thursday | Intervals 5×5min at 105% FTP | Z4/Z5 | 1h 30min |
| Friday | Rest | — | — |
| Saturday | Ride with 2×20min at 90% FTP | Z3 | 3h |
| Sunday | Aerobic ride | Z2 | 2h |

### Weeks 7-9: Realization

| Day | Session | Intensity | Duration |
|-----|---------|-----------|----------|
| Monday | Rest | — | — |
| Tuesday | Sprints 8×30s max effort | Z5/Z6 | 1h |
| Wednesday | Easy ride | Z1 | 1h |
| Thursday | Intervals 4×4min at 110% FTP | Z5 | 1h |
| Friday | Rest | — | — |
| Saturday | Open ride with free efforts | Z1/Z5 | 1h 30min |
| Sunday | Competition: Gran Fondo | — | — |

Weekly volume progression deliberately decreases: around 13.5 hours in accumulation, 11 hours in transmutation, and 5.5 hours in realization. This reduction is not weakness but physiological strategy: the body needs time to absorb accumulated load and express adaptations as performance.

## One Approach, One Block, One Adaptation

Block periodization does not replace or invalidate the traditional model. According to available evidence, it works better for cyclists with at least two years of base training who have plateaued with conventional schemes. Its advantage does not lie in an unknown physiological secret but in stimulus concentration: dedicating entire weeks to a single quality sends a clearer adaptive signal to the body, which responds with greater magnitude.

The risk of the block model is monotony and the temptation to abandon discipline within the current block to jump ahead to the next phase. A cyclist in the middle of an accumulation block who starts adding VO2max intervals “because they feel good” dilutes the aerobic stimulus without gaining enough at the high-intensity end. Discipline lies in respecting each phase, trusting that adaptations will come in the next, and arriving at race day having built each layer upon the last.
