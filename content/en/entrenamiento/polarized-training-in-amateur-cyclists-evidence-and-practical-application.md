## The gray zone trap

An 11.7% improvement in VO2max in just nine weeks. That was the difference recorded by the polarized training group compared to those who followed threshold, high-intensity, or high-volume protocols in the study by Stöggl and Sperlich published in Frontiers in Physiology in 2014. The 48 endurance athletes in the trial — runners, cyclists, triathletes, and cross-country skiers — also gained 17.4% more time to exhaustion and 5.1% in peak power. The numbers leave little room for doubt: distributing effort between two opposite poles produced superior adaptations over any other approach.

The problem is that most amateur cyclists train precisely in the range this model seeks to eliminate. They go out and ride "at pace," neither easy nor hard, accumulating hours in a moderate intensity zone that generates considerable fatigue without triggering the physiological adaptations of either extreme. Stephen Seiler, the Norwegian physiologist who documented training patterns of elite athletes worldwide, described it clearly in his 2010 review in the International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance: the best distribute approximately 80% of their sessions below the first ventilatory threshold and concentrate the remaining 20% at high intensities, above the second threshold. The middle zone remains nearly empty.

That distribution, known as the polarized model or the 80/20 rule, did not emerge from a laboratory. Seiler first observed it in the training logs of world-class rowers, cyclists, and cross-country skiers, then validated it with physiological data. The logic behind it is straightforward: easy sessions allow volume accumulation without compromising recovery, while hard sessions generate the mechanical and metabolic stimulus that forces the body to adapt.

## What the evidence says for cyclists

Neal and colleagues put the model to the test in 2013 with a crossover design in 12 trained cyclists, published in the Journal of Applied Physiology. Over six weeks, one group followed a polarized distribution (80% in the low zone, 0% in the middle zone, 20% in the high zone) and another followed a threshold distribution (57% low, 43% middle, 0% high). Results favored the polarized group across all measured variables: peak power increased by 8% versus 3%, lactate threshold improved by 9% versus 2%, and high-intensity exercise capacity surged by 85% compared to 37% in the threshold group.

There is a nuance that cannot be ignored. Neal's cyclists were trained, not beginners. The 2014 study by Muñoz and colleagues, also published in the International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, worked with 30 recreational runners — not cyclists, but endurance athletes with a profile comparable to the amateur we are interested in. Over ten weeks, the polarized group (77% low, 3% middle, 20% high) improved their 10-kilometer time by 5.0%, versus 3.6% for the group that concentrated work between thresholds. The difference did not reach statistical significance in the full group, but it did in the subgroup of athletes who best adhered to the prescribed program. The message is clear: the model works, but only if executed with discipline.

The meta-analysis by Rosenblat, Perrotta, and Vicenzino, published in 2019 in the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, gathered the available randomized controlled trials and found a moderate effect size (ES = -0.66; 95% CI: -1.17 to -0.15) favoring polarized over threshold training. The 2015 review by Hydren and Cohen in the same journal reached similar conclusions, though it cautioned that most evidence comes from interventions lasting between 6 and 16 weeks. There are no long-term studies confirming whether the advantage holds over years of consistent training.

## Power-based training zones

Before building any plan, zones must be defined. FTP (Functional Threshold Power — the power that can be sustained for approximately one hour) is the reference. A 20-minute all-out test, multiplied by 0.95, provides a reasonable estimate. From that value, zones are built.

| Zone | Name | % of FTP | Perceived effort |
|------|------|----------|------------------|
| Z1 | Active recovery | < 55% | Minimal effort, conversation flows effortlessly |
| Z2 | Endurance | 55–75% | Comfortable pace, controlled breathing, sustainable for hours |
| Z3 | Tempo | 76–90% | Maintaining a fluid conversation starts to become difficult |
| Z4 | Threshold | 91–105% | Sustained effort at the limit, maintainable for 20 to 60 minutes |
| Z5 | VO2max | 106–120% | Very labored breathing, sustainable for 3 to 8 minutes |
| Z6 | Anaerobic | > 120% | Maximum effort, between 30 seconds and 2 minutes |

In the polarized model, the bulk of time is spent in Z1 and Z2. Quality sessions are performed in Z5 and, occasionally, Z6. Zones Z3 and Z4 are not deliberately targeted — they appear only as transitions during warm-ups, recoveries between intervals, or brief stretches on a hilly route.

## Weekly plan: 6 to 8 available hours

The amateur cyclist with an office job, family, and commitments outside cycling typically has between 6 and 8 weekly hours available. With that constraint, the polarized model fits particularly well because it maximizes the return on every invested hour. The proposed structure distributes two interval sessions in Z5 separated by aerobic riding days in Z2 and one full rest day.

| Day | Session | Duration | Zone | Description |
|-----|---------|----------|------|-------------|
| Monday | Rest | — | — | Full recovery, no bike |
| Tuesday | VO2max intervals | 60 min | Z5 | 15 min progressive warm-up in Z2. Main block: 5 × 4 min at 108–112% FTP with 4 min recovery in Z1. 10 min cool-down in Z1 |
| Wednesday | Aerobic ride | 75 min | Z2 | Steady pedaling at 60–70% FTP, cadence 85–95 rpm. No abrupt pace changes |
| Thursday | Active recovery | 45 min | Z1 | Very easy pedaling below 55% FTP. Can be done on the trainer if weather does not allow outdoor riding |
| Friday | VO2max intervals | 55 min | Z5 | 15 min warm-up in Z2. Main block: 6 × 3 min at 110–115% FTP with 3 min recovery in Z1. 10 min cool-down |
| Saturday | Long ride | 120 min | Z2 | Long outing at aerobic pace. Varied terrain is fine as long as intensity stays in Z2 |
| Sunday | Easy ride or rest | 45 min | Z1–Z2 | Regenerative spin. If accumulated fatigue is high, rest is better |

Total volume is around 6 hours and 40 minutes. Of that total, approximately 47 minutes correspond to Z5 work (the intervals themselves, not counting warm-ups or recoveries), representing about 12% of volume. The rest is distributed between Z1 and Z2. That proportion is conservative relative to the 20% Seiler proposes, but more realistic for an amateur integrating high-intensity intervals for the first time.

## Weekly plan: 10 to 12 available hours

Those with more time can increase both aerobic volume and interval load. With 10 to 12 weekly hours, the advanced amateur cyclist has room to include a third intensity session or extend long rides, moving closer to the original 80/20 distribution. The key remains the same: respect the polarization and do not drift into moderate pace by inertia.

| Day | Session | Duration | Zone | Description |
|-----|---------|----------|------|-------------|
| Monday | Rest | — | — | Full recovery |
| Tuesday | VO2max intervals | 75 min | Z5 | 20 min progressive warm-up in Z2. Main block: 5 × 5 min at 106–110% FTP with 5 min recovery in Z1. 15 min cool-down |
| Wednesday | Aerobic ride | 90 min | Z2 | Steady pace, 60–70% FTP. Good day to work on pedaling technique and position |
| Thursday | Short intervals | 70 min | Z5–Z6 | 20 min warm-up in Z2. Block: 8 × 2 min at 115–120% FTP with 2 min recovery in Z1. Then 3 × 30 sec all-out (Z6) with 4 min recovery. 15 min cool-down |
| Friday | Active recovery | 60 min | Z1 | Regenerative pedaling below 55% FTP |
| Saturday | Long ride | 180 min | Z2 | Long outing, varied terrain. Maintain Z2 discipline even if the group surges on climbs |
| Sunday | Aerobic ride with sprints | 105 min | Z2 + Z6 | 90 min in Z2 with 4 sprints of 20 sec all-out every 20 min. Full recovery between sprints |

Total volume sits around 10 hours and 40 minutes. Time in Z5 and Z6 adds up to approximately 65 minutes across Tuesday's, Thursday's intervals, and Sunday's sprints, representing about 10% of total volume. Adding the progressive warm-ups that briefly pass through Z3–Z4, actual intensity proportion approaches 15–18%. Aerobic volume in Z1–Z2 comfortably covers the remaining 80%.

## Metrics for tracking progression

Training by power allows precise quantification of improvement. Three indicators are especially useful for the amateur cyclist following a polarized plan.

The first is FTP itself. A 20-minute test every 6 to 8 weeks allows recalibration of zones and verification of whether threshold power has increased. Progress of 3 to 8 watts per month is expected in an amateur starting from a moderate level. If FTP stagnates for more than two consecutive months, it is worth reviewing the plan structure or rest quality.

The second indicator is average power during Z5 intervals. As adaptations progress, the cyclist should be able to complete the same sets at slightly higher watts or, at the same watts, perceive less effort and recover faster between repetitions. Keeping a log of average power and heart rate for each interval makes it easier to spot trends.

The third is aerobic decoupling during long Z2 rides. It is calculated by comparing the power-to-heart-rate ratio in the first half of the ride with the second half. Decoupling below 5% indicates good aerobic efficiency. If that percentage decreases over the weeks at equal power, the base is improving.

## Discipline as the limiting factor

The greatest challenge of the polarized model is not physiological but psychological. Pedaling in Z2 feels slow, especially when a group mate passes at tempo pace or when the app shows a modest average power. The temptation to pick up the pace is constant. But every minute spent in Z3 without planning it is a minute that provides neither the Z5 stimulus nor the Z2 recovery. Seiler said it in a phrase that has been repeated to exhaustion: you must go slow enough on easy days to be able to go hard enough on hard days.

Neal and his colleagues demonstrated that the difference between polarized and threshold training is not in total volume or the amount of suffering, but in where the effort is placed. The cyclists in their study trained similar total hours. The difference was that the polarized group dedicated exactly zero percent to the middle zone. That restriction — which seems minor on paper — was what produced superior adaptations in peak power, lactate threshold, and anaerobic capacity.

For the amateur logging between 6 and 12 weekly hours, polarized training offers a structure backed by solid scientific evidence. It is not the only valid model, but the evidence accumulated from Seiler to Rosenblat suggests that, when executed with rigor, it produces greater improvements than conventional training based on moderate intensity. The condition is respecting the distribution. If easy days become tempo days and intervals get cut short because legs arrive tired, the model loses its reason for being. Polarizing is not just a training plan: it is a discipline.
