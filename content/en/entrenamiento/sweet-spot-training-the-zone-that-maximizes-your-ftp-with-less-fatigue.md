## The Point Where Effort Yields the Most

Between 88% and 94% of FTP, there exists a range where the training stimulus is high, yet fatigue does not prevent training the next day. Andrew Coggan, the physiologist who developed the power zone model based on FTP, identified this intensity range with particular properties in the early 2000s. It was located between 88% and 94% of Functional Threshold Power, right at the intersection between the tempo zone (Z3, 76-90% FTP) and the threshold zone (Z4, 91-105% FTP). Coggan called it the "sweet spot" because there the balance between adaptive stimulus and generated fatigue reached its optimum point. The concept was later popularized by Frank Overton, founder of FasCat Coaching, who built comprehensive training programs around this intensity.

The idea is simple in theory but counterintuitive in practice. Riding at 88-94% of FTP produces a sustained effort that feels hard but manageable. It is not the agony of maintaining 100% FTP for 20 minutes, nor the comfortable pace of an endurance ride in Z2. It is an intermediate territory where the body receives enough stimulus to generate significant aerobic adaptations without accumulating the fatigue debt that limits total training volume in subsequent days.

## What Happens in the Muscle at 88-94%

At the cellular level, sweet spot work produces adaptations that partially overlap with those from threshold training and volume in zone 2. Mitochondrial density increases significantly because the muscle operates at a metabolic rate high enough to stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis, but without depleting glycogen reserves as rapidly as occurs above threshold. This allows for longer sessions than typical Z4 efforts, accumulating more total effective stimulus time.

Fat utilization as an energy substrate also benefits. At sweet spot intensities, the body operates near the crossover point where fat oxidation begins to yield dominance to carbohydrates. Repeated training in this zone improves the muscle’s capacity to sustain a relevant contribution from fats even at intensities that, without training, would depend almost exclusively on glycogen. FTP, measured as the power sustainable over one hour, tends to progressively increase when the cyclist accumulates consistent weeks of sweet spot work.

## SST Compared to Polarized and Threshold Training

The debate between training models has no single answer, but practical differences are clear. The polarized model, which concentrates 80% of volume in Z1 and 20% in Z3/Z5, has demonstrated superiority in controlled studies such as Stöggl and Sperlich (2014) in *Frontiers in Physiology*. However, that model requires high weekly volumes, generally above 12 hours, for the 80% time in Z1 to generate the necessary aerobic base adaptations. A cyclist with 5 to 10 hours weekly loses much of that advantage.

Pure threshold training, with frequent sessions at 95-105% FTP, produces rapid adaptations but generates cumulative fatigue that becomes difficult to absorb beyond three or four weeks. Recovery capacity becomes the limiting factor before improvement potential is exhausted. The sweet spot positions itself as a practical middle ground: according to accumulated experience in power meter training, it produces an adaptive stimulus close to threshold work but with considerably less fatigue load. For the cyclist working full-time and training during the week with 60 to 90-minute sessions, this proportion marks the difference between completing three quality sessions or arriving wrecked by Wednesday.

| Aspect | Sweet Spot (88-94% FTP) | Polarized (80/20) | Threshold (95-105% FTP) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ideal weekly volume | 5-10h | 12-20h | 6-12h |
| Fatigue per session | Moderate | Low (Z1) / High (Z5) | High |
| Expected FTP gain | Moderate and sustained | Gradual with high volume | High but ephemeral |
| Long-term sustainability | 6-8 weeks | Indefinite | 3-4 weeks |
| Risk of overtraining | Medium | Low | High |
| Ideal profile | Cyclists with limited time | Cyclists with high available volume | Short specialization blocks |

## Effective Protocols

Sweet spot intervals are structured with long blocks and short recoveries. Unlike VO2max intervals, where the explosive component demands generous pauses, sweet spot allows maintaining the stimulus with active rests of 3 to 5 minutes between efforts. The three most used protocols are 2x20 minutes, 3x15 minutes, and 1x45 minutes, all executed between 88% and 94% FTP.

The 2x20 is the classic format: two 20-minute blocks with 5 minutes of active recovery in Z1 between them. It is the most referenced protocol in power meter training literature and best allows monitoring progression week to week. The 3x15 offers an alternative for those who find 20-minute blocks mentally demanding at the start. The 1x45 is an advanced format requiring sustained concentration and precise hydration and nutrition management during the effort. Any variant should be preceded by a 10 to 15-minute warm-up progressing from Z1 to Z3.

## Sessions by Level

Sweet spot prescription should be adjusted to the cyclist’s experience and load absorption capacity. A beginner with a newly established FTP should not face the same volumes as a cyclist with three seasons of structured training. Progression should be gradual, increasing first the duration of intervals and then the number of weekly sessions.

| Level | Typical Session | Intervals | % FTP | Weekly Frequency | Estimated TSS per Session |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner (FTP < 200W) | Warm-up + 2x12min + cool down | 2x12min, rec. 5min | 88-90% | 2 | 55-65 |
| Intermediate (FTP 200-280W) | Warm-up + 2x20min + cool down | 2x20min, rec. 5min | 90-92% | 2-3 | 75-90 |
| Advanced (FTP > 280W) | Warm-up + 3x20min + cool down | 3x20min, rec. 5min | 92-94% | 3 | 95-110 |

## When to Use SST in the Season

Sweet spot is not a year-round tool. Its greatest utility appears in the Build phase, generally between weeks 8 and 16 of an annual periodization plan, when the aerobic base is already built and the goal is to raise FTP before entering specificity or competition blocks. Sylta et al. (2016) compared different intensity distributions in well-trained cyclists and found that blocks of sustained submaximal intensity work produced the greatest FTP gains when placed after a volume accumulation period in Z1-Z2.

During the early base phase, the first 6 to 8 weeks, sweet spot can be introduced in reduced doses, but emphasis should be on building aerobic volume. In the peak phase before competitions, sweet spot loses prominence to more specific efforts in Z5-Z6 that simulate race demands. The most common mistake among self-coached cyclists is maintaining SST blocks for months without interruption, turning a building tool into a monotonous routine that ceases to produce adaptations.

## Signs You’re Overdoing It

Sweet spot carries a particular risk: since fatigue per session is moderate, it is tempting to accumulate sessions without respecting recovery. Signs of overexposure to SST are subtle at first and evident only when damage is done. The first sign is an FTP plateau lasting more than four consecutive weeks despite adherence to the plan. If FTP stops rising, the stimulus is no longer sufficient or accumulated fatigue is limiting adaptation capacity.

Inability to complete prescribed intervals is another unmistakable sign. If a cyclist who regularly completed 2x20 at 90% starts failing the second block, accumulated fatigue is exceeding recovery capacity. The third indicator is chronic fatigue extending beyond training, with poor sleep quality, irritability, and loss of motivation. In any of these cases, the correct response is a recovery week with volume reduced to 50-60% and exclusive work in Z1-Z2.

## SST Within a Polarized Plan

Sweet spot does not need to compete with the polarized model. In fact, the best results are obtained when SST is integrated as a tool within a polarized framework. In practice, this means maintaining the general structure of 80% volume in Z1 and 20% at high intensities, but replacing part of that 20% with sweet spot work instead of purely supra-threshold intervals.

A concrete example: in a 10-hour week, a polarized cyclist would do 8 hours in Z1 and 2 hours in Z5. A variant with SST could allocate 8 hours in Z1, 1 hour in sweet spot (88-94% FTP), and 1 hour in Z5. The sweet spot session complements high-intensity intervals without generating the fatigue of a second VO2max session. This combination is especially useful in weeks with three quality sessions, where one SST session can be placed between two short high-intensity interval sessions without compromising recovery.

The key is not to let sweet spot displace genuinely polarized work. SST is a complement, not a substitute. When a cyclist starts replacing all their Z5 sessions with SST sessions because "they feel more manageable," they are falling exactly into the gray zone trap that the polarized model seeks to avoid. Sweet spot is a powerful tool, but it only works when used judiciously, at the right time in the season, and in the appropriate dose.
