## The Norwegian Laboratory That Changed the Debate

For decades, the relationship between cycling and gym training was a matter of opinions rather than data. Cyclists avoided weights for fear of gaining muscle mass that would slow them down on climbs. Strength coaches insisted that more muscle meant more watts. Neither was entirely correct. It was Bent Rønnestad’s research group at the University of Lillehammer, Norway, that began producing controlled evidence on what type of strength training improves performance in already trained cyclists, and what is useless.

According to Rønnestad, Hansen, and Raastad (2010) in *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, a 12-week program of heavy squats combined with regular cycling training produced significant improvements in lactate threshold power, peak aerobic power, and average power in a 40-minute test. The group that only cycled showed no improvements in any of these variables over the same period. The difference was not marginal: the strength group increased their average power in the time trial test while the control group plateaued.

## Pedaling Economy: Doing the Same with Less Oxygen

The main mechanism by which heavy strength training improves cycling performance is not muscle mass increase. It is pedaling economy—the amount of oxygen required to maintain a given power output. A more economical cyclist expends less energy at the same wattage, allowing them to sustain efforts longer before fatigue sets in. According to Rønnestad and Mujika (2014) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, the strongest evidence for the additive effect of strength training on cycling performance is precisely in this variable.

The physiological explanation has two components. First, heavy strength training induces a fiber-type transition from type IIX to type IIA fibers, which are more fatigue-resistant and have greater oxidative capacity. Second, the increase in maximal strength means each pedal stroke represents a smaller percentage of the muscle’s maximum capacity, reducing motor unit recruitment and the associated metabolic cost. The net result is a cyclist who pedals with less relative effort at any submaximal intensity.

## The Three Exercises Supported by Evidence

Rønnestad and colleagues consistently use a core set of multi-joint exercises with heavy loads. The barbell squat is the cornerstone of all published protocols, performed either full depth or half squat in ranges of 4 to 10 repetitions. Deadlifts and leg presses appear as complements in several programs. Isolation exercises—quadriceps extensions, hamstring curls—are absent from most protocols that have demonstrated benefits for cycling performance.

The reason is biomechanical. Squats and deadlifts replicate the hip and knee extension pattern that dominates the pedaling cycle. They engage quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings, and core in an integrated manner, which transfers better to the sporting gesture than segmented movements. According to Rønnestad, Hansen, Hollan, and Ellefsen (2015) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, elite cyclists who followed a 25-week program based on squats and leg presses improved peak power in Wingate tests, peak aerobic power, lactate threshold power, and average power in a 40-minute test.

## How Much Weight, How Many Repetitions

The effective load range for cyclists lies between 70% and 90% of one-repetition maximum (1RM). Rønnestad’s protocols use a progression starting with sets of 10 reps at 70% 1RM and advancing toward sets of 4 reps at 85-90% 1RM over several weeks. The initial adaptation phase, lasting 3 to 4 weeks, allows the neuromuscular system to adjust to the demands of strength training without generating fatigue that compromises cycling sessions.

Volume is deliberately low. Two weekly sessions of 30 to 45 minutes, with 3 to 4 exercises and 3 sets per exercise, are sufficient to produce the adaptations documented in the studies. More volume does not yield better results and increases the risk of interference with endurance training. The principle is clear: the gym complements the bike, it does not compete with it.

## You Don’t Gain Weight, You Gain Watts

One of the most consistent findings in the literature is that heavy strength training in cyclists does not produce significant body weight increases. Thigh cross-sectional area may increase between 3% and 5%, but this is offset by the absence of fat mass gain and the fact that the stimulus is insufficient to generate the hypertrophy typical of bodybuilding programs. According to Vikmoen, Ellefsen, Trøen, Hollan, Hanestadhaugen, Raastad, and Rønnestad (2016) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, female cyclists who added 11 weeks of heavy strength training to their usual routine improved average power in a 40-minute test, VO2max utilization fraction, and pedaling economy without significant changes in body weight.

The fear of “getting bulky” is, in the context of concurrent training with high cycling volume, unfounded. The chronic aerobic stimulus of cycling acts as a natural brake on excessive muscle hypertrophy, a phenomenon known as the interference effect. What does change is the composition of existing muscle mass: more type IIA fibers, higher myofilament density, greater force production per unit area. More efficiency, not more volume.

## The Final Sprint: When Strength Makes the Real Difference

One of the most direct applications of strength training in cycling is the ability to produce power at the end of stages or long races, when accumulated fatigue has degraded muscle function. According to Rønnestad, Hansen, and Raastad (2011) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, cyclists who completed 12 weeks of concurrent strength training significantly improved their average power in a 5-minute test performed immediately after 185 minutes of submaximal pedaling. The control group, which only trained on the bike, showed no improvements in that same test.

The practical implication is straightforward. In a 4 to 5-hour road race, the ability to attack or respond to attacks in the final kilometers depends on neuromuscular reserves that aerobic training alone does not optimally develop. The cyclist who has trained strength arrives at the final stretch with muscle fibers more resistant to fatigue and with greater capacity to produce maximal force even in a glycogen-depleted state. This difference does not appear in a fresh FTP test on the trainer but manifests when the body has been pedaling for hours.

## When to Incorporate the Gym in the Season

The periodization of strength training within a cycling season follows a pattern consistently validated by research. The maximal strength building phase is concentrated in the preseason, during months when cycling volume is low or moderate and there are no competitions. This allows dedicating the necessary energy to the gym without compromising quality cycling sessions.

Once the competitive season begins, strength training shifts to maintenance: one session per week with loads around 80% 1RM and reduced volume. According to Rønnestad and Mujika (2014), maintaining at least one strength session per week during the competition season is sufficient to preserve the adaptations gained in preseason. Completely stopping strength work for more than 4 to 6 weeks partially reverses gains in maximal strength and pedaling economy.

The most common mistake is abandoning the gym as soon as races start. The second most common mistake is trying to do maximal strength phases during the competitive season, when cycling training volume and race fatigue leave no margin for recovery. Strength is built in winter and maintained in summer. That sequence, repeated season after season, produces cumulative gains not obtained with isolated gym blocks.

## The Minimum Effective Protocol for Time-Constrained Cyclists

For those with limited time, the evidence allows reducing the program to essentials without losing efficacy. Two weekly 30-minute sessions, each focused on heavy squats (4 sets of 4 to 6 reps) plus one complementary exercise—Romanian deadlift or barbell step-up—are sufficient to produce the adaptations documented in Rønnestad’s studies. Isolation machines, metabolic circuits, or hour-long sessions with fifteen different exercises are unnecessary.

The key is load, not variety. A cyclist who progresses gradually in the squat, from sets of 10 at 70% to sets of 4 at 85-90% 1RM, will gain more benefit for cycling performance than one who constantly rotates trendy exercises without accumulating real strength. The squat alone, executed with correct technique and consistent progression, is probably the exercise with the greatest transfer to pedaling according to available evidence. Everything else is complementary.
