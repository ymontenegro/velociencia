## More Time at the Cardiovascular Limit in Less Total Time

A cyclist riding for 40 minutes at a constant intensity of 80% of their maximum heart rate spends zero seconds in the VO2max zone. That same cyclist, using structured intervals within those same 40 minutes, can accumulate between 10 and 20 minutes with their cardiovascular system working at maximum capacity. This difference explains why high-intensity interval training (HIIT) produces superior adaptations compared to continuous work in already trained cyclists.

The mechanism is straightforward. Intervals allow the effort to be broken into tolerable blocks, with intermediate pauses that prevent premature fatigue. The result is more total time at intensities that the body could not sustain continuously. This translates into a greater stimulus for mitochondria, higher cardiac output demand, and increased recruitment of type II muscle fibers.

## Time in VO2max Zone: The Variable That Matters

Exercise physiology research has converged on a central metric to evaluate the effectiveness of a HIIT protocol: the accumulated time at or near maximal oxygen consumption, abbreviated as T@VO2max. Pedaling hard is not enough. What generates adaptations is how many minutes the cardiopulmonary system operates at its maximum capacity during a session.

Billat and colleagues demonstrated that short-interval formats with active recovery are particularly efficient at maximizing T@VO2max. In 30/30 protocols, cyclists reach the VO2max range during the final repetitions and maintain it even during recovery phases because the cardiovascular system does not have time to fully slow down. This allows accumulating 8 to 12 minutes of T@VO2max in a single session, a volume difficult to achieve with other formats.

## The 4x4 Protocol: The Norwegian Standard

Jan Helgerud’s research group at the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) popularized the format of 4 repetitions of 4 minutes at 90-95% of maximum heart rate, with 3 minutes of active recovery between each interval. Their studies showed improvements of 5-8% in VO2max after 8 weeks of training, three times per week.

The key to the 4x4 is the duration of the effort. Four minutes is sufficient for oxygen consumption to progressively rise until reaching values close to maximum near the end of each repetition. In terms of power, the range lies between 85% and 95% of maximal aerobic power (MAP). The complete session, including warm-up and cool-down, takes between 35 and 45 minutes.

This protocol works especially well for cyclists aiming to improve their threshold power and maximal aerobic capacity. The perceived exertion is high but manageable, allowing adherence over weeks.

## 30/30 Protocol: Maximum Efficiency, Lower Perceived Suffering

The 30/30 format consists of alternating 30 seconds of effort at 100-110% of MAP with 30 seconds of active recovery at 50% of MAP. A typical set includes between 12 and 20 repetitions and can be repeated in 2 or 3 blocks with 4 minutes rest between sets.

What makes 30/30 particularly interesting is the phenomenon of slow VO2 kinetics. After the first 4 or 5 repetitions, oxygen consumption stabilizes near maximal values and remains there for the rest of the set, including recovery phases. According to Thevenet et al. (2007) in *Journal of Sports Sciences*, cyclists accumulated more T@VO2max with 30/30 than with long 3-minute intervals, and with significantly lower perceived exertion.

## What the Tabata Study Really Showed

In 1996, Izumi Tabata published a protocol of 20 seconds of maximal effort followed by 10 seconds of rest, repeated 8 times, for a total of 4 minutes of work. The study compared this format against 60 minutes of continuous pedaling at 70% VO2max, five days per week for six weeks. The Tabata group improved both VO2max (+7 ml/kg/min) and anaerobic capacity (+28%), while the endurance group only improved VO2max.

The detail often omitted is that subjects pedaled at 170% of VO2max during each 20-second interval. This corresponds to an absolutely maximal effort, not the kind of intensity most cyclists associate with "doing a Tabata" on the trainer. The original protocol requires an intensity few can sustain properly outside a laboratory. Its practical applications in road cycling are limited, although it remains useful as a tool to develop anaerobic capacity in very short sessions.

## Long vs Short Intervals: When to Use Each Format

According to Rønnestad et al. (2019) in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*, a direct comparison of long intervals (5 minutes) versus short intervals (30 seconds) in trained cyclists over 11 weeks yielded clear results. Both groups improved, but the short-interval group showed superior adaptations in peak power and 20-minute time trial performance.

That does not mean short intervals are always better. Long intervals of 4 to 5 minutes produce more pronounced peripheral adaptations, such as increased mitochondrial density and improvements in lactate threshold. Short intervals, on the other hand, are more effective at improving maximal aerobic power and the ability to repeat high-intensity efforts. Prescription depends on the goal and the season phase. During aerobic base building, long intervals make more sense. During sharpening or competition phases, short intervals contribute more.

## Weekly Frequency: Balancing Stimulus and Recovery

Evidence converges on a range of 2 to 3 HIIT sessions per week as optimal for trained cyclists. According to Laursen and Jenkins (2002) in *Sports Medicine*, more than three high-intensity sessions weekly do not produce additional improvements and, in many cases, lead to overtraining symptoms within 4 to 6 weeks.

The rest of the weekly volume should be completed with low-intensity work, below the aerobic threshold, following the polarized training model. A cyclist training 10 hours per week could distribute them into 2 HIIT sessions of 60 to 75 minutes and 3 to 4 conversational pace rides. This proportion, with approximately 80% of time at low intensity and 20% at high intensity, has shown the best results in longitudinal studies with amateur and sub-elite cyclists.

## How to Prescribe the Correct Intensity

There are two main ways to define HIIT interval intensity. The first is the percentage of maximal aerobic power (MAP), obtained through an incremental test to exhaustion. The second is the percentage of maximum heart rate (HRmax), more accessible but less precise due to the inherent delay in heart rate response.

For long intervals like the 4x4, HRmax works reasonably well because there is enough time for heart rate to reach the target range. For short intervals of 20 or 30 seconds, power is the only viable reference, as heart rate does not even stabilize during the effort. A cyclist without a power meter can use the rating of perceived exertion (RPE) scale as an alternative: HIIT intervals should be between 8 and 9 out of 10, a level where talking is impossible.

## Which Protocol to Choose According to Your Goal

The choice of format depends on the specific goal and season phase. A cyclist needing to improve VO2max will find the Norwegian 4x4 and 30/30 protocols the most evidence-backed tools. Those seeking to develop anaerobic capacity for attacks and sprints will benefit more from Tabata or 40/20 formats. And those preparing for a long time trial should prioritize 5 to 8-minute intervals that develop sustained power near threshold.

What science has made clear is that there is no universally superior protocol. The key is accumulating time at intensities that challenge the cardiovascular system, respecting recovery between sessions, and adjusting the format as competition demands change. Two well-executed HIIT sessions per week, with the rest of the volume at low intensity, remain the formula that most consistently produces improvements in cyclists of all levels.
