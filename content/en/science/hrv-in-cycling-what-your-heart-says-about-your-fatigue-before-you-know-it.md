## A Mirror of the Autonomic Nervous System

Each heartbeat does not arrive exactly when the previous one predicts. There is a variation of milliseconds between one beat and the next that reflects the constant tug-of-war between the two branches of the autonomic nervous system: the sympathetic, which accelerates the heart in response to effort or stress, and the parasympathetic, which slows it down via the vagus nerve. This variation, measured as heart rate variability or HRV, has become the most accessible tool to assess an athlete's recovery status without the need for blood draws or laboratory settings.

The vagus nerve acts as a tonic brake on the heart’s sinoatrial node. At rest, parasympathetic activity dominates, producing greater variability between beats. When the body is under physiological stress—accumulated training, insufficient sleep, infection—the sympathetic activity gains ground, the vagal brake partially withdraws, and the intervals between beats become more regular. A high resting HRV indicates that the parasympathetic system is active and the body has the capacity to respond to new stimuli. A sustained low HRV suggests that this capacity is compromised.

## The Metrics Defining Variability

There is no single way to quantify HRV. Among time-domain methods, RMSSD (root mean square of successive differences between R-R intervals) is the most widely used in sports contexts. It captures short-term variability mediated by parasympathetic activity and is relatively resistant to respiratory artifacts. SDNN (standard deviation of N-N intervals) measures overall variability, combining sympathetic and parasympathetic influences, but requires longer recordings to be reliable.

The logarithmic transformation of RMSSD, known as LnRMSSD, solves a statistical problem that directly affects interpretation. The raw RMSSD distribution is skewed and non-normal: high values generate disproportionate dispersion that complicates comparing trends over time. The natural logarithm compresses this distribution, bringing it closer to normality and allowing relative changes to have comparable magnitudes regardless of the athlete’s absolute level. A cyclist with a baseline RMSSD of 40 ms and another with 120 ms may have LnRMSSD values of 3.7 and 4.8 respectively, and in both cases a 0.5-unit decrease has a similar physiological interpretation.

## The Coefficient of Variation as a Training Compass

Plews, Laursen, Stanley, Kilding, and Buchheit (2013) proposed in *Sports Medicine* an approach that transformed how HRV is used in endurance training. Instead of obsessing over the daily LnRMSSD value, they suggested analyzing the weekly coefficient of variation (CV): the standard deviation of LnRMSSD divided by its mean, expressed as a percentage. A low CV indicates that parasympathetic modulation remains stable day to day, a sign of good adaptation to training load. A high CV—above 10% sustained—reveals excessive oscillations associated with overload and poor tolerance to accumulated load.

Data from that study, conducted with elite New Zealand rowers, showed that athletes with better competition performance maintained a high mean LnRMSSD and low CV during heavy training blocks. Those exhibiting a high CV and declining mean LnRMSSD were the same athletes who underperformed in tests or reported overtraining symptoms. The CV does not predict a bad race, but its sustained deterioration precedes performance decline by days or weeks.

## Buchheit and the Complete Map of Cardiac Modulation

Buchheit (2014), in a review published in *Frontiers in Physiology*, consolidated evidence on how endurance training modifies autonomic activity and how these modifications are reflected in HRV. He documented that chronic aerobic training increases resting vagal tone, resulting in lower heart rate and higher RMSSD. However, this response is neither linear nor uniform. In elite athletes with years of accumulated training, HRV may reach a ceiling effect where additional volume or intensity increments do not produce detectable RMSSD changes.

Buchheit’s review also addressed a counterintuitive phenomenon that confuses many cyclists. After an intense training session, HRV can transiently increase during the first 24–48 hours before declining. This paradoxical increase reflects reactive parasympathetic saturation, not full recovery. Interpreting this peak as a signal to train hard the next day is a common mistake that the weekly trend of LnRMSSD avoids by smoothing daily oscillations.

## How to Measure HRV Without Ruining the Data

The measurement protocol conditions data quality as much as the sensor used. The standard recommendation is to measure every morning upon waking, in a supine position, for a minimum of one minute and optimally five minutes. Consistency matters more than duration: Plews et al. (2014) demonstrated in *International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance* that measurements as short as one minute produce LnRMSSD values statistically equivalent to five-minute recordings, provided the same position and time are maintained.

Breathing during measurement is debated. Some protocols require controlled breathing at a fixed frequency (e.g., 12 breaths per minute) to eliminate respiratory sinus arrhythmia as a confounding variable. Others advocate free, spontaneous breathing, arguing that conscious intervention alters the autonomic state intended to be measured. In sports practice, free and consistent breathing between days produces sufficiently reliable data to track weekly trends. What invalidates measurement is doing it after getting up, after coffee, or in different positions each day.

## Chest Straps, Rings, and Watches: Not All Measure Equally

The validity of HRV data depends directly on the sensor. Chest straps detecting R-R intervals, such as the Polar H10 and Garmin HRM-Pro Plus, capture the cardiac electrical signal via electrodes and produce data comparable to clinical electrocardiograms. Schaffarczyk et al. (2022) validated the Polar H10 in *Sensors* and found excellent concordance with reference ECG recordings both at rest and during incremental exercise.

Wrist-worn watches with optical photoplethysmography (PPG) measure changes in capillary blood volume to estimate pulse intervals. Precision decreases compared to chest straps, especially during exercise, but in morning rest—the relevant condition for HRV monitoring—latest-generation devices from Apple, Garmin, and WHOOP produce acceptable data for tracking trends. Rings like the Oura Ring measure HRV from the digital artery during sleep, accumulating hours of data instead of minutes, offering a nocturnal average that reduces noise from point measurements. The trade-off is loss of standardization: a nocturnal measurement includes light, deep, and REM sleep phases, each with distinct autonomic profiles.

## Confounders Your App Won't Explain

Alcohol causes a pronounced drop in RMSSD that can last 48–72 hours depending on the amount consumed, even when the cyclist feels subjectively recovered. A single glass of wine with dinner can reduce morning LnRMSSD by 0.3–0.5 units, enough to confuse a weekly trend if consumed two or three nights. Fragmented sleep, regardless of total duration, reduces nocturnal parasympathetic reactivation and produces depressed morning values that do not reflect training load but rather rest quality.

Chronic psychological stress—work, personal, financial—elevates basal sympathetic tone comparably to a week of overtraining. A cyclist undergoing a period of high mental demand may show a high CV and depressed LnRMSSD without having changed training load. Mild dehydration reduces plasma volume and alters cardiac preload, directly affecting R-R interval variability. Even measurement position matters: transitioning from supine to seated reduces RMSSD by 30–50% due to gravitational blood redistribution and compensatory sympathetic activation.

None of these factors invalidate HRV as a tool. But they require interpreting data within a broader context than the app’s simple graph. A low LnRMSSD after a sleepless night does not require reducing training if the prior weekly trend was stable and positive. A consistently low LnRMSSD over a week, absent identifiable confounders, does demand reconsidering load.

## Weekly Trends and the Smallest Worthwhile Change

The individual morning value is noise. The signal emerges by observing five- to seven-day trends. The concept of the Smallest Worthwhile Change (SWC), originally proposed in the context of magnitude-based statistics, applies directly to HRV: only a change in LnRMSSD exceeding the individual’s typical variability has practical meaning. This threshold is usually calculated as 0.5 times the standard deviation of LnRMSSD during a baseline reference period.

In practice, this means a cyclist must first establish their baseline: two to four weeks of consistent morning measurements during a normal training period. From there, weekly average declines exceeding the individual SWC indicate recovery is not keeping pace with load. Sustained increases above the SWC during tapering confirm the expected supercompensation. The combination of weekly LnRMSSD average and its CV provides a more informative early warning system than any subjective fatigue sensation.

## HRV as a Tool, Not an Oracle

HRV monitoring does not replace the coach or perceived exertion. It is one data point in a panel that includes training power, heart rate during sessions, sleep, nutrition, and direct communication between cyclist and coach. Its greatest value lies in detecting discrepancies: when a cyclist reports feeling good but their weekly CV has been rising for three weeks, there is a physiological signal that subjective perception is missing. When CV remains low and LnRMSSD stable or rising during a demanding block, confidence to maintain or increase load has objective support.

Plews and his group demonstrated that guiding training with HRV—prescribing hard days when LnRMSSD is above the individual mean and easy days when below—produced adaptations equal or superior to following a rigid predefined plan, with lower perceived fatigue. The heart offers a window into the organism’s internal state that no power file can open. Using it requires measurement consistency, patience to accumulate data, and discipline not to react to a single red point on the phone screen.
