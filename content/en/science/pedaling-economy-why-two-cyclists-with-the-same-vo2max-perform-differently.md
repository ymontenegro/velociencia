## The Hidden Variable of Performance

VO2max has dominated conversations about aerobic capacity for decades. It measures the ceiling of the oxygen transport system and is expressed in milliliters per kilogram per minute. A professional cyclist can reach 75–85 ml/kg/min, a well-trained amateur 55–65, and a recreational rider 40–50. But within each range, performance differences are enormous. Two cyclists with 65 ml/kg/min can differ by 30 or more watts at threshold, and the explanation is not in the heart or lungs. It lies in how much of that energy the body produces actually reaches the pedals.

That proportion is called mechanical efficiency, and in cycling it is commonly quantified as gross efficiency (GE): the ratio between the external mechanical work produced and the total energy expenditure, expressed as a percentage. The formula is straightforward: $GE = W_{ext} / E_{tot} \times 100$. A cyclist producing 250 watts of mechanical power and expending metabolic energy equivalent to 1250 watts has a GE of 20%. This means that the remaining 80% dissipates as heat. Typical values range from 18% in recreational cyclists to 25% in elite professionals, a seemingly narrow range with enormous consequences for performance.

## What Two Percentage Points of Efficiency Mean

The difference between 20% and 22% GE equates, for identical energy expenditure, to producing 10% more mechanical power. Concretely: if a cyclist with 20% GE produces 250 watts at a given oxygen consumption, another with 22% GE and the same oxygen consumption produces 275 watts. Those 25 watts are the difference between staying on the peloton’s wheel and dropping off on the first climb, or between completing a 40 km time trial in 58 or 61 minutes. Efficiency acts as a silent multiplier of the entire physiological machinery.

Hopker, Coleman, and Passfield (2009) documented in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* that GE improves with long-term endurance training. They evaluated cyclists over periods of up to four years and observed significant efficiency increases not explained by changes in VO2max. Cyclists who accumulated more training volume in zone 2 showed the greatest GE improvements, suggesting that efficiency is a peripheral—muscular and biomechanical—adaptation rather than a central one.

## Cadence and Its Nonlinear Relationship with Efficiency

Foss and Hallén (2005) published in the *European Journal of Applied Physiology* a study quantifying the relationship between cadence and efficiency in well-trained cyclists. GE was maximal between 60 and 80 revolutions per minute and progressively declined at higher cadences. At 100 rpm, efficiency dropped approximately 3–4% relative to the optimum, equating to wasting several watts moving the legs faster without generating more useful power. At low cadences, below 60 rpm, efficiency also declined due to increased muscular stress per revolution and greater co-contraction of antagonist muscles.

The paradox is that most professional cyclists pedal between 85 and 95 rpm, above the range of maximum efficiency measured in the lab. This preference has explanations that GE alone does not capture. Higher cadences reduce force per pedal stroke and stress on type II muscle fibers, delaying peripheral fatigue in prolonged efforts. A cyclist may be slightly less efficient at 90 rpm than at 70 rpm in a five-minute test, but sustaining that intensity for three hours at 70 rpm is unsustainable due to accumulating muscle damage. Optimal cadence depends on context: effort duration, relative intensity, terrain profile, and individual muscle composition.

## Muscle Fibers: The Genetic Lottery of Efficiency

The proportion of type I (slow, oxidative) and type II (fast, glycolytic) fibers in the vastus lateralis and other primary pedaling muscles directly influences efficiency. Type I fibers generate force with lower energy cost per unit of work thanks to their higher mitochondrial efficiency and slower actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling velocity. A cyclist with 70–80% type I fibers in the quadriceps—a typical profile of time trial specialists and Grand Tour climbers—starts with a physiological efficiency advantage that no training protocol can fully replicate in someone with the opposite muscle composition.

Ettema and Lorås (2009) reviewed in *European Journal of Applied Physiology* the determinants of mechanical efficiency in cycling. They concluded that muscle fiber composition explains a significant fraction of interindividual variability in GE, but other factors—such as pedaling technique, force distribution throughout the pedal stroke, muscle co-activation, and tendon elasticity—also contribute in ways difficult to isolate experimentally. Efficiency is not a single trait but the sum of multiple processes ranging from mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation to ankle joint biomechanics.

## Bicycle Position as an Efficiency Variable

A suboptimal bike fit can cost between 1% and 3% of GE, depending on the magnitude of the mismatch. A saddle set too high forces excessive knee extension at bottom dead center, reduces force generation capacity during the push phase, and increases pelvic rocking. A saddle set too low increases knee flexion, overloads the extensor apparatus, and forces the quadriceps to work in unfavorable joint ranges. Handlebar reach, drop, and crank length interact with each cyclist’s anatomical proportions in ways only individualized adjustment can optimize.

The aggressive aerodynamic position of time trialists sacrifices mechanical efficiency in exchange for reducing air resistance. Extreme hip flexion restricts excursion of the psoas and hamstring muscles, reduces the effective force range on the pedal, and increases respiratory cost by compressing the diaphragm. Studies with professional cyclists show that GE can drop between 1% and 2% when switching from a road position to triathlon bars. This cost is more than offset on flat terrain by aerodynamic savings, but on climbs where speed decreases and aerodynamics lose relevance, a more upright position allows producing more watts per calorie consumed.

## Body Temperature as a Hidden Modulator

Mechanical efficiency decreases as core body temperature rises. Each degree Celsius above 37.5°C increases energy expenditure for a given power output, partly due to increased ventilation and cardiac work to dissipate heat, and partly due to direct alterations in muscle contraction efficiency. A cyclist producing 250 watts at 20°C ambient temperature with 22% GE may see that efficiency drop to 20.5% or less when ambient temperature exceeds 35°C and core temperature approaches 39°C.

This efficiency drop explains why performance in hot Tour de France stages degrades disproportionately relative to what heart rate and power predict. The body expends more energy to produce the same power, and that extra energy converts into additional heat, worsening the problem in a positive feedback loop. Pre-cooling strategies and ingestion of cold fluids during effort not only delay heat stroke but improve mechanical efficiency by maintaining a lower core temperature longer.

## Strength Training: The Indirect Path to More Watts per Calorie

Sunde, Støren, Bjerkaas, Larsen, Hoff, and Helgerud (2010) demonstrated in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* that eight weeks of maximal strength training—heavy squats with low repetitions—improved pedaling economy in well-trained cyclists without increasing body mass. Cyclists in the strength group increased their time to exhaustion at maximal aerobic power by 17.2% compared to controls. The economy improvement was attributed to greater recruitment of high-threshold motor units and higher force production per unit of neural activation.

The proposed mechanism is that maximal strength allows the cyclist to produce the same submaximal power with a lower percentage of total strength capacity, reducing the recruitment rate of type II fibers and associated energy cost. It is like running a six-cylinder engine at low load instead of forcing a four-cylinder engine: power is the same, but relative system effort is lower. Subsequent studies have replicated this finding in runners, rowers, and cross-country skiers, consolidating the idea that maximal strength improves movement economy in endurance sports.

## The Gap Between Professionals and Amateurs

World Tour professional cyclists consistently show GE values between 22% and 25%, while motivated recreational cyclists range between 18% and 20%. This four to five percentage point difference reflects years of accumulated adaptations: a higher functional proportion of type I fibers induced by training, refined biomechanics from thousands of hours of pedaling, optimized position from multiple bike fits, and pedaling technique that minimizes muscle co-contraction and maximizes tangential force application on the crank axis.

Hopker et al. (2009) observed that GE continued improving even in cyclists with more than five years of structured training, suggesting efficiency is a slow-developing adaptation that does not easily reach a ceiling. This has direct practical implications: a cyclist with a modest VO2max of 60 ml/kg/min but 24% GE can produce the same threshold power as one with 68 ml/kg/min and 21% GE. VO2max sets the ceiling, but efficiency determines how much real power is extracted from that ceiling. Ignoring efficiency is like calculating a car’s performance by measuring only the fuel tank size without considering engine consumption.

## Preferred Cadence vs. Efficient Cadence

The cadence a cyclist naturally chooses—their preferred cadence—does not match the cadence of maximal efficiency measured in the lab. Preferred cadence tends to be 10–20 rpm higher than the optimum according to GE. This discrepancy does not imply cyclists are wrong. Preferred cadence minimizes perceived effort and neuromuscular fatigue in prolonged efforts, which may be more relevant for real-world performance than instantaneous efficiency measured in a ten-minute test.

The practical recommendation moves away from prescribing a fixed cadence. Cyclists should understand that low cadences (60–75 rpm) are more metabolically efficient but more muscularly stressful, while high cadences (90–100 rpm) save muscular stress at the cost of greater energetic and cardiorespiratory expenditure. On long, steep climbs where speed is low and maximal muscle recruitment occurs, cadence naturally tends to drop, coinciding with efficiency. On flat terrain at high speed, higher cadence protects muscle fibers for efforts that may last hours. Efficiency is not optimized by choosing a number; it is optimized by understanding the demands of each situation.

## Beyond Watts per Liter of Oxygen

Pedaling economy is the variable that connects laboratory physiology with power on the road. A cyclist can improve efficiency through three pathways: accumulating aerobic volume over years to induce peripheral muscular adaptations, incorporating maximal strength training to improve neural recruitment, and optimizing bicycle position to minimize biomechanical losses. None of these interventions produce immediate results, and all require the patience of those who understand efficiency is a long-term adaptation. VO2max can improve 10–15% in a season with proper training. GE improves 1–2% in the same period. But that 1–2% can translate into the watts that separate a podium from a mediocre result.
