## The Most Cited Number in Cycling with a Power Meter

Functional Threshold Power (FTP) became the dominant metric in power-based cycling since Hunter Allen and Andrew Coggan popularized it in the mid-2000s. Its original definition was straightforward: the maximum power a cyclist can sustain for one hour. In practice, almost no one performs a maximal 60-minute effort to determine it. The standard protocol consists of a maximal 20-minute effort, whose average power is multiplied by 0.95 to estimate the hourly power. This simplicity made it accessible to any cyclist with a power meter and 20 minutes of willpower.

FTP is used to define training zones, calculate the Training Stress Score (TSS), compare performance between cyclists, and evaluate progression throughout a season. Platforms like Zwift, TrainerRoad, and Training Peaks build their training algorithms around this number. However, research over the last decade has questioned both the physiological validity of the concept and the accuracy of the 20-minute protocol to estimate it.

## The Problem with 0.95

Allen and Coggan established the 95% correction factor based on observations of trained cyclists who performed approximately 5% better in 20-minute efforts than in 60-minute efforts. Sitko, Cirer-Sastre, and López-Laval (2023), in a study published in the *International Journal of Sports Medicine*, analyzed this factor in cyclists of different levels and found that the optimal multiplier varies significantly. Professionals require a factor of 0.96, well-trained cyclists 0.95, trained cyclists 0.92, and recreational cyclists barely 0.88.

This variation has direct practical consequences. A recreational cyclist producing an average of 200 watts in their 20-minute test and applying the standard factor obtains an FTP of 190 watts. The real factor for their level suggests an FTP of 176 watts. The 14-watt difference means all their training zones would be miscalibrated, with zone 2 set too high, the threshold zone unreachable for prescribed times, and endurance sessions turned into moderate-intensity work without the cyclist realizing it.

## FTP Is Not Lactate Threshold

Borszcz, Tramontin, and Costa (2019) investigated whether FTP was interchangeable with Maximal Lactate Steady State (MLSS) in trained cyclists and published their findings in the *International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance*. MLSS is the highest intensity at which blood lactate remains stable during prolonged effort and is considered the gold standard for delineating the severe intensity domain. The results showed that although there was a strong correlation between the two metrics (r = 0.89), the limits of agreement were too wide to use them interchangeably. In some cyclists, FTP overestimated MLSS by more than 20 watts, while in others it underestimated it by a similar magnitude.

Wong, Burnley, Mauger, and Hopker (2022) were more categorical in their study published in the *Journal of Sports Sciences*. They concluded that FTP is not a valid marker of MLSS. The cyclists evaluated could not sustain their estimated FTP for 60 minutes under controlled laboratory conditions, and the blood lactate response to FTP varied widely between individuals. The study’s title leaves no ambiguity: "Functional threshold power is not a valid marker of the maximal metabolic steady state."

## What the Body Tells You When You Pedal at FTP

Borszcz et al. (2018), in the *International Journal of Sports Medicine*, evaluated physiological responses to pedaling at FTP intensity for 30 minutes in trained cyclists. They observed that heart rate and perceived exertion progressively increased—a phenomenon known as "cardiovascular drift"—and that blood lactate concentration did not stabilize in most participants. This indicates that FTP lies above MLSS in many cyclists, meaning that pedaling "at FTP" for an hour is not physiologically sustainable for them.

The concept of FTP assumes a clear-cut transition point between sustainable and unsustainable exercise. Physiology is less orderly. The transition between intensity domains is gradual and depends on factors such as prior effort duration, muscle glycogen status, ambient temperature, and hydration. A cyclist may sustain 10 watts above their MLSS for 35 minutes but not for 50, depending on the day’s conditions.

## The 20-Minute Test and Its Hidden Pitfalls

Mackey and Horner (2021) conducted a scoping review on the FTP20 test published in the *Journal of Sports Sciences*. They identified several recurring methodological problems. The first is dependence on anaerobic capacity: cyclists with a high anaerobic contribution perform disproportionately well in 20 minutes but cannot sustain that proportion for an hour. The 0.95 factor does not adequately correct for this variability. The second problem is lack of warm-up protocol standardization: Allen and Coggan include a prior 5-minute maximal effort to "empty" anaerobic contribution, but many cyclists skip this step and obtain artificially inflated FTP values.

The third problem is psychological. A maximal 20-minute effort requires pacing management that improves with experience. A cyclist doing their first test tends to start too hard and collapse in the last five minutes or hold back excessively and finish with unused power. FTP values obtained by the same cyclist can vary 5-8% between tests weeks apart, not due to physiological changes but differences in effort strategy.

## Critical Power: The Better-Supported Alternative

Critical Power (CP) presents itself as a metric with a more solid theoretical basis. It uses multiple maximal efforts of varying durations to mathematically model the boundary between sustainable and unsustainable exercise. Karsten et al. (2021) compared both metrics in cyclists and found that CP (256 ± 50 W) was systematically higher than FTP (249 ± 44 W), with a 91.7% probability that the difference was significant. Although the correlation was high (r = 0.969), the values were not interchangeable in terms of training prescription.

The advantage of CP over FTP lies in that it does not depend on a single protocol or fixed correction factor. Derived from the power-duration relationship, CP adjusts to the individual cyclist’s physiological profile. Additionally, the model provides W' (W prime), which quantifies the work capacity above CP. A cyclist with a CP of 280 watts and a W' of 20 kJ has a very different profile than one with the same CP but a W' of 30 kJ: the latter can tolerate efforts above threshold for longer before fatiguing.

## When FTP Is Still Useful

None of the above means FTP is useless. For a cyclist training with a power meter who needs a reference number to structure sessions, FTP has practical value. Its protocol is simple, reproducible, and does not require laboratory equipment. The problem arises when a field estimate is confused with physiological truth.

FTP works best when understood as an operational approximation rather than a precise biological threshold. If a cyclist knows the test’s limitations and adjusts zones according to experience and training responses, FTP fulfills its function. If treated as a sacred number defining real capacity without margin for error, training decisions will be based on a flawed premise.

## FTP as a Starting Point, Not a Destination

Accumulated research over the last decade points in a clear direction. FTP is a convenient field tool but physiologically imprecise. It does not equal MLSS, lactate threshold, or critical power, although it correlates with all of them. The 95% correction factor works reasonably well for well-trained cyclists but fails at the extremes of the performance spectrum.

For those seeking greater precision, critical power offers a more robust framework with better scientific backing. For those seeking simplicity, FTP remains functional as long as its limitations are acknowledged. The best use of FTP is as a starting point to adjust training zones, complemented by perceived exertion, heart rate response, and, when possible, capillary lactate data. One number cannot capture the complexity of exercise physiology.
