## The Wall That Isn’t in the Legs

Every cyclist has reached that point on a long climb where the legs still respond, but something signals to reduce the pace. Cadence drops, power plummets, and the mind starts negotiating how far it is to the summit. The sensation is unmistakable: you can no longer sustain the effort. Yet if someone asked you to sprint for 10 seconds at that moment, you would discover your muscles still have the capacity to generate force. The question is straightforward: if the legs can give more, why do you stop?

The answer involves two fatigue systems operating in parallel and regulating each other. Peripheral fatigue occurs in the muscle: accumulation of metabolites, depletion of energy substrates, failure in excitation-contraction coupling of muscle fibers. Central fatigue occurs in the nervous system: progressive reduction of the motor signal the brain sends to the muscles. During endurance exercise, both progress simultaneously, but the brain acts as a regulator limiting muscle recruitment before the periphery reaches irreversible damage.

## The Critical Threshold of Peripheral Fatigue

Amann (2011) proposed a model in which the central nervous system constantly monitors the metabolic state of active muscles through group III and IV muscle afferents. These thin nerve fibers detect changes in acidity, extracellular potassium concentration, inorganic phosphate accumulation, and other metabolites associated with intense muscle contraction. When the level of metabolic disturbance approaches an individual critical threshold, the brain reduces voluntary activation to prevent the muscle from crossing a potential damage point.

This mechanism explains a recurring observation in exercise physiology labs. At the end of a maximal cycling effort, peripheral fatigue measured by electrical stimulation of the quadriceps converges toward a similar value regardless of effort duration or environmental conditions. Whether a test at sea level or in simulated hypoxia, with fresh or prefatigued legs, the level of peripheral fatigue at exhaustion tends to stabilize within a narrow range. The brain adjusts effort intensity so the periphery does not exceed that ceiling.

## What Changes According to Effort Duration

Thomas et al. (2015) measured central and peripheral fatigue in male cyclists immediately after 4, 20, and 40-kilometer time trials, publishing their results in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*. The findings revealed a clear pattern. After the 4 km time trial, a high-intensity short-duration effort, peripheral fatigue was dominant: quadriceps force evoked by electrical stimulation dropped by 35%, while maximal voluntary activation (an indicator of central fatigue) decreased only 8%.

After the 40 km time trial, the pattern reversed. Peripheral fatigue was relatively lower, but maximal voluntary activation fell between 15% and 25%. The brain had progressively restricted the motor signal during the prolonged effort, limiting the power the cyclist could produce well before the muscles reached their maximal peripheral fatigue capacity. In the 20 km time trial, the contribution of both fatigue types was more balanced.

## Signals Traveling from Muscle to Brain

Group III muscle afferents respond to mechanical stimuli: deformation of muscle tissue during contraction. Group IV afferents are metabosensitive: they detect chemical changes in the muscle’s extracellular environment. Together, they project information to the brainstem and cerebral cortex that modifies perceived effort and descending motor signals. The result is that exercise feels progressively harder as peripheral fatigue accumulates, even if power output remains constant.

Decorte et al. (2012) documented the kinetics of both fatigue types during constant-load cycling to exhaustion in a study published in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports*. Peripheral fatigue developed progressively and linearly. Central fatigue, however, showed acceleration in the last third of the effort. This temporal dissociation suggests the brain tolerates a moderate level of afferent signaling during most of the exercise, but as the critical peripheral disturbance threshold approaches, central activation reduction becomes abrupt. This is when the cyclist feels they "can’t go on," although an external stimulus like a nearby finish line or an attacking rival can temporarily reverse this restriction.

## The Sensory Tolerance Limit

Hureau, Romer, and Amann (2018) proposed the concept of the "sensory tolerance limit" in the *European Journal of Sport Science*. According to this model, voluntary exercise termination does not occur because the muscle mechanically fails nor because the brain imposes a rigid limit. It happens when the sum of all afferent signals—from locomotor muscles, respiratory muscles, cardiovascular system, and other organs—plus anticipatory signals processed in the central nervous system, reaches a threshold perceived by the individual as intolerable.

This threshold is not fixed. Motivation, competitive experience, ambient temperature, nutritional status, and even music can shift it. A professional cyclist with thousands of hours of competition has learned to tolerate levels of afferent signaling that a recreational rider would interpret as an emergency. It’s not that the professional feels less pain, but their brain has recalibrated the relationship between afferent signal and decision to reduce intensity. This recalibration is part of training, although rarely described as such.

## Serotonin, Dopamine, and the Chemistry of Effort

Meeusen et al. (2006) reviewed the serotonin hypothesis in central fatigue in a *Sports Medicine* publication examining over 200 studies. The original hypothesis proposed that increased brain serotonin during prolonged exercise caused lethargy, drowsiness, and loss of motivation, contributing to fatigue. Accumulated evidence showed the relationship is more complex: what modulates central fatigue is not serotonin alone, but the ratio between serotonin and dopamine in specific brain regions.

An increase in the serotonin/dopamine ratio is associated with sensations of tiredness and reduced motor drive. A decrease in that ratio, caused for example by increased dopamine, is associated with greater effort tolerance. Environmental heat exacerbates the serotonergic effect, partly explaining why endurance performance disproportionately declines in heat compared to predictions from purely cardiovascular or muscular models. The brain reduces muscle recruitment as a protective mechanism against hyperthermia, mediated in part by these neurotransmitters.

## The Central Governor and Its Critics

Noakes (2005) proposed an integrative model: the "central governor" in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine*. According to this hypothesis, the brain continuously regulates the number of motor units recruited during exercise, using anticipatory control and afferent feedback to ensure the organism never reaches catastrophic homeostasis failure. Fatigue, according to Noakes, is not a peripheral event but an emotion generated by the brain to regulate exercise behavior, a concept he expanded in a later publication in *Frontiers in Physiology* (2012).

The model sparked intense debate in the scientific community. Critics argue that fatigue is task-dependent: mechanisms vary according to intensity, duration, muscle group, and environmental conditions. There is no single "governor" but multiple interacting regulatory systems. Current evidence suggests an intermediate position: the brain indeed modulates performance anticipatorily and reactively, but peripheral limits are real and cannot be indefinitely overcome by will or motivation. Fatigue is both central and peripheral, and the proportion between them changes according to the effort context.

## What This Means for Training

The central-peripheral duality has practical implications for cyclists structuring their training. Short, high-intensity efforts, such as intervals of 30 seconds to 4 minutes, predominantly generate peripheral fatigue. They train the muscle to tolerate and clear metabolites, work under low pH, and maintain contraction force under metabolic stress. Prolonged efforts at moderate-high intensity, such as intervals of 20 to 60 minutes or long rides in threshold zones, challenge the central component more. They train the brain to maintain motor drive when muscle afferents pressure to reduce it.

Practicing completing long efforts despite accumulated fatigue not only improves muscular endurance but recalibrates the sensory tolerance limit. Each time a cyclist sustains power when their brain asks to ease off, they slightly shift that threshold. It’s not about ignoring body signals or seeking overtraining but progressively expanding the window of tolerable effort. Competition, with its motivational load and social pressure, allows access to muscle activation levels that solo training rarely reaches. This explains why many cyclists produce their best times in races, not on the home trainer.

## Fatigue as a Protective System, Not an Enemy

The contemporary view of fatigue understands it as an adaptive mechanism, not a weakness. The central nervous system does not limit performance capriciously but to protect the organism from damage that could compromise its integrity. Group III and IV muscle afferents are not a hindrance but a safety system calibrated by millions of years of evolution. The cyclist who understands this framework can work with their biology rather than against it, respecting signals when they indicate real risk and learning to tolerate discomfort when it is simply the price of performance.

The line between "the brain protects me" and "the brain limits me" is fine and changes according to context. In a Tour de France stage with millions of euros at stake, professional cyclists operate closer to their true peripheral limits than a recreational rider on a Sunday ride. But even the most aggressive professional finishes the effort with functional reserve: a final sprint after five hours of racing shows that the muscles never reached absolute failure. The brain always saves something. Training is, in part, learning to negotiate how much.
