## The Ceiling and the Usable Fraction

Two cyclists with a VO2max of 70 ml/kg/min can perform radically differently in a 40-kilometer time trial. The reason is the lactate threshold. VO2max defines the maximum limit of oxygen the body can consume, but the lactate threshold determines what percentage of that limit can be sustained during prolonged effort.

Lucia et al. (2001) analyzed the physiological determinants of professional Tour de France cyclists and found that the lactate threshold better explained performance differences among riders of similar level than VO2max alone. Among cyclists with comparable VO2max values, those who sustained a higher percentage of their maximum at the threshold achieved better results in time trials and mountain stages.

## What the Lactate Threshold Really Is

The term "lactate threshold" encompasses at least three distinct physiological concepts. The first is LT1 or aerobic threshold, the point where blood lactate begins to rise above resting levels, generally around 2 mmol/L. The second is LT2 or anaerobic threshold, the intensity at which lactate accumulation accelerates exponentially, typically near 4 mmol/L. The third is MLSS (Maximal Lactate Steady State), the highest intensity that can be maintained without continuous lactate accumulation in the blood.

Faude et al. (2009) reviewed the literature and concluded that MLSS is the most robust concept for predicting endurance performance. Unlike fixed thresholds of 2 or 4 mmol/L, MLSS reflects the actual capacity for balance between lactate production and clearance in each individual.

## Why VO2max Doesn’t Tell the Whole Story

A recreational cyclist may have their lactate threshold at 60% of their VO2max. A WorldTour professional will have it at 85-90%. That 25-30 percentage point difference represents a huge amount of sustainable watts.

The fraction of VO2max utilization at the threshold is the bridge between these two metrics. If two riders have a VO2max of 75 ml/kg/min but one sustains the threshold at 78% and the other at 88%, the latter produces approximately 0.7-0.8 W/kg more sustainable power. Over a 30-minute climb, that can mean several minutes of difference.

Between an elite cyclist and a WorldTour rider, VO2max may differ by only 5-10 ml/kg/min, while the fraction of utilization at the threshold marks the true competitive separation.

## FTP and Its Link to the Threshold

Functional Threshold Power (FTP) became popularized as the maximum sustainable power over one hour. In practice, Allen and Coggan (2019) defined it as a field estimate of the functional lactate threshold. The classic 20-minute maximal effort test, multiplied by 0.95, produces an approximation of MLSS.

The relationship between FTP and MLSS is not perfect. Morgan et al. (2019) showed that the 0.95 factor can vary between 0.90 and 1.00 depending on the cyclist’s physiological profile. Riders with high anaerobic capacity tend to overestimate their FTP with the 20-minute test, while cyclists with a more aerobic profile tend to underestimate it.

## Critical Power: The Modern Alternative

Critical Power (CP) is a mathematical concept that separates sustainable and non-sustainable intensity domains. Unlike FTP, CP is calculated from multiple maximal efforts of different durations, making it less dependent on a single protocol.

Jones et al. (2019) demonstrated that CP approximates MLSS more accurately than FTP in most cyclists. The critical power model also provides W' (W prime), a measure of work capacity above CP, useful for understanding performance in efforts with pace changes such as criteriums or mountain stages with repeated attacks.

## How They Are Measured: Lab vs. Field

VO2max reference measurement requires a gas analyzer in a laboratory, with an incremental protocol to exhaustion on a cycle ergometer. Lactate threshold additionally demands capillary blood samples at each intensity step. A full lab test with both measurements costs between 150 and 400 euros depending on the center and region.

In the field, VO2max can be estimated from a 5-minute maximal effort with a power meter, although accuracy is lower than in the lab. For lactate threshold, the 20-minute FTP test or critical power protocols (maximal efforts of 3, 7, and 12 minutes on separate days) offer practical approximations without the need for blood sampling.

## What to Train According to Your Level

A cyclist who has been training with structure for less than two years has a large margin to improve their VO2max. Intervals of 3 to 5 minutes at 90-100% of VO2max, performed twice a week, can raise the aerobic ceiling by 5% to 15% in one season. At that level, raising the ceiling brings a proportional improvement in the threshold.

For cyclists with more than four years of consistent training, VO2max tends to stabilize near their genetic maximum. Buchheit and Laursen (2013) described how in trained athletes the response to high-intensity training progressively attenuates. At this point, performance gains come from shifting the lactate threshold to a higher percentage of VO2max. Intervals of 8 to 20 minutes at 88-95% of FTP, combined with high volumes of zone 2 training, are the main tools.

The evidence is consistent: VO2max sets the ceiling, but the lactate threshold defines where you live within that ceiling. For most trained cyclists seeking to improve road race performance, raising the fraction of utilization at the threshold produces more tangible results than chasing additional milliliters of VO2max.
