## The Physics That Slow Down the Cyclist

A cyclist traveling at 40 km/h dedicates between 80% and 90% of their power to overcoming air resistance. The remainder is split between tire friction with the asphalt and mechanical losses in the drivetrain. At 50 km/h, the aerodynamic proportion exceeds 90%. Filippo Ganna, when he broke the hour record in 2022 with 56.792 km, optimized his CdA to values close to 0.17 m², allowing him to save tens of watts compared to a conventional time trial position. These figures explain why WorldTour teams invest hundreds of thousands of euros in wind tunnels.

The aerodynamic drag force is described by a well-known equation in fluid dynamics:

**F = 0.5 × ρ × CdA × v²**

Where **F** is the drag force in newtons, **ρ** (rho) is the air density (approximately 1.225 kg/m³ at sea level at 15 °C), **CdA** is the product of the drag coefficient and the cyclist’s frontal area in square meters, and **v** is the velocity relative to the air in meters per second. The power required to overcome this force is obtained by multiplying F by v, which means the power needed grows with the cube of the velocity.

## CdA: The Number That Defines Your Speed

CdA combines two factors into a single value. The drag coefficient (Cd) depends on the shape of the body and surface roughness. The frontal area (A) is the silhouette the cyclist projects against the wind. Defraeye et al. (2010), in wind tunnel measurements published in the *Journal of Biomechanics*, determined that a 1.80 m tall cyclist pedaling upright with hands on the hoods has a CdA close to 0.32 m². The same person in the drops reduces it to 0.27 m². With aero bars and an optimized time trial position, CdA drops to 0.22 m².

That 0.10 m² difference between the upright and time trial positions has a measurable wattage cost. At 40 km/h, reducing CdA from 0.32 to 0.22 m² saves between 60 and 70 watts of power. In a 40-kilometer time trial, that translates into several minutes of difference.

## Watts Required by Position

The following table shows the approximate power needed to maintain 40 km/h on flat terrain, no wind, at sea level, for a 75 kg cyclist with an 8 kg bike. Values assume a rolling resistance of 0.004 and 3% mechanical losses.

| Position | CdA (m²) | Power at 40 km/h | Difference vs. Upright |
|----------|-----------|------------------|-----------------------|
| Upright (hands on hoods) | 0.32 | ~280 W | — |
| Hands in drops | 0.27 | ~245 W | -35 W |
| Drops with elbows bent | 0.25 | ~230 W | -50 W |
| Aero bars (time trial) | 0.22 | ~205 W | -75 W |
| Optimized aero bars (TT pro) | 0.19 | ~180 W | -100 W |

The difference column shows watts the cyclist saves without improving their physiological engine. These are free watts.

## Why Speed Changes Everything

Aerodynamic drag grows with the square of velocity, but the power required to overcome it grows with the cube. A cyclist needing 70 watts to go 30 km/h will require about 165 watts at 40 km/h and roughly 325 watts at 50 km/h. Doubling speed from 25 to 50 km/h multiplies required power by eight.

This has a direct consequence on strategy. At low speeds, such as climbing at 15 km/h, aerodynamics barely matter. At peloton speeds on flat terrain, above 40 km/h, every square centimeter of frontal area counts. That’s why sprinters crouch low over their bikes in the last 200 meters and time trialists optimize every detail of their position.

## Drafting: The Oldest Aerodynamic Tactic

Riding behind another cyclist reduces air resistance by 30% to 40%. Wind tunnel measurements and computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations confirm that at a distance of 0.5 meters behind the lead cyclist’s rear wheel, drag reduction reaches 40%. At 2 meters, the reduction drops to 25%.

In the peloton, cyclists in central positions can experience reductions of up to 50% in aerodynamic drag. This explains why a domestique working at the front of the group expends 350 watts while their protected teammates maintain the same pace with 200 watts. Drafting is why road cycling is a team sport.

## Clothing, Helmet, and Equipment

Documented tests in sports engineering publications confirm that aerodynamic skinsuits can save between 5 and 15 watts compared to a loose jersey with an open zipper. The difference depends on fit and fabric roughness. Manufacturers like Endura and Vorteq use tripping strips—bands with controlled texture that manage airflow separation in high-curvature zones such as the shoulders.

Aero helmets provide between 2 and 5 watts of savings at 40 km/h compared to conventional ventilated helmets. Shoes with smooth covers, tight gloves, and tape on helmet straps are minor details individually but add up. In time trials, where every second counts, the accumulation of marginal gains can sum to 20 to 30 watts.

## The Trade-off Between Aerodynamics and Power

A more aggressive position does not always produce higher speed. Fintelman et al. (2015), in research published in the *Journal of Sports Sciences*, demonstrated that a time trial position with a very low torso can reduce CdA by 15%, but also decrease sustainable power by 5% to 14% due to restricted hip opening and diaphragmatic compression.

The optimal point varies between cyclists. Factors such as flexibility, femur length, and the ability to maintain the position throughout the effort determine how much aerodynamic aggressiveness is advisable. An aerodynamic bike fit seeks the lowest CdA the cyclist can sustain without losing power. Lower than that yields aerodynamic gains that don’t compensate for the wattage loss.

## How CdA Is Measured

The wind tunnel remains the gold standard. Facilities like GST in Los Angeles or the Boardman Performance Centre in the UK allow measuring CdA with a precision of ±0.002 m². A typical session lasts between two and four hours and costs between 1,000 and 3,000 euros.

The accessible alternative is field testing, which combines a calibrated power meter with GPS data, barometric altimetry, and weather conditions. Chung (2012) developed a field CdA estimation protocol that achieves ±0.01 m² precision in low-wind conditions. Platforms like Aerolab and MyWindsock have automated these calculations. They don’t replace wind tunnels but enable iterative adjustments between lab sessions.

## Free Speed

The concept of *free speed* summarizes aerodynamic philosophy. Improving VO2max by 5% requires months of structured training. Reducing CdA by 10% can be achieved in an afternoon with a bike fit and a skinsuit. Both produce gains of similar magnitude at 40 km/h.

This does not mean training is irrelevant. It means ignoring aerodynamics is leaving watts on the table. A cyclist who trains rigorously but rides upright in loose clothing is sacrificing performance. Aerodynamic science does not replace the engine; it complements it. And unlike fitness, aerodynamic improvements do not fade when you stop training.
